## Contents

### 1. The Core Argument
The CTO has the impossible task of being the most things during their tenure, in the role most structurally deprived of peer support, and is typically the most under-compensated for it on a risk-adjusted basis. This is a design flaw, not a character flaw.

### 2. Role Evolution Timeline
Four-phase stack (pre-taxonomy version):
- Pre-seed: The Builder — solo engineer, identity entirely in the code
- Seed→A (⚠ highest failure rate): The Transition — must shift from maker to manager simultaneously under fundraising pressure
- Series A-B: The Org Builder — installs process, splits CTO/VP Eng roles
- Series B+: The Executive — predominantly strategic

### 3. Failure Anatomy: The Control Collapse Pattern
Five-stage failure sequence:
1. The grip tightens — scaling pressure triggers control
2. Strategic decisions stall — absorbed in operations
3. Team burns out — engineers lose autonomy, top performers leave
4. CTO self-relegates — becomes very senior IC
5. Replacement becomes inevitable but delayed — company loses 12-24 months of strategic momentum

### 4. The Peer Gap
CTOs are often the most senior technical authority in their organization, yet frequently without a true peer internally. CEO can't model the transition (no technical context). Engineering team can't challenge (they revere the CTO's depth). Board waits until failure is visible. Nobody in the room has both the standing and the knowledge to name the transition.

### 5. Compensation Data (Kruze Consulting, 250+ startups)
| Stage | Founding CTO | Hired CTO | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seed | $133,000 | $190,000 | −$57K |
| Series A | $177,000 | $293,000 | −$116K |
| Series B | $218,000 | ~$300,000+ | −$80K+ |

The equity offset assumes a liquidity event that, statistically, most startups don't reach.

### 6. Self-Reflection Framework (5 questions)
1. When did I last make a decision I was uncomfortable making?
2. Did the team need my approval for things they were more qualified to decide?
3. Who, inside the company, could have told me I was in the wrong mode — and would I have listened?
4. At what point did I shift from building the company to protecting what I'd built?
5. What did the next version of the CTO role actually require — and did I want to do that job?

### 7. Interview Narrative Guidance
Three scenarios with what to avoid and what works:
- "Why did you leave / what happened at your last company?"
- "Tell me about a time you made a mistake as a leader."
- "How do you see the CTO role at our stage?"

Core framing: own the pattern with specificity. Interviewers have seen it. The question isn't whether it happened — it's whether you understand it.

### 8. What to Look for in the Next Role
- A CEO who has run a scaled engineering team before
- An explicit VP of Engineering hire in the plan
- A board with at least one technical operator
- **Avoid:** Companies that want a "full-stack CTO" indefinitely
