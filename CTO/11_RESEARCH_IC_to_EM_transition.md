# Can the CTO Skills Be Trained? The IC → EM → VP Engineering Transition
## Research Finding: The Career Path That Would Prove Training Works — And What It Actually Shows

**Research date:** June 24, 2026  
**Status:** Empirical test of the training solution  
**Relationship to corpus:** `12_RESEARCH_rational_solutions.md` identified training as the largest attempted solution to the irrational composite, with no CTO-specific outcome research. This document investigates the nearest empirical proxy: whether engineers can successfully traverse the IC → EM → VP Engineering career path that would produce the Multiplier-mode skills a founding CTO needs.

---

## The Question

The training solution to the CTO composite problem requires that one person can successfully develop:

1. **Builder-mode skills** — deep technical craft, architecture, direct IC contribution
2. **Multiplier-mode skills** — people management, coaching, delegation, team infrastructure
3. **Strategist-mode skills** — board communication, external vision, long-horizon bets

The most optimistic reading of the training solution is that a strong engineer can traverse the full IC → EM → VP Engineering career path, developing each skill set in sequence. If that path works reliably, training has a case. If it doesn't — if the transitions within that path fail at high rates — then training is asking CTOs to beat a transition that breaks experienced engineers who have years of organizational context, dedicated support, and time to do it properly.

---

## The Full Transition Timeline

The career path from Staff/Principal Engineer to VP Engineering — covering the Builder→Multiplier transition that is most critical for CTOs — takes:

- **Senior Engineer → Staff/Principal Engineer:** 5–10 years
- **Staff Engineer → Engineering Manager:** sideways move, requires demonstrated people leadership traits; typically 6–12 months preparation
- **Engineering Manager → Director:** 2–4 years
- **Director → VP Engineering:** 2–4 years, described as "a career change, not a promotion"

<!-- APPENDIX-REF: AX-VPENG-TIMELINE — 8–15yr IC→VP Eng path vs 18–24mo CTO demand. Source: em-tools.io (content site). Underpins the compression argument. See 07_APPENDIX. -->
> "The path from first-time engineering manager to VP of Engineering typically takes **eight to fifteen years**, though there is significant variation based on company size, growth rate, and individual capability."
> — em-tools.io, VP of Engineering Skills, 2026

**The founding CTO is expected to traverse the equivalent of this 8–15 year path within 18–24 months, under fundraising pressure, with no organizational support structure, no peers to learn from, and an identity built around the skills they are supposed to be leaving behind.**

## Stage 1: IC → Engineering Manager

### The transition is a role change, not a promotion

> "The transition to an EM is a **role change, not a promotion**. Being a great IC does not automatically translate into being a great EM."
> — Pat Kua, How Engineering Managers Are Set Up to Fail

> "Management isn't a promotion in the traditional sense; it's a change in role, focus, and skill set. A respondent put it well: 'Becoming an engineering manager doesn't entitle you with power and authority. It's more like you're responsible for people, projects, products, happiness.'"
> — Intuit Engineering, 82% of survey respondents confirmed managers spend most time balancing team needs against management expectations

### The failure rate is high and underdiscussed

<!-- APPENDIX-REF: AX-REVERSION — ~50% IC→EM reversion rate (Weekes/Figma, First Round Review). Single most load-bearing empirical number in the corpus; practitioner quote, not a study. See 07_APPENDIX. -->
> "At least **half the time** those newly-minted engineering managers will leave their post, retreating back to the IC sphere. If this was the failure rate of a service, we would do a postmortem on it and be concerned that the failure rate was so high."
> — Marcel Weekes, VP of Product Engineering at Figma (formerly VP Engineering at Slack), First Round Review

This is the most important number in this document. A **~50% reversion rate** for the IC → EM transition at companies with organizational support, dedicated management development programs, and experienced peers. The founding CTO makes this transition alone, mid-company, under funding pressure.

### Why engineers fail the EM transition

The failure mechanisms are well-documented and map directly to the Builder→Multiplier transition the taxonomy describes:

1. **Identity investment** — engineers are promoted because they are excellent at technical work; management requires them to stop doing the thing that defines them
2. **Feedback loop loss** — engineering produces immediate, concrete feedback (code works or doesn't); management produces slow, ambiguous feedback (team culture improves over months)
3. **Skill set mismatch** — companies promote their best ICs to EM because they have technical credibility, not because they have demonstrated people skills; these are different capabilities
4. **Support gap** — most organizations provide EMs far less training and support than they provide to ICs; the expectation is that the "best engineer" should figure it out

> "Never, ever accept a managerial role until you are already solidly senior as an engineer. To me this means at least **seven years or more** writing and shipping code; definitely, absolutely no less than five."
> — Charity Majors, CTO at Honeycomb.io, The Engineer/Manager Pendulum

The prerequisite for a *likely-to-succeed* IC → EM transition is 5–7+ years of senior engineering experience. The founding CTO is often making this transition with 3–7 years of total engineering experience, much of it at the company they are also trying to run.

### The "pendulum" observation

The most influential framework for IC → EM transitions is Charity Majors' "engineer/manager pendulum" — the idea that the best engineering leaders swing back and forth between IC and management rather than committing permanently to one track. This is descriptively accurate for how the best leaders develop over a 15–30 year career.

It is not a model that applies to a founding CTO who needs to make a permanent forward transition in 18 months.

> "For every step up the ladder you go, the opportunities drop by another order of magnitude, and the scrutiny becomes much more intense and particular. If you're looking for VP roles, it may take months to find a place you want to work at, and then they might not choose you."
> — Charity Majors, Twin Anxieties of the Engineer/Manager Pendulum

## Stage 2: Engineering Manager → VP Engineering

### This transition is described as a career change, not a step

> "The jump from engineering manager to VP is **not just a promotion — it is a career change**. You go from owning a team's output to owning the entire engineering function: its strategy, budget, culture, and reputation at the executive table. Most of the skills that made you a great EM will not get you there."
> — em-tools.io, VP of Engineering Skills, 2026

> "The skills required from being a coder to a VPE are **completely different**."
> — VP Engineering, quoted in SheTO leadership series

### The specific skills gaps at VP level

At VP Engineering, entirely new skill sets are required that have no precedent in either IC or EM work:

- **Budget ownership** — managing engineering headcount and infrastructure spend against company financial constraints
- **Org design** — structuring multiple teams, defining reporting lines, managing managers rather than engineers
- **Executive communication** — presenting to boards and CEO peers without technical translation
- **Cross-domain leadership** — leading engineering teams in domains the VP is not expert in
- **Strategic planning** — operating on 1–3 year horizons rather than sprint or quarterly cadences

> "At a VP level, you will lead at least some team whose **domain is unfamiliar to you**. It can be challenging, as you do not know what you don't know."
> — Medium, Director to VP Engineering, 2021

> "The planning span is now at least a year, often several years. The common knowledge we deem everyone knows within engineering should be assumed unknown to your peer executives."
> — Medium, Director to VP Engineering, 2021

### What VP Engineering actually requires

Most VP Engineering roles require:
- **Minimum 10 years** of experience managing teams as EM, senior engineer, or Director
- Demonstrated expertise in running multiple teams simultaneously
- Budget ownership experience
- Track record of hiring and developing managers, not just engineers
- Executive communication fluency with non-technical stakeholders

> "Expect most VP Engineering roles to require a minimum of **10 years** of experience managing teams."
> — University of San Diego, How to Become a VP of Engineering

This is the role that performs the Multiplier function in an established company. The founding CTO is doing this work with a fraction of this preparation.

## The Compounding Problem: Technical Skills Atrophy

While the above transitions are happening, the technical skills that defined the founding CTO as a Builder are actively degrading.

After moving into management, technical skills atrophy — widely reported across the practitioner literature, though the exact timeline varies and is not precisely established (an earlier draft asserted "within 2–3 years"; that precision is unsourced and has been removed — see `07_APPENDIX` #14). This isn't a personal failing — it's the nature of the job. You can't stay current with the latest frameworks, keep deep expertise in your domain, and do the work of management well. Something has to give. (One caveat worth noting: a minority of practitioners report the opposite — that a stint in management *sharpens* higher-level technical judgment on return to IC work. The dominant pattern is atrophy, but it is not universal.)

The founding CTO who successfully navigates the IC → EM transition is simultaneously:
- Losing the Builder-mode skills that made them valuable at seed stage
- Developing Multiplier-mode skills that take 8–15 years to develop properly
- Being evaluated against Strategist-mode expectations by investors and boards
- Running a company

## Summary: What This Means for the Training Solution

| Transition | Typical timeline | Failure/reversion rate | Founding CTO has |
|---|---|---|---|
| IC → Engineering Manager | 1–2 years with support | **~50% reversion** (Weekes/Figma) | 0–6 months, no support |
| EM → Director | 2–4 years | Undocumented but significant | Never holds this role |
| Director → VP Engineering | 2–4 years; described as "career change" | Undocumented | Never holds this role |
| Full path: IC → VP Engineering | **8–15 years** | Cumulative, compounding | Expected in **18–24 months** |

The training solution asks the founding CTO to compress 8–15 years of career development into 18–24 months, while simultaneously running a company, without the organizational infrastructure that gives the full path its best chance of success, and against a ~50% reversion rate at the very first transition.

---

## The Critical Implication

Training does not fail because CTOs are unwilling to develop. It fails because:

1. **The baseline transition (IC → EM) has a ~50% failure rate even in optimal conditions** — with organizational support, peers, and time
2. **The full transition (IC → VP Eng) takes 8–15 years** — longer than most startups' pre-Series B lifespan
3. **The skills actively work against each other** — technical depth and people management are not just different, they are in competition for the same cognitive and calendar resources
4. **Technical skills atrophy during the transition** — the Builder loses their primary value while the Multiplier skills are not yet developed

Training is not a solution to a structural problem. It is an attempt to make one person beat a transition that breaks experienced engineers who have everything working in their favor.

## What No One Has Studied (The Actual Research Gap)

CTO-specific transition research does not exist. All of the above is inferred from:
- General IC → EM transition literature (which is itself mostly practitioner, not peer-reviewed)
- VP Engineering career path documentation (practitioner)
- Compensation and tenure data (Kruze, Spencer Stuart)

<!-- GAP: GAP-CTO-TRANSITION — no study measures founding-CTO Builder→Multiplier success rate or its predictors. The framework's central falsifiable test. Registered in CONVENTIONS.md. -->
**The unstudied question:** Of founding CTOs who attempt the Builder→Multiplier transition, what proportion succeed? What differentiates them from those who don't? Is it prior management experience, personality profile, organizational support, timing, or some combination?

This is the empirical test that would either validate or challenge the entire framework. It doesn't exist. That absence is consistent with everything else we found: the Builder mode is structurally invisible to researchers, and the transition failure is attributed to individuals rather than studied as a structural phenomenon.
