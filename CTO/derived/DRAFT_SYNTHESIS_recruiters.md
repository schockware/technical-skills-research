# Stop Writing the Unicorn CTO Req
## A practitioner brief for the people who write and place the job descriptions

You write a line into a CTO job description without thinking twice: *"hands-on AND strategic — can architect the system today and lead the org tomorrow."* It reads like raising the bar. It's actually the first link in a chain that ends with a good technical leader being told, eighteen months later, that they "couldn't scale."

This brief is about that one line, and what to write instead. It is not a guide to finding someone who can do all three. That person is the unicorn, and chasing the unicorn is the problem, not the fix.

---

## The line you're writing demands both sides of a boundary most people cross once

The software industry built two separate career ladders — individual contributor and management — and told engineers to *pick one*. No other major profession formalized that split the way software did. The whole point of the split is that most engineers never have to be both at once: you choose the IC track or the management track, and the org staffs the other side.

"Hands-on AND strategic" un-does that split in a single sentence. It asks one person to be excellent on *both* sides of the one boundary the field itself decided was two different jobs — and to be it from the founding stage, before there's anyone else to staff either side.

And it's a boundary that's genuinely hard to cross. About **half of newly promoted engineering managers go back to being individual contributors** — and that holds *even at companies with real management-development programs* (the ~50% figure is corroborated in large-sample management research, n≈30,000). It's not a training gap. Crossing that boundary means giving up being the person who does the work — an identity change, not a promotion. A req that demands "hands-on AND strategic, permanently" is asking for someone who has crossed a one-way boundary and stayed on both sides of it.

> **The honest boundary on that number:** the ~50% is about the IC→manager transition in general, not about CTOs specifically — there is no measured CTO-specific rate. We're using it as *the measured difficulty of the boundary the composite JD straddles*, not as a failure prediction for any candidate.

---

## You already apply the fix — in sales

Here's the part that should land for anyone who writes reqs across functions: **engineering is the only place you don't already split this role.**

Sales has the same craft-to-strategy rotation. Closing deals is genuinely different work from building and running the revenue organization. The industry resolved it by splitting the title: the **VP of Sales** carries the building-and-operating load, and the **Chief Revenue Officer** is the strategic, market- and board-facing role. You write those as two reqs, with two different criteria, without blinking.

The exact same split exists for engineering on paper — **CTO** (technical vision, strategy) and **VP Engineering** (people, delivery, operations) — but the founding-stage req collapses them back into one person and calls it a CTO. The template you already trust in sales is the one you're not applying to engineering.

---

## "Full-stack CTO forever" is a budget line dressed as a requirement

When a founder insists the early CTO must do everything, the real sentence underneath is usually *"we can't afford to split this yet."* That's a budget constraint — a reasonable one. The trouble is it gets written into the req as a *requirement*, as if the ideal hire is someone who can do all three modes indefinitely.

The tell that it's a budget line, not a real spec: **companies break the role into a CTO, a VP Eng, engineering managers, and staff engineers the moment they can afford to.** Nobody keeps the composite once they have the budget to split it. A "requirement" that everyone abandons as soon as they have money was never a requirement — it was a compromise. Writing it as a permanent spec sets the hire up to be measured against a job that the company itself doesn't believe is one person's job.

---

## What to put in the req instead: hire for the mode, not the unicorn

The usable move is to stop writing one timeless CTO spec and start writing for the **mode the company is actually in.** Three operational profiles, three different reqs:

| Stage / mode | What the req should actually ask for | What to stop asking for |
|---|---|---|
| **Builder** (pre-seed → seed) | Hands-on architecture and build; ships the product; deep IC technical depth. Search filters: staff/principal-engineer signals, founding-engineer track record. | Don't load it with "board-ready strategic visionary." That's not the job yet. |
| **Multiplier** (~15–20 engineers) | Builds and runs the org: hiring, delegation, engineering-management infrastructure. This is the **VP-Eng** load. Filters: people-leadership, org-scaling experience. | Don't assume the Builder hire auto-converts. This is the boundary half the field reverts at. |
| **Strategist** (scaling) | Technology strategy, board and external, market-facing. The classic "CTO" the title was originally coined for. | Don't expect this person to still be hands-on in the codebase. |

Two practical notes for the taxonomy:

- **Name the mode in the req and on the board.** "Founding CTO (Builder-mode, hands-on)" or "VP Engineering (Multiplier-mode, org-scaling)" tells candidates and hiring managers which side of the boundary the job lives on. A title with no mode attached is what lets a Builder get hired and then judged as a Strategist.
- **If the budget genuinely only allows one person, say *that*** — "founding technical leader, Builder-mode now, transition planned at scale" — rather than writing "does everything forever." That single change turns a unicorn hunt into an honest, fillable req, and it sets up the eventual split as planned rather than as a failure.

---

## Why this matters past the hire

When the composite hire predictably hits the boundary, the language that follows is always personal — *"he's not scaling," "she's not strategic enough," "the company outgrew them."* Said once, that's a candidate. Said every time, across every company, for years, it's a structure nobody's looking at — and the req that demanded "hands-on AND strategic" is where the structure starts. The composite JD selects for someone who will then be blamed for not being superhuman.

You can't write a req for a transition you won't name. Naming the mode in the spec is the small, concrete thing the people who write the JDs can actually do.

---

> **One thing this brief is *not* saying** (kept honest against the spine): it is **not** telling you to stop writing CTO roles. CTO is a real, needed role. The message is narrower: stop writing the *composite* one — the single req that demands all three modes at once, forever. And there is deliberately **no recipe here for finding someone who *can* do all three.** That's the unicorn hunt, and the whole point is to stop running it.
