# The Under-Resourced Founding CTO: A Framework With Strong Component Grounding and an Unrun Central Experiment
## A research-agenda framing for the people who could confirm or break the thesis

**What this version is.** This is the citable projection of the rigorous spine (`DRAFT_SYNTHESIS.md`). For this audience it is the version *closest* to the spine — it keeps the concessions in full, expands the falsifiers into study designs, and foregrounds the one thing that makes the difference between a framework and a finding: the central experiment has not been run. Everything below is downstream of the spine; where this file is terser than the spine, prefer the spine.

**The bottom line, stated first (because this audience rewards it).** The argument that follows is **a framework with strong grounding in its decomposed components and an unrun central experiment.** Its component claims rest on a hard institutional fact, a large-sample behavioral number, and a peer-reviewed mechanism — none of which require the framework to be accepted. Its *composite* claim — that the founding CTO faces these components combined, compressed, and uncushioned, at a severity that concentrates a particular harm — is an **extension**, argued rather than measured, and it stakes itself on falsifiers (§5b) that have not yet been tested. We present it as exactly that, with the experiment specified.

## §0 — The disclosure that belongs at the top, not the footnote

Because this audience treats provenance as a credibility variable rather than a liability, the origin is stated up front (this is `R2` Bias 8, disclosed by the author 2026-06-25):

> The thesis originated in the author's firsthand knowledge of **four founding CTOs who could not make the transition — and whose organizations expected them to transition *or step down on their own.*** Stated precisely, this is a **failure-only, author-selected sample of n≈4 with observer involvement.**

The honest reading, which is also the methodological one:

- **What the n≈4 legitimately did:** generate the hypothesis. Four firsthand cases are a defensible *source of a question* and an illegitimate *source of evidence*. Disclosure is what lets a reader confirm they are doing only the former.
- **The mitigation that makes the disclosure safe — and it is structural, not rhetorical:** the load-bearing evidence is **independent of the four cases.** The n≈30,000 IC→EM reversion rate, the IC/management institutional split (verifiable without the framework), the peer-reviewed founder-offload mechanism, and the CRO/VP-Sales structural fact were none of them derived from or selected to fit the four. The four motivated the search; the search returned evidence that stands without them. *That separation — motivating observation cleanly severable from load-bearing evidence — is the test of whether a firsthand-anchored thesis is honest, and it is claimed here only because the separation can be stated, not merely asserted.*
- **The residual risk that does not fully clear:** a *shaping* effect on what got researched (failure-first emphasis — the slippage mode got a mechanism; the success paths got a 2×2 cell). The corrective is `GAP-CTO-TRANSITION` — a base-rate (success) study — and the de-escalation of the success-case finding (`AX-NO-TRIPLE-MODE`, demoted from "survivor-proof"). The shaping is partially, not wholly, corrected. We say so.

The detail that is *research signal, not just bias*: "transition *or step down on their own*" is the demand-side cultural-expectation pattern (`TODO` T2) — the organization locating the burden in the individual and never asking whether the demand was coherent. It is §3's misattribution observed in the wild. It is **not evidence** (n≈4, selected); it is the legitimate originating observation of an open adjacent question (see "Open adjacent research" below).

## §0.1 — The contribution, sized for citation

This work does **not** argue "the CTO role is an irrational composite." Stripped to what survives adversarial review (`R1`/`R2`), the contribution is:

> **A precise, non-pejorative vocabulary for a real, institutionally-grounded skill discontinuity that the founding CTO is asked to cross under-resourced — so that a difficulty the field currently narrates as personal failure ("he's not scaling") can instead be named as structural ("we're at a Multiplier stage and resourced no transition for it"), which is the prerequisite to resourcing it.**

## §1 — The established floor: one institutionally-real discontinuity (the claim that needs no framework)

Everything an outside reviewer can check without first accepting the framework reduces to a single institutional fact:

> **The software industry built two formal, separately-laddered, separately-evaluated career tracks — individual contributor and management — and told engineers to choose between them. No other major profession formalized that split the way software did.**

This is not a finding the corpus produced. It predates the research, is visible in the published career ladder of essentially every technology company, and was not surfaced by a confirmation-oriented search. **It is the one seam in the whole framework an independent rater can verify without being handed the taxonomy** — the floor a skeptic who rejects everything else still has to account for.

Two load-bearing inferences, and only two:

1. **The split is the industry's own admission that the two are different work.** You do not build separate ladders, titles, rubrics, and performance criteria for the same job at different levels; you build them when excellence at the first stops predicting excellence at the second. Medicine did not split "senior doctor who treats patients" from "senior doctor who manages doctors," because there the senior version is *more of the same craft.* Software did — the org chart is the industry telling us the two diverge enough to require choosing.

2. **The founding CTO is asked to span exactly that boundary, pre-scale and alone.** The split exists so most engineers never hold both at once. The founding CTO is the one seat positioned where the organization has staffed *neither* side.

### The cleanest external exhibit: the CRO

The strongest single piece of evidence is the **closest C-suite peer to the CTO** facing the same craft→strategy rotation in the same founding, board-visible, revenue-critical context — the revenue leader — and whether *it* got a cushion the CTO did not. The industry resolved that rotation by **splitting the title**: VP of Sales (build/operate) from **Chief Revenue Officer** (strategic, market-and-board-facing). The split happened because the composite was visibly too much for one person, and naming it as two roles was the fix.

This is damning rather than incidental precisely because the CRO is the *nearest structural peer* — a skeptic cannot dismiss it as "sales is just different," because sales is the *least* different case. Engineering's equivalent founding-stage split never happened; the VP-Eng split, where it exists, typically arrives only post–Series A, after the founding-stage span has already been demanded and often failed.

*(Source discipline, `AX-CRO-SPLIT`: the CRO/VP-Sales distinction is corroborated across multiple independent industry sources — strategic/multi-quarter vs. operational/short-horizon, the split appearing at org-maturity not founding — and verified 2026-06-25 as a genuine **rotation**, not mere scaling: industry sources describe VP-Sales→CRO as "fundamentally different from a typical promotion" and document sales leaders failing the transition for lack of the new skill set — the same failure pattern, in the nearest peer. The load-bearing element is the bare structural fact, not any single source. Convergent content-class sources, not a primary study — flagged as such.)*

### The discipline this section holds to (stated so it cannot be quietly widened)

1. **One discontinuity, not three.** Only the Builder→Multiplier (IC→management) seam is corroborated by a fact outside the framework. The three-mode lens (Builder → Multiplier → Strategist) usefully describes the arc; the Multiplier→Strategist seam is carried as *plausible framework, not a second proven discontinuity.*
2. **Most severe, not different in kind.** Other C-suite roles rotate too (§5a.2). The CTO's distinction is that it rotates across the *one* boundary the industry formalized into separate tracks — sharpened, not unique. *(Premise 2: this holds regardless of the title's etymology.)*

## §2 — The rotation is identity-gated and uncushioned (and this is where the borrowed-evidence boundary lives)

§1 established one boundary is institutionally real. §2 establishes three things about *crossing* it: it is measurably hard, hard for a specific reason (identity, not skill), and the founding CTO crosses it stripped of the supports that make the same crossing survivable elsewhere.

**This section leans hardest on evidence about populations other than CTOs — deliberately, and under license, with the terms stated once and honored at every claim (Premise 1, component-equivalence):** the founding CTO is, by construction, doing the IC engineer's work and making the IC→manager move, so evidence about that move is evidence about the component transition the CTO must make. What that evidence does **not** do is measure the CTO-specific *compression* (all of it at once, pre-scale, alone); that severity is the corpus's extension, argued, not the source papers' finding.

### 2a. The behavioral anchor (the established number)

> **The IC-to-engineering-manager reversion rate: roughly half of newly promoted engineering managers return to individual-contributor work** — corroborated by large-sample management research (CEB/Gartner: ~50% of new managers/executives fail within 18 months, **n≈30,000**; McKinsey: ~40% executive failure). *(`AX-REVERSION`.)*

It is the strongest empirical fact in the work for one reason: it is **behavioral, not sentiment** — it measures what people *did* (reverted), across a population that includes both successes and failures, at large sample. The boundary it measures is the *exact* boundary §1 identified as institutionally real.

**Two discipline notes the academic reader will check for, both stated:**
- It is **not** a CTO-specific failure rate. It is the measured difficulty of the *component boundary*. The CTO-specific rate is unmeasured (§5 / `GAP-CTO-TRANSITION`).
- The competing "engineers just need better management training" explanation predicts the rate should fall with support. It does not: the ~50% holds *at companies with dedicated management-development programs.* This is stated as what the number is *consistent with* — the controlled study is not ours.

### 2b. The peer-reviewed mechanism (the established mechanism, in a buffered population)

The reason the boundary resists training is that crossing it is an **identity transition, not a skill upgrade** — the one place with *peer-reviewed* grounding, and the one place the boundary on that grounding must be stated most carefully.

Mathias & Williams (2018, *J. Business Venturing*) and Van Lancker et al. (2023, same journal) — the corpus's two full-text-verified anchors — establish, **in founders:**
- Role transition is a matter of which roles one will *give up, retain, adopt* — the hard part is **giving up a self-defining role** (identity loss, not task hand-off). *(MW2018.)*
- Successful offload runs through *physical* disengagement without *psychological* disengagement, and through **role-identity imprinting**. *(MW2018.)*
- Offload *sticks* only under **psychological safety and value-fit** in the receiving team. *(VL2023.)*

> **The boundary on this evidence (Premise 1 + `R3`):** these papers study **founders/entrepreneurs — the buffered, CEO-analog case** with support infrastructure the CTO lacks. They establish the *mechanism* — role transition is identity-gated and offload-conditional. They do **not** study CTOs and do **not** establish CTO-specific severity. The claim: *the mechanism is peer-reviewed in founders; the founding CTO faces the same mechanism with fewer of the conditions that let it succeed.* That last clause is the extension, argued in 2c — not borrowed from the papers.

### 2c. The comparative move (the argued extension)

Every other profession that rotates provides at least one cushion. The founding CTO, pre-scale, gets none:

- **Honest labeling** — the military names the Warrant Officer track; consulting's up-or-out is explicit. The CTO title signals *continuity* while the role demands a career change. *(Labeling axis borrowed from the sibling Industry thread, `CROSS_REFERENCES`.)*
- **A respected fallback** — the surgeon stays licensed; the professor keeps researching. The founding CTO has **no external license**: the "technical leader" identity was constructed entirely by doing the work, so when the work stops the identity has nothing to hold. This is also the mechanism behind 2a's reversion — reverting to IC reaches for the only identity anchor that exists.
- **A scaffolded timeline** — residencies, pupillage, officer academies are multi-year supported transitions. The CTO gets "new title, new expectations" on the funding clock — and the offload conditions VL2023 identifies (psychological safety, value-fit, capable receiving team) are exactly what a pre-scale startup has *least* of.

The synthesis: real (§1), measurably hard (2a), hard because identity-gated (2b), crossed stripped of every condition that makes it survivable elsewhere (2c). That is the defensible meaning of "under-resourced at the worst possible moment" — a list of specific missing supports, not a metaphor.

## §3 — The harm is the misattribution, and its falsifiable form

The previous sections describe a hard, under-resourced transition — which by itself is not yet a finding (*many* roles are hard). The contribution is what happens **after** the predictable difficulty arrives: it is read as the individual's failure, the structure is never examined, and so the structure is never resourced — guaranteeing the next occupant hits the same wall. Notably, this is the section needing the *least* evidence: it does not require the CTO to be uniquely broken, or the reversion rate to be CTO-specific, or any contested number. It requires only that **attribution is misdirected** — observable directly.

### 3a. The pattern

The language is remarkably consistent and remarkably personal: *"He's not scaling." "She's not strategic enough." "The company outgrew him." "Management just wasn't for them."* Each is individually plausible — which is why the pattern is hard to see. The *consistency* across cases, companies, and decades is the tell: a difficulty the structure would produce **for almost anyone placed in it** is recorded, every time, as a fact about the person. *(Corpus M5 axis: the failure is slow, internal, hard to attribute — which is what lets the personal reading go unchallenged.)* This is a claim about **default attribution**, not a claim the CTO never under-performs.

### 3b. Why it is self-perpetuating

The loop is short: (1) structure produces predictable difficulty at the boundary; (2) attributed to the individual; (3) remedy is therefore individual — replace/coach/hire "a stronger CTO"; (4) the replacement enters the *same* unstructured boundary with the *same* missing cushions; (5) return to (1). The de-facto industry solution — cycle CTOs until one fits — *is* this loop. It is expensive (lost institutional knowledge; a 12–24-month strategic gap per displacement, **practitioner-sourced, not primary-verified — flagged**) and never converges, because each iteration treats recurring structural failure as fresh individual failure.

### 3c. The contribution, in its falsifiable form (the version this audience should hold)

> **A precise, non-pejorative vocabulary that re-attributes the difficulty from the person to the structure — so a difficulty currently narrated as personal failure can instead be named structurally, which is the prerequisite to resourcing it.**

The load-bearing sentence: **you cannot resource a transition you cannot name.** As long as the difficulty is "he's not scaling," the only move is replacement; the moment it is "we're at a Multiplier stage and provided no transition support," a different move-set becomes available (a boundary-timed VP-Eng hire, an explicit mode hand-off, a named fallback).

For this audience the contribution must be stated **as a testable prerequisite, not a motte** (`R2` Bias 7 — legibility was the corpus's retreat position; here it is claimed forward):

> **The falsifiable form:** legibility is claimed as *necessary-but-not-sufficient.* **If mode-aware organizations intervened no more effectively than mode-blind ones, the legibility claim would fail.** That is the empirical commitment — not "we only offer words" retreated to under fire.

It does **not** claim the vocabulary *solves* the structural problem (the under-resourcing, funding constraints, unstaffed seat persist when named; §4 addresses why the structure survives understanding). The honest size: it converts a difficulty the field spends on *serial replacement* into one it could spend on *transition design* — by changing the sentence. Smaller than "we diagnosed an irrational composite," and more durable, because it survives every open question in §5.

## §4 — The strongest objection concedes the thesis (the number-independent argument)

> **The objection:** "You're describing an economic necessity, not a design flaw. A seed-stage startup *can't* staff three specialists. Demanding one person cover the span isn't irrational; it's the only thing the budget allows."

This is the best objection because it is *true* — and true in exactly the way that establishes the thesis. Track what it assumes to be coherent: *we would staff specialists if we could afford to.* That sentence only makes sense if specialists are *what the work requires* — if the span exceeds one person. You do not say "we can't afford to split this job" about a job one person handles comfortably. **The objection's own logic presupposes the capacity ceiling the thesis asserts.** It concedes the span exceeds one person, and pleads budget.

So the thesis and the objection agree on the fact underneath — one calls it necessity, the other under-resourcing. The thesis does **not** claim the composite is *irrational*; the equilibrium may be efficient (most startups die before mode-3 matters). It claims what the objection grants: **the composite exceeds one person's reach and is sustained because splitting it is unaffordable until later.**

The industry *acts out* this belief: it distributes the composite the moment it can afford to (`10_RESEARCH` — a CTO, VP-Eng, eng managers, staff/principal engineers; nobody keeps the composite once they can break it up), and it already ran the split in sales (CRO/VP-Sales).

**Why this argument is the one to foreground for this audience — it is number-independent:** it does *not* depend on the ~$1.1M distributed-cost figure or any compression ratio. It needs one uncontested behavioral fact — **companies split the role when they can afford to** — a revealed-preference argument that holds even if every contested number is wrong. It is the rare move that makes the skeptic's strongest point do the work.

## §5 — The payload: concessions in full, falsifiers as study designs

This is the section that, for this audience, *is* the deliverable. The spine keeps it in full; this projection keeps it in full and *expands the research agenda.* A thesis that has surrendered its weakest points and named its own disconfirmers cannot be dismantled by pointing at them.

### 5a. Five concessions, made up front

**1. The three-mode taxonomy is a lens, not a measured structure.** Only the first seam (Builder→Multiplier, IC→management) is corroborated by a fact outside the framework (§1). The Multiplier→Strategist boundary is plausible description, not measured discontinuity. The argument rests on the *discontinuity*, not the count; redraw the lens at any resolution. *(Construct validity is itself an open study — `R4` Q5 / F5; see below.)*

**2. Other C-suite roles rotate too — the CTO is the most severe case, not a different kind.** The CFO shifts books → capital markets; the CMO campaigns → brand. We do **not** claim the CTO uniquely rotates. Its distinction: it rotates across the *one* boundary the industry formalized into separate tracks, which exists in no other function — sharpened, not unique. (This retires the earlier "mismatch of *kind*, uniquely" claim.)

**3. The arrangement is under-resourced, not "irrational."** The composite may be a perfectly efficient bet under uncertainty; the harm we identify is to the *person* and is real whether or not the *system* is rational. We deliberately do not use "irrational" of the role's design. *(Whether the irrationality instead lives in the cultural **expectation** placed on the CTO — a different locus, and `U4`: "irrational" is normative, not empirical — is an open question this work does not resolve. See "Open adjacent research," T2/T4.)*

**4. The headline numbers are not primary-sourced, and we do not lean on them.** The "~4–6:1 compression ratio" (content-site salary medians; pre-scale job vs. post-scale org chart) and the "no documented CTO crossed all three modes" result (five anecdotal cases; an absence in a small biased sample) are *consistent with* the thesis and are *not evidence for* it. The argument stands on §1, §2a, §2b, §3 — and nothing requiring those two numbers.

**5. CTO failure may be less *visible*, not more *severe* (the documentation-artifact possibility).** It is a live, untested possibility that founding-CTO failure is no worse than other rotating executive roles and only *looks* worse because it is slower and harder to attribute. We rest the argument on *behavioral* outcomes (reverted to IC; left at the boundary) precisely so the thesis does not depend on the severity gap being real where only a visibility gap can be shown.

### 5b. Four falsifiers — the study designs

Each is a study that could be run; we predict its outcome and would abandon the corresponding claim if it returned the other way. These map directly onto the forward agenda (`R4`): **F1↔Q1, F2↔Q2, F3↔Q3, F4↔Q4.**

| # | If this were found… | …then this claim falls | `R4` design |
|---|---|---|---|
| **F1 (central)** | A systematic cohort study of founding CTOs shows a *substantial fraction* successfully span the IC→management boundary (or all three modes) as continuous tenure | The rarity claim falls; the thesis reduces to "this transition is hard." **Unrun** (`GAP-CTO-TRANSITION`). We bet low; we admit it is unmeasured. | **Q1** — see below |
| **F2** | Startups that split CTO/VP-Eng from seed (funding held constant) show *no better* technical-leadership survival than composite-CTO startups | The "uncushioned composite is the problem" claim weakens — it may be under-resourcing in general, not the missing split specifically. | **Q2** |
| **F3** | Founding CFOs and CMOs show *comparable* rotation-driven failure rates to founding CTOs | §1's "most severe case" narrows toward "one of several rotating roles" — the IC/management split would not be doing the sharpening we claim. | **Q3** |
| **F4** | CTO failure, measured *behaviorally* (tenure, replacement-at-boundary), is indistinguishable from other C-roles once legibility/visibility is controlled | The thesis documents a *visibility* gap, not a *severity* gap (concession 5 wins). | **Q4** |

**The wager, stated plainly:** the *behavioral* comparators — the n≈30,000 reversion rate, executive tenure data, the bare existence of the IC/management track split — will hold where *sentiment* and *anecdote* will not, because the split is a hard institutional fact (not a survey) and the reversion rate is a large-sample behavioral measure (not self-report). If a well-run study measuring behavior finds no difference, the thesis is wrong, and we would say so.

### 5c. The forward research program (the expansion — `R4`)

The falsifiers, as a commissionable program, ordered by leverage. The first would move the thesis more than all existing citations combined.

- **Q1 — The base-rate cohort study (the keystone).** Of founding CTOs, what fraction crosses Builder→Multiplier as continuous tenure; what fraction spans all three; what predicts which? This is `GAP-CTO-TRANSITION` — the number the entire thesis is shaped around and has never measured. *Method:* a defined cohort (e.g., all U.S. seed-funded startups with a titled technical co-founder, vintages 2014–2017), tracked via cap-table/PitchBook/LinkedIn to a terminal event, each trajectory coded (stayed-and-led / replaced-at-boundary / promoted-out / departed / company-died-first). **The denominator is the trick — you must include the dead companies, or you rebuild survivorship bias.** This is the one study designed to partially escape the survivorship floor (`U2`). Either result wins epistemically, because for once it could go against us.
- **Q5 — The inter-rater construct-validity study (the cheap precondition, run *first*).** Given CTO histories and *blinded to the three-mode scheme*, can independent raters reliably segment Builder/Multiplier/Strategist phases (Cohen's/Fleiss' κ)? Low agreement → the modes are an authorial overlay and concession 1's demotion to "descriptive lens" becomes mandatory, not precautionary — and the expensive cohort studies would be measuring an artifact. Should be run before Q1.
- **Q2 — The early-bifurcation natural experiment (F2).** Funding-matched seed-stage pairs: early CTO+VP-Eng split vs. composite-CTO controls; outcomes = 24-month technical-leadership retention, eng-org attrition, time-to-Series-B, cause-of-death coding. *Permanent confound:* founders who split early may differ in unobservable org sophistication — always quasi-experimental.
- **Q3 — The matched-comparator study (F3).** Same cap-table method, run in parallel for founding CFO/CMO/CTO; compare replacement-at-boundary rates and tenure curves. Tests whether the CTO is categorically distinct or the worst case on a shared continuum (the corpus's own CMO data predicts the latter).
- **Q4 — The documentation-artifact test (F4).** Behavioral signals only (involuntary-departure rates, tenure distributions, post-role trajectory, board-minute language) compared across C-roles, so the result does not depend on the failure being narrated.
- **Q6 — The CTO-specific mechanism study.** MW2018's own method: inductive interviews with ~30–45 CTOs across success/failure trajectories, coded for whether the transition followed offload-and-acquire or some other path (e.g., staying technical while leading effectively). This is the study that earns — or revokes — the corpus's right to apply MW2018/VL2023 to the CTO.
- **Q7 — The domain-axis effect study.** Within the Q1 cohort, code domain-identity-narrowness × company domain-dependency against transition outcome. The curse-of-expertise *mechanism* is peer-reviewed (`AX-CURSE`) but the CTO-level *effect* is unmeasured — a mechanism in search of an outcome.

### 5d. The permanent caveats (the part most agendas omit — `R4` Part III)

Intellectual honesty requires distinguishing *unstudied* from *unstudiable.* These are the latter: no future citation closes them; the honest move is to hold them as permanent caveats, not promissory notes.

- **U1 — The person-vs-structure counterfactual is unobservable.** The causal claim "the structure, not the person, produced this failure" requires running the same CTO twice. It cannot be done; even Q2 compares *different* companies. **The attribution is permanently underdetermined for any individual case** — which is exactly why §3 claims only *misattribution* (a default-attribution error, observable) and never "the structure caused this specific failure."
- **U2 — The survivorship floor on success-case work.** The CTOs who did what the survivors did and failed are in no sample, ever. Q1's denominator *partially* escapes this; the *narrative* success-case literature is permanently survivorship-bound. **Success cases illustrate; they never establish.**
- **U3 — Identity investment is self-report about the self.** The mechanism (degree of identity fusion with craft) is accessible only through self-report, and a CTO narrates identity-attachment differently *after* a failure than before. The mechanism may be real and **permanently unmeasurable cleanly**, because the instrument is part of the phenomenon.
- **U4 — "Irrational" is normative, not empirical.** Even with perfect base-rate data, whether the composite "should not exist" is a value question about whose welfare counts. Data can show it is *costly to the CTO* and *efficient for the system*; it cannot adjudicate "irrational." The only honest resolution is the one taken: drop "irrational," keep "under-resourced" (descriptive) and "individually costly" (measurable) — or argue the normative claim *as values, explicitly.*
- **U5 — The role is a moving target.** The thesis describes the 2014–2026 startup CTO. AI-generated code (already eroding some Builder-mode productivity metrics) may transform or collapse the Builder mode itself — in which case the taxonomy describes a configuration dissolving as it is documented. This is a **scope condition to state**, not a flaw to fix.

## Open adjacent research (flagged, per the brief — `TODO` T2/T4)

Two thesis-level questions are deliberately **out of scope here** and flagged as open, because they belong to a different locus than this work tested (the structure) and must not become a backdoor to re-assert the "irrational" claim concession 3 retired:

- **T2 — Is the irrationality in the cultural *expectation*, not the role design?** §3 establishes the *harm* is misattribution ("he's not scaling"). T2 asks: misattribution *against what expectation?* — and proposes the expectation itself may be the irrational object (a claim about *belief/attribution*, the corpus's most defensible ground, not about org design, which §4 conceded is rational-under-budget). The diagnostic — *"do you actually expect one person to transition all three modes?"* — splits cleanly: "no, of course not" → CTOs are judged against an unendorsed standard; "yes" → an expectation sincerely held but (per §1–§4) exceeding one person's reach. Either branch relocates the irrationality from structure to belief. **Discipline:** survives only if cleanly separable from the role-design claim §4 conceded; if it cannot be separated, it does not survive. *Proposed study:* the expectation audit (do CTO JDs / diligence questions / board-review criteria implicitly demand all three modes at once?) + the reflection-gap test.
- **T4 — Cultural expectations of intelligent people (a separate study).** The doctor thought-experiment ("surgery + colonoscopies + taxes + drive an 18-wheeler + launch rockets — where does it cross into absurd?") shows the reasonable/absurd line is *culturally drawn, not capacity-drawn.* T4 maps that line; T2 locates the CTO on it. The strongest available form is the **collective-akrasia** framing: the field accepts the Mythical Man-Month (people aren't fungible) *and* staffs/scales/writes JDs as if it were false — a *demonstrated contradiction in the field's own commitments* (the structure of "smoking is known to cause cancer and people still smoke"), which is value-judgment-proof and therefore the candidate form of "irrational" that survives the role-design rebuttal. **Discipline:** akrasia is itself a claim needing both halves evidenced — (a) the field genuinely accepts MMM (canonical, citable) and (b) it genuinely acts against it (the JD, headcount-as-capability, the composite CTO) — both plausibly documentable, neither yet *measured.* Falsifiable: if the belief is lip-service nobody endorses, the akrasia claim weakens to "the field is just wrong."

These are named, not folded in. Either could feed a future §3.5; neither enters this draft until researched and adversarially checked.

## The honest closing (for this audience)

The corpus's most valuable property is that **it left its seams visible** — it flagged its weak sources, declined to ship a harmful diagnostic, and corrected its own citation errors. The natural extension of that honesty is the one this version foregrounds: from *"here are the citations we haven't verified"* to *"here are the studies that could prove us wrong, and we haven't run them."*

A thesis that names the experiment that would kill it, commits to running it, and states plainly which of its questions *no* experiment can ever close — that is the difference between a framework and a finding. **This is a strong framework with strong component grounding and an unrun central experiment.** Q1 (with Q5 as its cheap precondition) is what would let one or two of its claims earn the word "finding"; §5d is the list of claims that must, honestly, stay "consistent with" forever. We present it as exactly that.
