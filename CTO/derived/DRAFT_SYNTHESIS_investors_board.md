# A Mispriced Risk and a Cheap Diligence Change
## What the founding-CTO transition looks like to the people who price it

There is a risk in your portfolio you are almost certainly underwriting and not pricing: the founding CTO who has to cross, alone and pre-scale, the one skill boundary the software industry formalized into two separate career tracks. It is the kind of risk that shows up not as a line item but as a recurring 12–24-month strategic gap each time a portfolio company cycles its technical leader — a gap the practitioner literature puts a number on (practitioner-sourced, not primary-verified), and one that, on inspection, the standard diligence reflex helps *select for* rather than screen out. None of this is a moral story about investors. It's an analytical one about a risk that's cheaper to reprice than to keep absorbing.

## The reflex that installed the demand

Historically, the diligence question was *"do they have a CTO?"* — a completeness check on the team slide, a named senior executive standing in for "technical execution risk is covered." At pre-seed and seed, with little traction to underwrite, you're betting mostly on people, and a filled C-suite reads as reduced risk. That reflex is not irrational; it's pattern-matching under genuine uncertainty.

But notice what the reflex does downstream, stated at the strength the evidence supports: **the evidence is consistent with the CTO title functioning as an investor signal** — a fundraising instrument as much as an org-design decision — re-installed each funding cycle by an incentive (signaling) orthogonal to organizational coherence. This is *signaling and co-authorship, not causation.* We are not claiming investors *caused* the failures; we're claiming the demand signal and the failure pattern are entangled — the party pricing execution risk is, on the evidence, a co-author of a structure whose difficulty later gets billed to the individual. The observation is sharper for being narrower: you don't have to have broken anything to be paying for it.

## The boundary you're funding across

The structural fact underneath, the one that needs no framework to verify: software built two formal, separately-laddered, separately-evaluated career tracks — individual contributor and management — and told engineers to choose. No other major function did this. The split is the industry's own admission, written into every tech-company career ladder, that doing the work and managing the people who do it are *different work*.

The whole function of that split is that most engineers never span both at once — they pick a ladder, the org staffs the other side. **The founding CTO is the one seat where neither side is staffed yet.** They're the IC *and* the manager *and* (the title implies) the executive, before the company is large enough to separate any of them. The institutional protection every other engineer gets — never having to hold both — is precisely what the founding seat lacks. And it is *across this boundary that your capital is deployed*: the seed check funds a single person to span a discontinuity the industry built two ladders to keep people from spanning.

You can confirm the industry treats this rotation as real by looking at the function it could afford to split earlier — sales. The senior closer who personally lands deals is doing different work from the person who builds and runs a revenue org, so the industry split the title: VP of Sales (operating) from Chief Revenue Officer (strategic, board-facing). Same craft→strategy rotation; the title got split the moment it was worth doing. **Engineering's founding seat is simply where that split hasn't been affordable yet — not where it's unnecessary.** The VP-Eng split you defer until post-Series-A isn't a luxury you're prudently delaying; it's a *cushion you've removed* from the seat for the stretch when the span is hardest and the support thinnest.

## The behavioral number, priced as a base rate

The load-bearing figure, and the reason it's load-bearing: it's *behavioral*, not sentiment — it measures what people did, in a large sample, across both those who succeeded and those who didn't.

**Roughly half of newly promoted engineering managers return to individual-contributor work.** Large-sample management research finds the same shape — around half of new managers and executives wash out inside 18 months (n≈30,000), with a corroborating ~40% executive failure rate from a separate source. Critically, the ~50% *holds at companies with dedicated management-development programs* — which is what makes it look structural rather than a training deficit you could underwrite your way around.

The diligence read: this is a **base rate at the boundary you fund across**, not a tail risk. (Discipline: this is the reversion rate for the IC→management transition the CTO-Builder must make — *not* a measured CTO-specific failure rate; that cohort study is unrun. It's the measured difficulty of the boundary, not a CTO failure number.) The reason it resists training is that crossing the boundary is an *identity* transition, not a skill upgrade — giving up being the person who does the work is a loss, not a promotion, and training doesn't touch it. (Peer-reviewed in *founders*; the founding-CTO application is this work's extension, not the studies' own subject.)

What follows is a cheap diligence change, not a new screen to build: **the question isn't "do they have a CTO?" but "what mode does this company need next, and is the person in the seat in it — or resourced to get there?"** Builder-stage company, Builder-mode CTO: matched. Scaling company, Builder-mode CTO with no VP-Eng timed to the boundary and no fallback: that's the mispriced risk, visible *before* it converts into a strategic gap. It costs nothing to ask, and it prices what the completeness check misses.

## The replacement loop is a cost you're already paying

Here is where the risk compounds. When a founding CTO hits the boundary, the language is consistent and personal — *"he's not scaling," "she's not strategic enough," "the company outgrew him."* Each is individually plausible; the *consistency* across companies and decades is the tell that a structural difficulty is being recorded, every time, as a fact about the person. The structure never enters the sentence, so it never gets examined, so it never gets resourced — which guarantees the next person in the seat hits the same wall.

The de-facto industry solution — cycle CTOs at the boundary until one fits, then cycle again — *is* that loop:

1. The structure produces predictable difficulty at the boundary.
2. The difficulty is attributed to the person.
3. The remedy is therefore individual — replace the CTO, hire "a stronger one."
4. The replacement enters the *same* unstructured boundary, with the *same* missing cushions.
5. Return to step 1.

This loop is a line item on your returns. Each cycle burns institutional knowledge and the 12–24-month strategic gap per displacement — and it never converges, because every iteration treats a recurring structural failure as a fresh individual one. **You are paying, repeatedly, for a failure the demand signal helped select for, and then penalizing the operator for it.** That's the mispricing in one sentence. The move isn't to fire faster or screen harder for a unicorn — that's the loop running harder. It's to stop penalizing the failure you co-authored and start pricing the cushion you removed.

## §4 from the inside: you're already pricing the composite

You price this composite better than anyone, because you watch what companies do with the money. The strongest objection to all of the above is the budget one: *a seed-stage startup can't staff three specialists — one capable generalist is all the round allows; the composite is rational under constraint.* That's true. And it concedes the point.

You only say *"we can't afford to split this job"* about a job that **should** be split — nobody says it about work one person handles comfortably. So the objection doesn't dispute that the span exceeds one person; it concedes it and pleads budget. This is a **revealed-preference argument, and it's number-independent** — it needs no cost figure to land (any headline dollar figure you'd recompute yourself anyway; the argument doesn't rest on one). It rests only on a behavior you observe across the whole portfolio: **companies distribute the composite the moment they can afford to** — CTO, VP-Eng, eng managers, staff engineers — exactly as they split CRO off VP-Sales. The capacity problem didn't vanish at seed stage; only the funding to acknowledge it did, replaced by a single title.

That's not a debating point — it's a pricing input. The composite is sustained by inability-to-fund-the-fix, which is *precisely why the cost lands on the person rather than the structure*, and why it's underwritten by you without appearing on any cap table. The arrangement may be perfectly efficient for the company — most startups die before the third mode matters, and "one affordable generalist, resource the transition when it's time" can be the rational play. We're not calling it irrational. We're saying the span is real, the deferral is a financing decision, and the deferred split is a priceable, plannable event rather than a surprise.

## F2 is your portfolio experiment to run

This audience respects stated disconfirmers, so here are two, with the actionable one foregrounded.

**F2 — the early-split experiment (yours to run).** If startups that split CTO / VP-Eng from seed — *funding held constant* — showed **no better** technical-leadership survival than composite-CTO startups, then "the uncushioned composite is the problem" weakens; it would be under-resourcing in general, not the missing split specifically. You are one of the few actors positioned to actually run this. You have the cohort: across a portfolio (or a syndicate's), early-split companies and composite-CTO companies, with stage and funding controlled. Track technical-leadership continuity and replacement-at-boundary. If early-split survives better, you've found a cheap structural intervention with a measurable return; if it doesn't, you've disconfirmed a claim worth disconfirming. Either outcome is information you can price — which is exactly why it's worth running rather than assuming.

**F1 — the base-rate falsifier (central, and unrun).** The thesis bets that a systematic cohort study of founding CTOs would find only a small fraction span the boundary as a continuous tenure. That study has not been done (`GAP-CTO-TRANSITION`); we bet the fraction is low and admit it is unmeasured. If a well-run study found a *substantial* fraction crossing successfully, the rarity claim falls and this reduces to "the transition is hard." The bet is on *behavioral* comparators — the reversion rate, executive tenure, the bare existence of the track split — holding up where sentiment and anecdote won't.

## The one-paragraph version

The founding CTO is funded to span, alone and pre-scale, a boundary the software industry built two separate career tracks to keep people from spanning — and roughly half the field reverts when it crosses that boundary even with good training, because it's an identity switch, not a skills gap. The diligence reflex that asks "do they have a CTO?" is, on the evidence, *consistent with* the title functioning as a fundraising signal — co-authoring, not causing, a structure whose later difficulty gets billed to the individual through a replacement loop that never converges and costs you a 12–24-month strategic gap per cycle. That's a mispriced risk you're already underwriting, and the fix is a diligence change, not a new screen: ask "what mode does this company need next, and is the seat in it or resourced to get there?" — and treat the deferred VP-Eng split as a cushion you removed, a plannable event rather than a surprise. The budget objection is your own revealed-preference admission that the job should be split: companies distribute the composite the instant they can afford to, exactly as they split CRO off VP-Sales. And there's a portfolio experiment only you can run — do early-split startups survive technical leadership better, funding held constant? Run it, and you price a structural risk the completeness check has been quietly missing.
