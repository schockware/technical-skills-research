# Abstract — CTO

*Folder-level abstract. Summarizes the flagship case study for a reader who wants the claim before deciding whether to read further. For the rigorous spine, see [`DRAFT_SYNTHESIS.md`](DRAFT_SYNTHESIS.md); for audience-specific versions, see [`derived/`](derived/); for the honest boundary between solid and provisional claims, see [`README.md`](README.md).*

---

The software industry built two formal, separately-laddered career tracks — individual contributor and management — and told engineers to choose between them. No other major profession formalized that split the way software did. The founding-stage CTO is the one role positioned where neither side of that split has been staffed yet: before the company has scaled, and largely alone, they are asked to cross the exact discontinuity the industry's own org chart says is a different job — on a compressed timeline, without the fallbacks, honest labeling, or scaffolded transition that make the same rotation survivable in every profession that provides them (a respected craft-track fallback, an honest name for the career change, a multi-year supported transition).

The crossing is measurably hard: roughly half of newly promoted engineering managers revert to individual-contributor work, even at companies with dedicated management-development programs (CEB/Gartner, n≈30,000) — a large-sample behavioral number, not a survey. It is hard for a specific, peer-reviewed reason: role transition is an identity loss, not a task hand-off (Mathias & Williams 2018; Van Lancker et al. 2023, both *Journal of Business Venturing*, read in full) — established in founders generally; the founding CTO's severity is this corpus's argued extension, not the source papers' finding.

The paper's central move is not diagnosing the difficulty — many roles are hard — but tracing what happens after it arrives. When a founding CTO hits the wall, the language is remarkably consistent and remarkably personal ("he's not scaling," "she's not strategic enough"), and that consistency across companies and decades is the tell: a difficulty the structure would predict for almost anyone is recorded, every time, as a fact about the person. This misattribution is self-perpetuating — it produces the industry's de facto solution (replace the CTO, repeat) rather than the structural one (resource the transition), because a difficulty you can't name structurally, you can only fix by swapping the person. The contribution is a precise, non-pejorative vocabulary that makes the structural version namable — the prerequisite to resourcing it, not a cure for it.

The strongest objection — "startups can't afford to hire three specialists for this" — turns out to concede the thesis: you only say you "can't afford to split a job" about a job that should be split. The industry proves it believes this by distributing the composite the moment it can afford to (CTO, VP Eng, EMs, staff engineers) and by splitting the analogous craft→strategy rotation in sales (VP Sales / CRO) — a cushion engineering's founding seat never got.

**What this does not claim.** Not that the CTO is uniquely broken — it is the most severe case of a rotation the whole C-suite shares (confirmed by the sibling comparator program in [`../Cultural Expectations/`](../Cultural%20Expectations/): the composite demand is C-suite-wide). Not that the arrangement is irrational — the composite may be a rational, efficient bet for the company; the harm lands on the person, not on the logic. Not that the headline compression ratios or survivorship numbers are proven — the draft explicitly declines to lean on them. The paper stakes itself on four named falsifiers (an unrun cohort study foremost among them) and states, in its own voice, what would prove it wrong.
