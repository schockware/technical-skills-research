## 2.1 — JD Archaeology

**Argument being tested:** CTO job descriptions encode an unreasonable composite demand — Builder (IC technical work), Multiplier (team leadership), Strategist (business/board/external) — simultaneously, without acknowledging these are distinct modes requiring different cognitive states, different skill sets, and different organizational contexts.

**Status:** Template pass complete (2 of 4 URLs usable). Real JD pass pending. Two URLs require replacement (one 403, one wrong role).

**Methodology note:** Templates are the most load-bearing artifact — they are the source of the lossy compression chain. Real JDs are downstream copies. Template audit establishes the canonical composite; real JD audit tests how faithfully it propagates and whether org demographics modulate it.

### Cross-Template Observations (raw)

**Scope-limiting language:** Consistently absent or partial. URL 1: entirely absent. URL 3: one downward bound only (CTO vs. VP of Engineering). No template examined bounded the CTO upward or laterally — no template stated what the CTO does not own, is not responsible for, or cannot be asked to do.

**Mode distribution pattern:** Strategist-dominant in both usable templates. Builder is present in URL 3 but consistently bundled with Strategist ("own the AI strategy, including foundation model selection"). Builder in URL 1 is structurally absent from responsibilities while present in credential requirements — the halo does the work of bridging them.

**The credential stacking pattern:** Both usable templates require deep Builder-origin credentials (10–12+ years technical experience) as entry requirements while assigning Strategist-dominant responsibilities. The Builder credential is the entry gate; the role as performed is Strategist + Multiplier. The title then carries all three modes as capability signals regardless of which are actually exercised.

**What would falsify the composite demand claim:** A template that: (a) names the three modes explicitly, (b) assigns responsibilities to specific modes, (c) states which mode is primary for this role/stage/company, or (d) includes scope-limiting language specifying what the CTO does not own. None of the usable templates do any of these. The absence of scope-limiting language in every template examined is itself a finding — the composite is the template's default, not a deviation from it.

**Hypothesis to test in real JD pass:** Does org stage modulate the composite? Prediction: seed-stage JDs load Builder + Strategist; growth-stage JDs load Multiplier + Strategist; but all stages retain the composite framing without naming the tension.

**Methodological constraint — JD transience:** JDs disappear when the role is filled. The progression from seed → Series A → Series B cannot be captured longitudinally for any single company without real-time archival. Two workarounds:

1. **Cross-sectional sampling:** Capture JDs across companies at different stages simultaneously (10 seed, 10 Series A, 10 Series B). Infer the progression pattern from the population rather than tracking any single company. Wellfound is a good source for this — listings typically include funding stage, headcount, and founding year, providing demographic tagging at the point of capture. Limitation: establishes correlation between stage and JD content, not causation. Sufficient for the structural argument.

2. **Post-mortem as longitudinal proxy (bridge to 2.2):** "Why we let our CTO go" narratives (section 2.2) frequently timestamp the failure to a specific stage transition — "we were fine until Series B" or "the wheels came off when we hit 50 engineers." The failure narrative reconstructs the implicit JD that was in force at the failure moment, even if the posted JD no longer exists. The post-mortem is the longitudinal data point the JD database can't provide. **2.1 and 2.2 should be read together:** 2.1 establishes what the composite looks like at the point of hire; 2.2 establishes where in the stage progression the composite fails and how the failure is attributed. Together they bracket the demand: what was asked (2.1) and what happened when it couldn't be delivered (2.2).

### Real JD Pass — Findings (deep-research run wf_565535ba-e35, June 28, 2026)

**What was confirmed, what was disconfirmed, and what remains under-powered — in order of confidence.**

⚑ **Source-class ceiling (stated up front).** No peer-reviewed source appears in this corpus. The behavioral artifacts are content-site / recruiter-template / jobs-platform sources (Wellfound, cto.academy, startups.com, softkraft, LinkedIn Talent Solutions) — but *these are exactly the artifacts the argument is about* (the JD as the lossy compression artifact), so source quality matches the descriptive claim. The strongest sources are **Spencer Stuart** and **SPMB** (named exec-search firms, but writing marketing copy) and **Camille Fournier** (named practitioner, 2015 normative opinion, not data). Treat confidence accordingly.

#### Finding 1 — CONFIRMED (3-0): the composite is the default JD framing, with no named tension

Real CTO JDs frame the role as a three-mode composite (technical/architecture **+** people-leadership/hiring **+** business/board strategy) presented as a **single unified responsibility set**, with no scope-limiting language and no named primary mode. Wellfound (fetched 2026-06-28), verbatim, lists all modes as parallel: technical strategy/roadmap; "Lead the engineering team in developing and deploying high-quality software products"; "attract and retain top technical talent… motivate and inspire team members"; "Work with other executives to identify opportunities for innovation." Two independent WebFetch passes confirmed **no** tension/tradeoff language — the JD "presents them as complementary rather than competing priorities." Corroborated by cto.academy, startups.com, Indeed, Monster, LeadDev, CIO.com. **This is the template-pass finding confirmed on real-JD-class artifacts: the composite is the default, not a deviation.**

#### Finding 2 — CONFIRMED (3-0): the composite is STAGE-MODULATED but never names the tension

The mode-loading shifts by stage exactly as the template pass hypothesized — and the composite framing is retained at every stage:
- **SPMB** (named exec-search firm) gives three stage-tagged archetypes verbatim: "**The Architect (Early-Stage)**… capable of writing code, selecting the initial tech stack, and shipping the first version"; "**The Scaler (Growth-Stage)**… grow an engineering organization from 10 to 100+ people"; "**The Strategic Operator (Enterprise)**… translates technology strategy into business outcomes for the board."
- **startups.com** gives engineer-count bands: early (1–10) "Writes most of the code personally"; growth (10–50) "spends more time on architecture, hiring, and team building"; scale-up (50–500+) "**Rarely writes production code**; serves as executive technology leader."

⚑ The hypothesis from the template pass ("seed loads Builder+Strategist; growth loads Multiplier+Strategist; all retain the composite without naming the tension") is **confirmed**. Caveat: engineer-count brackets are illustrative bands, not empirically derived; softkraft is aggregated boilerplate.

#### Finding 3 — CONFIRMED (medium): scope-limiting language is structurally ABSENT

Wellfound (two WebFetch passes): "No such sentence exists… no mention of difficulty, overload, conflict, or the role asking too much of a single person." cto.academy: "No scope-limiting language (what the CTO does NOT own) and no named primary mode." ⚑ Confidence is **medium not high** — this is a negative finding by inspection of a small sample (two JDs directly tested for the absence), not an exhaustive corpus audit.

#### Finding 4 — CONFIRMED (3-0): the tension is named ONLY in advice/commentary, never in the JD

The composite-demand tension surfaces explicitly **only** in prescriptive commentary. startups.com names it verbatim: "**The unhealthy path: trying to be both hands-on builder and executive leader without splitting the role, leading to burnout and gaps in both**" — plus "Don't drift between modes; choose deliberately." Corroborated by **Camille Fournier** (ex-CTO, *The Manager's Path*): the operational VP-Eng role and strategic CTO role are each "a full-time job," and one-person dual-handling "breaks down as the organization scales" (~20–50 engineers). **This is the key structural point: the people who *describe* the role name the tension; the artifacts that *demand* the role (JDs) never do.** The JD is where the compression happens — the tension is stripped at the point of hire.

#### Finding 5 — CONFIRMED (3-0): authoritative voices say the role is "an executive first, a technologist second"

Camille Fournier (named practitioner), verbatim: "CTO is not an engineering role. CTO is not the top of the technical ladder"; "The CTO is **an executive first, a technologist second**"; "it's a business strategy job, first and foremost. It's also a management job." Spencer Stuart (top-tier exec-search firm): "there is **no one-size-fits-all** approach when it comes to engineering and technology leadership." ⚑ **Internal tension worth keeping (not a synthesis error):** Fournier says the CTO is *not* centrally hands-on, while early-stage JDs load *heavily* toward hands-on Builder work. The disagreement is itself evidence of the role's ill-definition — "what the role IS" depends on whether you privilege the prescriptive practitioner or the descriptive JD.

#### Finding 6 — THE LOAD-BEARING COMPARISON: under-powered; tilts toward CTO-specificity but NOT decisive

This is the disconfirming test the priority doc flagged (if CFO/CMO/CRO are equally composite, the CTO isn't special). **The honest result: suggestive, not resolved.**
- The **one fully-verified comparator** — the LinkedIn **CRO** template — is *more cleanly scoped to leadership/strategy* than the CTO. Its Required skills gate on **leadership tenure** ("Ten or more years in diverse leadership roles"), "P&L supervision," and "business strategy" — with **zero** individual-contributor / deal-closing requirement. Its only "hands-on" wording modifies *leadership style*, not IC work. Corroborating (steerlab.ai): "a CRO does not manage salespeople or close deals." This tilts toward CTO-specificity: the CRO does **not** retain craft the way the CTO is expected to.
- **BUT the comparison set is thin.** The parallel **CMO** claims — that the CMO is *equally* composite (which would weaken CTO-specificity) — were **REFUTED** at verification (Indeed CMO three-mode claim 1–2; CMO hands-on-IC claim 0–3). And **no CFO JD claim survived.** So the comparison rests on a single CRO data point.

⚑ **Verdict: the retained-Builder composite is plausibly CTO-specific, but this is NOT decisively established.** It remains possible that all C-suite JDs are composite and the corpus simply lacked verified CFO/CMO composite evidence. **This is the one open evidentiary gap in 2.1** — a targeted audit of verbatim CFO and CMO JDs at matched stages would resolve it. (See Open Questions.)

#### Finding 7 — Builder-atrophy paradox: LOW confidence / unresolved

The *descent* of hands-on work by stage is well-supported (scale-up CTOs "rarely write production code"; Fournier's "executive first"). But the **specific** paradox framing — *a deep technical credential is required as an entry gate while the technical work is not actually done day-to-day* — **failed verification at the JD level** (LeadDev 1–2; Wellfound 1–2). The paradox is plausible as a directional observation but **not cleanly established from JD text**. ⚑ This *softens* the template pass's confident "Builder-atrophy is a named finding" — at real-JD level it's directional, not proven. Treat as unresolved.

## 2.4 — The Reflection-Gap Test

**Status:** Written as analysis on the Track 5 mechanism core (5.6) + the 4.7 founder-separation accounts + the 2.1 finding that the tension is named only in commentary — per STOP_LINE §5 ("construct it, don't hunt for it"; direct reflection-gap evidence is rare by definition, plan line 140). **No separate research run.**

**The diagnostic question (from the plan):** *"Do you actually expect a CTO to be an excellent hands-on architect, an excellent engineering people leader, and an excellent board-level strategist — simultaneously, across the company's growth from seed to scale?"*

---

### The mechanism is now grounded — but as a DIAGNOSTIC, not an intervention

The plan's Dependency Note #2 made 2.4 contingent on whether the Fernbach/IOED effect transfers from policy beliefs to role-design beliefs. **Track 5 (5.6) resolved this with a sharp split** — and it changes what 2.4 can claim:

- **The reflection gap itself is real and transfers** (Rozenblit & Keil 2002; Alter et al. 2010; Zeveney & Marsh 2023). Asking a decision-maker to explain the *mechanism* — "by what process does one person hold all three modes across seed→scale?" — reliably reduces their felt understanding. The illusion of explanatory depth is specific to exactly this kind of causal-mechanistic belief, and the puncturing move is demanding a mechanism (not a justification). **2.4's question is well-formed: it must ask *how*, not *why*.**
- **But the *moderation* of the expectation does NOT reliably follow** (Crawford & Ruscio 2021 — three failed preregistered replications; Sloman & Vives 2022 — works only for consequentialist, not value/identity beliefs; Zeveney & Marsh 2023 — the humility produced is diffuse, not targeted). The behavior-change leg has **never been tested on organizational/role/hiring beliefs**, and the genius-composite expectation is plausibly held as an *identity/status* belief (Axis 0/7/8), exactly the kind Sloman & Vives predicts explanation bounces off.

**So 2.4 is reframed:** it is a **diagnostic that locates the irrationality**, not an **intervention that dissolves it.** The reflection gap reliably *exposes* that the asker cannot explain the mechanism they are enforcing; it does not reliably *change* what they enforce. Writing 2.4 as a fix would overstate the evidence.

### What each answer reveals (unchanged structure, sharpened reading)

| Answer | What it locates | Track 5 reading |
|---|---|---|
| **"No, of course not"** | Applied standard ≠ endorsed standard. The gap *is* the irrationality's location. | The reflection gap fired cleanly — the asker, forced to the mechanism, can't endorse what their JD enforces. The most sophisticated answer — and the one the JD evidence actually supports. |

---

### The reflection gap as the bridge between 2.1 and the failure record (4.7 / future 2.2)

The strongest *constructed* evidence for the reflection gap is the **before/after contrast in founder-separation accounts** (plan line 140). 2.1 established that the tension is named only in *commentary about* the role (Fournier: VP-Eng and CTO are "each a full-time job," breaks down ~20–50 engineers; startups.com's "unhealthy path… burnout"), never in the JD that hires for it. That is the reflection gap *in the literature itself*: the people reflecting on the role can articulate the mechanism failure; the artifact that enforces the role compresses it out.

The prediction 2.4 makes testable: **a founder who has lived a CTO failure will describe a pre-failure expectation closer to the composite JD, and a post-failure expectation closer to the stage-modulated archetypes.** The gap between those two is the reflection gap with a timestamp — and it is the same Builder→Strategist transition the IOED question targets. This is the bridge to 2.2 (post-mortems): 2.1 = what was asked at hire; 2.2 = what was learned at failure; 2.4 = the question that, asked *before* the failure, would have surfaced the learning early.

---

### The honest limit (carried to synthesis)

⚑ **Do not let synthesis present 2.4 as an intervention that fixes the composite expectation.** The evidence supports it as:
1. A **diagnostic** that reliably reveals the applied-vs-endorsed gap (robust).
2. A **locator** of where the irrationality sits (in the gap, in the believer, or in an unspoken stage-theory).

The actual *lever* for changing the expectation is the **dissenter convergence** (5.1/5.2 — one credible person naming the composite as unreasonable), not the reflection gap. The strongest candidate intervention — combining the dissenter with the forced mechanistic explanation — is currently a hypothesis no study has tested (Track 5 open question). 2.4 should be written into the synthesis as **the diagnostic that makes the irrationality visible**, with the dissenter as the lever and the combination flagged as untested.
