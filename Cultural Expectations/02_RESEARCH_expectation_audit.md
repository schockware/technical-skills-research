## 2.1 — JD Archaeology

**Argument being tested:** CTO job descriptions encode an unreasonable composite demand — Builder (IC technical work), Multiplier (team leadership), Strategist (business/board/external) — simultaneously, without acknowledging these are distinct modes requiring different cognitive states, different skill sets, and different organizational contexts.

**Status:** Template pass complete (2 of 4 URLs usable). Real JD pass pending. Two URLs require replacement (one 403, one wrong role).

**Methodology note:** Templates are the most load-bearing artifact — they are the source of the lossy compression chain. Real JDs are downstream copies. Template audit establishes the canonical composite; real JD audit tests how faithfully it propagates and whether org demographics modulate it.

#### Template 1 — Indeed.com CTO Job Description Template
**URL:** https://www.indeed.com/hire/job-description/cto-chief-technology-officer
**Source class:** Recruiter platform / content site

**Responsibilities by mode:**

| Responsibility | Mode |
|---|---|
| Present reports on company technology status, goals, progress | Strategist |
| Create and implement technology strategies | Strategist |
| Align technology resources with short- and long-term goals | Strategist |
| Serve on executive committee to align technology goals | Strategist |
| Identify technologies to improve products and services | Builder / Strategist |
| Create and oversee high-level KPIs for IT department | Multiplier |
| Assist in recruiting, onboarding and training IT managers | Multiplier |
| Manage department budget | Strategist |

**Mode distribution:** Builder: 0 explicit. Multiplier: 2. Strategist: 5. Composite: 1.

**Builder is structurally absent** from the responsibility list — yet Builder-origin credentials are required (10+ years in IT, progression through technical support / development / programming / database / network administration / engineering). The template assumes Builder competence as the entry credential while assigning zero Builder responsibilities. The halo fires in the credential requirements; the role as described is Strategist + Multiplier only.

**Requirements:**
- Excellent verbal and written communication skills
- Organization and time-management skills; multitasking skills
- Knowledge of latest technological advances
- Knowledge of all functions and roles within IT department
- Knowledge of best information security practices
- Excellent leadership skills; ability to manage large budgets
- Bachelor's degree in computer science, information systems, or related field
- Business administration or management degree valued
- 10+ years experience in IT and management
- Entry-level progression: technical support → development/programming → database/network admin → engineering

**Scope-limiting language:** Absent. No statement of what the CTO does not do.

**Three most composite-demand sentences:**
1. *"communicating with other executives, performing research on new technologies that could enhance their business and monitoring the use and implementation of new and existing technologies across departments"* — collapses Strategist + Builder + Multiplier into one undifferentiated description
2. *"a background in IT and business development role experience"* — credential demand fuses Builder origin with Strategist function
3. *"innovative mindset to make beneficial changes to current technology usage among company departments, even when experiencing budget cuts"* — fuses Builder judgment with Strategist constraint management in a single cognitive demand

**Axis flags:**
- Axis 7 (halo): Builder credential required → Strategist responsibilities assigned; the halo is the bridge
- Axis 8 (capability signal): "CTO" signals all three modes; no scope limit in any direction
- Axis 11 (wild-originated language): "technology strategy," "IT leadership," "innovative mindset" — no scope information encoded in any term

#### Template 3 — Kore1.com CTO Job Description Template
**URL:** https://www.kore1.com/cto-job-description-template/
**Source class:** Recruiter platform / staffing agency template

**Responsibilities by mode:**

| Responsibility | Mode |
|---|---|
| Define and own company technology strategy and multiyear technical roadmap | Strategist |
| Lead and scale the engineering organization, hiring decisions, team structure | Multiplier |
| Make architecture and platform decisions, cloud infrastructure selection | Builder |
| Own AI and machine learning strategy, including foundation model selection | Builder / Strategist |
| Represent technology at board level, quarterly updates, risk reporting | Strategist |
| Partner with CEO, CPO, CFO on roadmap prioritization, build-versus-buy decisions | Strategist |
| Own engineering hiring strategy, headcount planning, compensation banding | Multiplier |
| Set and maintain standards for software quality, security posture, engineering productivity | Builder / Multiplier |
| Evaluate and manage key technology vendor relationships | Strategist |

**Mode distribution:** Builder: 1 explicit, 2 composite. Multiplier: 2 explicit, 1 composite. Strategist: 5 explicit, 2 composite.

**Requirements:**
- 12+ years of experience in software engineering or technical infrastructure
- At least 5 years in technology leadership at director level or above
- Demonstrated ownership of a technology roadmap at a company with 40+ engineers
- Direct experience making AI platform decisions in a production context
- Experience scaling an engineering organization through a significant growth event
- Strong command of cloud architecture on at least one major platform (AWS, Azure, GCP)
- Proven ability to communicate technology decisions to non-technical executives and board members
- *"Technically credible enough to evaluate architecture proposals critically"*

**Scope-limiting language:** One partial downward bound: *"A VP of Engineering manages what is. A CTO decides what needs to change and why."* — This draws a boundary downward (CTO is not day-to-day management) but does not bound the CTO upward or laterally. No mode is excluded.

**Three most composite-demand sentences:**
1. *"equally comfortable presenting a three-year technology roadmap to investors and spending the afternoon doing a deep technical review"* — Strategist + Builder simultaneous cognitive demand within a single working day; the word "equally" is the tell
2. *"managing VPs of Engineering, sitting in vendor negotiations worth millions, and may not have written production code in three or four years"* — Multiplier + Strategist + implicit Builder credential in one sentence; **notable: explicitly acknowledges Builder atrophy while retaining Builder-mode credential requirements**
3. *"Own the company's AI and machine learning strategy, including foundation model selection"* — collapses Builder-depth technical judgment (foundation model selection requires current hands-on knowledge) with Strategist-scope ownership

**The Builder-atrophy sentence is a named finding:** This template acknowledges that the CTO "may not have written production code in three or four years" while simultaneously requiring them to be "technically credible enough to evaluate architecture proposals critically" and make "AI platform decisions in a production context." This is the credential-as-capability-signal gap stated explicitly in the source document — the title retains Builder-mode authority after Builder-mode capability has demonstrably lapsed. Axis 8 named in the template itself.

**Axis flags:**
- Axis 7 (halo): "technically credible enough" — the halo is the bridge between lapsed Builder capability and ongoing Builder-mode authority
- Axis 8 (capability signal): credential retains scope it no longer accurately signals; explicitly stated in the document
- Axis 11 (wild-originated): "technically credible," "AI strategy," "engineering productivity" — capability signals with no scope boundary

### Cross-Template Observations (raw)

**Scope-limiting language:** Consistently absent or partial. URL 1: entirely absent. URL 3: one downward bound only (CTO vs. VP of Engineering). No template examined bounded the CTO upward or laterally — no template stated what the CTO does not own, is not responsible for, or cannot be asked to do.

**Mode distribution pattern:** Strategist-dominant in both usable templates. Builder is present in URL 3 but consistently bundled with Strategist ("own the AI strategy, including foundation model selection"). Builder in URL 1 is structurally absent from responsibilities while present in credential requirements — the halo does the work of bridging them.

**The credential stacking pattern:** Both usable templates require deep Builder-origin credentials (10–12+ years technical experience) as entry requirements while assigning Strategist-dominant responsibilities. The Builder credential is the entry gate; the role as performed is Strategist + Multiplier. The title then carries all three modes as capability signals regardless of which are actually exercised.

**What would falsify the composite demand claim:** A template that: (a) names the three modes explicitly, (b) assigns responsibilities to specific modes, (c) states which mode is primary for this role/stage/company, or (d) includes scope-limiting language specifying what the CTO does not own. None of the usable templates do any of these. The absence of scope-limiting language in every template examined is itself a finding — the composite is the template's default, not a deviation from it.

### Pending — Real JD Pass

**Org demographic dimensions to capture per JD:**
- Company stage (seed / Series A-B / growth / public / enterprise)
- Company size (headcount band)
- Industry vertical
- Technical complexity (greenfield / legacy / regulated / safety-critical)
- Reporting structure (reports to CEO / board / COO)
- Whether a CTO previously existed (first CTO vs. replacement)

**Collection method:** Under discussion. JDs are transient; Wayback Machine, LinkedIn historical data, and commercial JD databases (Burning Glass / Lightcast) are candidate sources. Manual capture with demographic tagging is the fallback.

**Hypothesis to test in real JD pass:** Does org stage modulate the composite? Prediction: seed-stage JDs load Builder + Strategist; growth-stage JDs load Multiplier + Strategist; but all stages retain the composite framing without naming the tension.

**Methodological constraint — JD transience:** JDs disappear when the role is filled. The progression from seed → Series A → Series B cannot be captured longitudinally for any single company without real-time archival. Two workarounds:

1. **Cross-sectional sampling:** Capture JDs across companies at different stages simultaneously (10 seed, 10 Series A, 10 Series B). Infer the progression pattern from the population rather than tracking any single company. Wellfound is a good source for this — listings typically include funding stage, headcount, and founding year, providing demographic tagging at the point of capture. Limitation: establishes correlation between stage and JD content, not causation. Sufficient for the structural argument.

2. **Post-mortem as longitudinal proxy (bridge to 2.2):** "Why we let our CTO go" narratives (section 2.2) frequently timestamp the failure to a specific stage transition — "we were fine until Series B" or "the wheels came off when we hit 50 engineers." The failure narrative reconstructs the implicit JD that was in force at the failure moment, even if the posted JD no longer exists. The post-mortem is the longitudinal data point the JD database can't provide. **2.1 and 2.2 should be read together:** 2.1 establishes what the composite looks like at the point of hire; 2.2 establishes where in the stage progression the composite fails and how the failure is attributed. Together they bracket the demand: what was asked (2.1) and what happened when it couldn't be delivered (2.2).
