# Standing and Limits

*Where [[__DISCLAIMERS_EXPECTATIONS_AND_BELIEFS]] fences the work, this one places me relative to it. See also [[README]] in Author's Experiences — the author submitting to the apparatus first.*

## On not being first

People have been looking at this far longer than I have — sociologists, historians, organizational theorists, the people who lived the occupational transitions firsthand. What surprised me is how little of that reaches the people setting expectations day to day. This research is not a discovery of new ground. It is an attempt to carry old ground into a room — SDLC management — where it somehow never arrived. Treat the framing as one practitioner's synthesis, not as a settled finding, and check it against the people who got here first.

## Why a logic engineer can write `1 + 0 != 4` and still be careful

I am not a neutral outside party calculating other people's rationality. I am a logic engineer. **I dance the line of assumptions, irrationality, and unreasonableness every single day** — it is the job. Software is the one place where "irrational" has a hard, checkable meaning, and where you are constantly discovering that what looked irrational was just not-yet-formalized, and what looked rigorous was an assumption you never noticed you made.

That is what earns the words — and what makes me distrust them. Living in that gap professionally is exactly why I will not pretend the same words behave the same way once they leave it.

## The false-precision trap

`1 + 0 != 4` reads like arithmetic. It is meant to. But beliefs, expectations, and assumptions are not the kind of object that arithmetic closes over. The calculable *feel* of this work is a rhetorical handle, not a measurement. Nothing here should be mistaken for having quantified a belief or proven an expectation irrational the way a compiler proves a type error. The rigor is in the decompression of the words and the discipline of the scope — not in a number.
