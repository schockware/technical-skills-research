# Language Precision Framework: "Common" vs. "Normal"
### Cultural Expectations Research — Style & Methodology Reference

---

## Preamble

Human language is inherently ambiguous and incomplete. This document does not assert that everyday usage of the words "common" and "normal" is wrong. Rather, it establishes precise working definitions for use within this research to prevent inadvertent harm, misapplication, or weaponization of findings.

> **How this fits the framework.** This is the **second word-pair gradient** in the area, parallel to the failure-word gradient in [[00_TAXONOMY]] (illogical/irrational/unreasonable). Same shape: a word that *prescribes* (normal) hiding inside the work a word that only *describes* (common) — exactly the word-compression the disclaimers guard against ([[__DISCLAIMERS_EXPECTATIONS_AND_BELIEFS]] guards #4–#6). "Common→Normal" is to *characterization* what "unreasonable→irrational" is to *failure*: both slide from describing a state toward sentencing it. The Access Restriction Loop below is the historical engine that the Author's Experiences fence ("verdict on the expectation, never the person") exists to interrupt. Enforced by [[language_auditor_prompt]]; companion docs [[failure_language_framework]] and [[witch_hunting_vs_process_gaps]]. See [[00_README]] for the map.

## Core Definitions

### Common
**Definition:** Occurring with statistical frequency; prevalent within a population or context.

> Diabetes is **common**. It affects a significant percentage of the population. Its frequency is measurable and documented.

### Normal
**Definition:** Conforming to a standard, baseline, or expectation; implies that deviation is aberrant or undesirable.

> Diabetes is **not normal** in this framework. Calling it normal would imply that non-diabetic physiology is a deviation, or that diabetes is an expected state one should accept without question.

---

## Why This Distinction Matters

The word "normal" does not merely describe — it **prescribes**. It carries an implicit judgment about what *should* be. When applied to health conditions, social circumstances, or cultural behaviors, "normal" can:

- Stigmatize populations by framing their experiences as aberrant
- Normalize harmful conditions by framing them as acceptable or expected
- Be weaponized to dismiss lived experiences ("that's just normal")
- Be weaponized to pathologize difference ("that's not normal")

By anchoring our language to **frequency** (common) rather than **prescription** (normal), we protect the integrity of the research and the dignity of the populations it describes.

## Illustrative Examples

| Statement | Preferred | Rejected | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| Diabetes affects 1 in 10 adults | Diabetes is **common** | Diabetes is normal | "Normal" implies healthy physiology is the aberration |
| Lead-based makeup was widely used historically | It was **common** | It was normal | "Normal" would imply it was safe or appropriate |
| Many workers exceed 40 hours per week | Overworking is **common** | Overworking is normal | "Normal" obscures that it may be harmful and systemic |
| Wage suppression affects many workers | Being underpaid is **common** | Being underpaid is normal | "Normal" forecloses the recognition that it is a problem |
| Vaccine uptake is high in many populations | Vaccination is **common** | Vaccination is normal | "Normal" implies the unvaccinated are aberrant rather than statistically less frequent |

## The Access Restriction Loop

A recurring pattern in historical and cultural research is the **manufactured evidence loop** — where an institution creates the conditions that produce an outcome, then cites the outcome as proof of the original assumption, while omitting the restriction that caused it.

### The Mechanism

```
Restriction → Reduced Access → Outcome Gap →
Gap cited as proof of incapacity →
Restriction justified → Original restriction omitted
```

### Real World Idiom Test Cases

These idioms are useful precisely because they are culturally familiar. Their familiarity makes the mechanism harder to see — which is why they are valuable audit anchors.

**"You throw like a girl"**
- Implies girls are constitutionally weaker throwers (capacity language)
- Girls who throw well are reclassified as exceptions ("she throws like a boy") rather than evidence against the assumption (reclassification)
- The practice gap caused by exclusion from sport disappears from the narrative entirely (omission)
- Outcome data now contradicts the assumption — female athletes in softball, baseball, cricket, and javelin are documented and measurable. The assumption did not update when access did. That lag is itself a data point. <!-- ⚑ ACCURACY-FLAG [accurate facts, watch rhetorical reach]: participation/performance in these sports is solidly documented. **"Women are bad drivers"**
- Women were restricted from or discouraged from driving, reducing accumulated drive time
- Reduced experience produced a measurable outcome gap
- The outcome gap was cited as proof of incapacity
- The original access restriction was omitted from the narrative
- The closed loop produced its own confirming evidence

### The Hidden Labor Variant

When explicit restriction is impractical or visible, labor can be **reclassified** to achieve the same omission:

```
Labor performed → Labor reclassified as clerical →
Credit flows upward to institution or supervisor →
Outcome data exists but is unlabeled or misattributed →
Incapacity assumption persists despite contradicting evidence
```

**Historical examples:**
- The ENIAC programmers (six women) were classified as sub-professional "computers," not engineers, and were excluded from the commemorative record — absent from the machine's 1946 public unveiling and from the histories until recovered by later research (Kathryn Kleiman's work and Jennifer Light's *When Computers Were Women*, 1999).
- Harvard Observatory women computers processed astronomical data at roughly 25–30 cents an hour — below what a man would have earned for the same work; Henrietta Leavitt's discovery of the period-luminosity relationship — the tool that let Hubble measure the universe — was issued in a 1912 Harvard Circular under director Edward Pickering's name, though the text itself credited the work to Leavitt.
- NASA (then NACA) human computers (Hidden Figures) were titled "computers" as a job category, depersonalizing the intellectual labor; when electronic computers arrived, the women programmed them — their expertise transferred, their status did not.
- Grace Hopper built the A-0 system (1952), widely credited as the first compiler, after being told it was impossible; COBOL, the language she pioneered, still underpins systems processing — by one widely-cited industry estimate (Reuters, 2017) — roughly $3 trillion in daily commerce.


The reclassification mechanism is particularly resistant to audit because the labor *is* documented — just under a different category that obscures its intellectual nature.

## Gradient Measurement Methodology

A key component of this research is measuring the **interpretive gradient** between "common" and "normal" as perceived by both human and AI respondents.

Respondents are asked to classify statements using one of the two terms. Their choices reveal embedded assumptions, cultural biases, and normative frameworks — not just in the subject matter, but in the respondents themselves.

### Example Probe Questions

- *Diabetes is ______ (common / normal)?*
- *Vaccinations are ______ (common / normal)?*
- *Overworking is ______ (common / normal)?*
- *Being underpaid is ______ (common / normal)?*
- *Working multiple jobs is ______ (common / normal)?*
- *Chronic stress is ______ (common / normal)?*

The gradient between responses — particularly where human and AI respondents diverge — surfaces culturally embedded assumptions that would otherwise remain invisible in the language.

## Flagged Language — Audit Reference

The following terms carry similar risks of prescriptive connotation and are subject to review in all research outputs. Risk level indicates how frequently the word carries implicit normative weight.

### High Risk — Almost Always Prescriptive
- **normal** *(primary flagged term)*
- **standard**
- **typical**
- **expected**
- **baseline**

### Context-Dependent — Review Required
- **regular** *(fine in "regular intervals," problematic in "a regular occurrence" implying acceptability)*
- **common** *(our preferred term, but can slip — e.g., "it's only common sense")*
- **average** *(statistical use is fine; "just average" carries judgment)*
- **conventional**

### Subtle / Structural Risk — Harder to Catch
These are not single words but **framing constructions** that smuggle in normative assumptions:
- *"as we would expect"*
- *"unsurprisingly"*
- *"as is typical"*
- *"it goes without saying"*
- *"of course"*
- *"naturally"*

These constructions are particularly difficult for human reviewers to catch because they feel like neutral transitions. AI-assisted auditing is recommended for this category.

## Enforcement & Auditing

This framework is enforced through:

1. **Style Guide** — This document, distributed to all contributors
2. **Methodology Section** — Rationale embedded in research documentation as an epistemological choice, not merely a stylistic one
3. **AI-Assisted Clause Review** — Automated flagging of high-risk and context-dependent terms across all prose, including structural framing constructions

AI auditing is preferred over human-only review for this task. Human reviewers develop familiarity blindness to their own habitual language. Automated review applies consistent scrutiny regardless of authorship.

---

## A Note on Scope

This framework applies to:
- All written research outputs
- Spoken language in formal research contexts and recorded sessions
- Anecdotes and prose used to illustrate findings

It does not retroactively declare everyday usage of "normal" to be harmful. The goal is precision within this research, not policing of language outside it.
