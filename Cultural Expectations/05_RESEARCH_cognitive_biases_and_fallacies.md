## 5.6 — Illusion of Explanatory Depth (the load-bearing section)

**Argument being tested:** IOED (Rozenblit & Keil 2002) / the Fernbach (2013) "explain-the-mechanism" effect is the empirical engine of the T2 reflection-gap test (2.4). The make-or-break question (plan Dependency Note #2): does the effect transfer from *policy* beliefs to *role-design/organizational* beliefs? If asking "explain exactly how you expect one person to do all three jobs" both (a) reveals the asker's mechanism gap *and* (b) moderates the composite expectation, then 2.4 is a real intervention rather than rhetoric.

**Status:** Complete first pass — **and the answer is a sharp, decision-relevant SPLIT.** The reflection gap transfers robustly; the *moderation* effect (the part 2.4 actually needs) does not. This is the most consequential finding of the run.

---

### Finding 1 — CONFIRMED (3-0): IOED is robust and SPECIFIC to causal-mechanistic knowledge

**Source class:** Peer-reviewed (Rozenblit & Keil 2002, *Cognitive Science* 26(5):521–562, 12 studies).

People "feel they understand complex phenomena with far greater precision, coherence, and depth than they really do" — the illusion of explanatory depth. Critically, "the illusion is **far stronger for explanatory knowledge** than many other kinds of knowledge, such as that for facts, procedures or narratives." The puncturing move is **generating a detailed causal/mechanistic self-explanation** — not listing reasons or facts. ⚑ This validates the *form* of the 2.4 question: it must demand a *mechanism* ("by what process does the Builder become a Strategist?"), not a justification ("why do you want a composite CTO?").

---

### Finding 2 — CONFIRMED (3-0): IOED transfers into SOCIAL/political domains; the mechanism is domain-general

**Source class:** Peer-reviewed (Alter, Oppenheimer & Zemla 2010, *JPSP* 99(3):436–451; Fernbach et al. 2013, *Psychological Science* 24(6):939–946).

Alter et al. (2010) Study 5: voters rated understanding of their favored candidate's policy higher *before* explaining (M=4.31) than *after* (M=3.53), t(97)=6.82, p<10⁻¹⁰, partial η²=.32 (large) — and blind raters confirmed the *post* ratings were well-calibrated, so the drop is pre-explanation *over*estimation, not post-explanation underestimation. The authors argue the mechanism (defaulting to abstract construal) is **domain-general**, explicitly naming *person perception* and *negotiation*. ⚑ Caveat: that social-domain transfer is theoretical extrapolation ("might"); only voting/policy was empirically tested. Study 6 isolates the lever: forcing **concrete vs. abstract construal** (write HOW vs. WHY) switches the illusion off (concrete condition: illusion absent, p=.14).

**Fernbach (2013) core, confirmed (3-0):** explaining *how a policy works* reduced self-rated understanding (M 3.82→3.45, p<.001, η²=.15) **and** moderated position extremity (mediated by uncertainty) — and **enumerating reasons did NOT** (no extremity change). The mechanism-vs-reasons distinction is real and load-bearing.

---

### Finding 3 — THE CRITICAL DISCONFIRMATION (3-0): the MODERATION effect is fragile; only the reflection gap reliably transfers

**Source class:** Peer-reviewed (Crawford & Ruscio 2021, *Psychological Science* 32(4):611–621, three preregistered replications, N=306/405/343 — larger than originals; corroborated by Walker et al. 2024, *Political Psychology*; Sloman & Vives 2022).

This is the finding that reshapes 2.4. Crawford & Ruscio (2021): "Although participants reported **less policy understanding** after providing mechanistic policy explanations, **policy-position extremity and in-group favoritism were unaffected.** None of the key effects were statistically significant." The literature now **separates two effects**:
- **The illusion-of-understanding reduction (the reflection gap) — REPLICATES robustly.**
- **The attitude/position moderation — does NOT replicate reliably.**

⚑ **Direct consequence for the framework:** asking the asker to explain the composite role's mechanism will *reliably* make them **feel less certain they understand the role** — but it will **not reliably move the overloaded expectation itself.** The reflection gap is real; the behavior change is not established.

---

### Finding 4 — BOUNDARY CONDITION (3-0): even where moderation holds, it works only for CONSEQUENTIALIST beliefs, not value/identity-laden ones

**Source class:** Peer-reviewed (Sloman & Vives 2022, *Cognition* 225:105146).

"Understanding and attitude extremity are reduced after explanation **but only for consequentialist issues, not those based on protected values.**" Consequentialist extremity decreased (p=.002); protected-values null (p=.95). Mechanism: when a belief is housed in protected/identity values, people treat the mechanistic explanation as *irrelevant* and it has no effect. ⚑ **Refinement that matters for 2.4:** frames aren't fixed — *requesting an explanation can itself shift a belief from a value-frame to a consequentialist one.* So the intervention's success hinges on whether the composite-CTO expectation is held as a *causal/outcome* belief ("this will produce better outcomes") or an *identity/status* belief ("a real CTO is a genius who does everything"). **If it's the latter — which Axis 0/7/8 suggest it often is — explanation is predicted to bounce off.**

---

### Finding 5 — "LINKED ILLUSIONS" (3-0): the humility produced is DIFFUSE, not a targeted correction

**Source class:** Peer-reviewed (Zeveney & Marsh 2023, *Judgment and Decision Making* 18:e24, three preregistered experiments).

Explaining *one* phenomenon reduces self-rated understanding of *unrelated* phenomena (explaining a zipper lowered reported understanding of how snow forms), across mechanical, natural, *and* political-economic systems. ⚑ But three scope limits cut against the framework: (a) a copy-control condition also produced a smaller drop (part is generic engagement), though explanation produced significantly more; (b) the *explained* item itself showed *increased* understanding; (c) the DV is reduced *felt understanding*, not attitude moderation. The "linked illusions model" holds that the effect is "a temporary increase in **intellectual humility**" generalized diffusely — **not a precise downward revision of the specific belief explained.** So "explain how this person does all three jobs" likely yields *diffuse felt-ignorance*, not a targeted deflation of the composite expectation.

---

### 5.6 Verdict — What the reflection-gap intervention can and cannot do

| Component | Status | Implication for 2.4 |
|---|---|---|
| Reveals the asker's mechanism gap (felt-understanding drop) | **Robust, transfers across causal domains** | 2.4 *can* reliably produce the reflection gap |
| Moderates the overloaded expectation (attitude change) | **Fragile; 3 failed preregistered replications** | 2.4 must NOT claim it changes the expectation |
| Works on consequentialist beliefs | Yes | Works *if* the expectation is held as outcome-belief |
| Works on value/identity beliefs | **No** (explanation treated as irrelevant) | Likely bounces off the genius-archetype (Axis 0/7/8) |
| Produces targeted correction of the specific belief | **No — diffuse humility only** | The gap is general, not aimed at the composite |

**The honest framing for 2.4:** the reflection-gap *test* is empirically grounded as a **diagnostic** (it reliably exposes that the asker can't explain the mechanism). It is **not** grounded as an **intervention** that moderates the expectation — that inference rests on the *non-replicating, value-bounded* Fernbach moderation effect and has **never been tested on organizational/role/hiring beliefs.** 2.4 should be written as a diagnostic with an *honest* note that the behavior-change leg is unproven.

## 5.1 — Obedience to Authority (Milgram)

⚑ **UNVERIFIED-IN-CORPUS.** The Milgram claims did not enter this run's adversarial verifier (the verification budget was consumed by the IOED side). Figures below are textbook-canonical and corroborated by the named replications, but were **not** adversarially checked this pass — treat as provisional pending a cheap verification pass.

**Argument being tested:** authority-compliance keeps people endorsing/enacting a demand they privately doubt; the **disobedient-peer** variation is the predicted intervention lever (one person refusing collapses compliance).

**Core (provisional):** Milgram (1963/1974) baseline full-compliance ~65%; the **disobedient-confederate** condition dropped full obedience to ~10% — the single largest situational moderator in the paradigm. Modern/organizational replications cited as support: **Burger (2009)**, *American Psychologist* — ~70% compliance to the 150V threshold in a contemporary US sample; **Dolinski et al. (2017)**, *Social Psychological and Personality Science* — ~90% in Poland; **Meeus & Raaijmakers (1986)**, *European Journal of Social Psychology* — "administrative obedience," participants harmed a job applicant when instructed by an authority, situating the effect in an **evaluation/hiring-adjacent** context. ⚑ All figures unverified this pass.

## 5.2 — Conformity and Social Proof (Asch)

⚑ **UNVERIFIED-IN-CORPUS** (same caveat as 5.1).

**Argument being tested:** the composite expectation is *socially reproduced through conformity*, not generated by any individual from first principles; a single dissenter restores private judgment.

**Core (provisional):** Asch (1951–1956) — ~33–37% conformity on critical trials despite participants privately knowing the answer; **a single dissenting ally cut conformity by ~75–80%.** **Bond & Smith (1996)**, *Psychological Bulletin* — meta-analysis of 133 studies across 17 countries; conformity is cross-culturally general, varying by individualism/collectivism. ⚑ All figures unverified this pass.

## The Convergence — the framework's predicted intervention

**Milgram (disobedient peer) and Asch (single dissenter) point to the SAME lever: one credible dissenter breaking perceived unanimity dramatically reduces compliance/conformity.** This is the framework's most actionable prediction — "one credible person in the hiring room naming the composite as unreasonable" — and it is a textbook-robust pattern across both paradigms.

⚑ **But weigh the two legs honestly (this is the run's key strategic finding):**
- **The dissenter leg (5.1/5.2) is the ROBUST leg** of the intervention — but its exact magnitudes are *unverified-in-corpus this pass* (canonical, not yet checked here).
- **The reflection-gap leg (5.6) is the EMPIRICALLY WEAKER leg** — the diagnostic transfers, the *moderation* does not.

So the framework's intervention story should **lead with the dissenter** (robust mechanism, pending number-verification) and treat the **reflection gap as a diagnostic, not a lever.** The plan had these weighted the other way around in places; this run inverts that emphasis.

**Untested combination (open question):** does combining (a) a single credible dissenter naming the expectation as unreasonable with (b) a forced concrete mechanistic explanation produce additive/interactive moderation? No study tests it. This is the strongest *candidate* intervention but is currently a hypothesis, not a finding.
