## 2. The trade-off, named plainly

### Draft B is the stronger *rhetoric* — and that is exactly the problem.

"The expectation is **irrational** — no one can be an excellent builder, people-leader, and strategist
at the same time" is a punchy, intuitive, almost self-evident sentence. It will persuade a reader in
one line. **That persuasive force is borrowed from a claim about a person's cognitive capacity.** The
sentence's grammatical subject is "the expectation," but its *truth-condition* is "no **one** can" — a
fact about people. The strength is real and the strength is the liability:

- **It re-imports role-design.** The adversarial pass (R1 Lead 1) retired "the role itself is
  irrational." Draft B's §1 verdict reduces to "the role demands the impossible," with the subject
  swapped back to "the expectation." That is graveyard **G-6** — the move the corpus already ruled out.
- **It is one pronoun from a fence violation.** Taxonomy §4.1: "irrational" *migrates toward a mind*
  ("you are irrational"); "not-reasonable" stays on the expectation ("the demand is unreasonable").
  Draft B's load-bearing word is the migrating one. The verdict reads as on-the-expectation on the page
  and lands on the person the moment it is spoken aloud about a *specific* CTO who failed: "the role was
  irrational" becomes "he was irrational to think he could do it." **The grammar does the targeting
  whether or not the author intends it** (§4.1's central rule).
- **It is unfalsifiable as stated.** "You can't occupy three states at once" is asserted from intuition,
  not measured. R2 flagged this: a verdict that no observation can overturn is not a finding.

### Draft A is the weaker *argument* — and that is its safety.

"The standard is applied context-free to a role everyone's own commentary calls context-dependent, and
failure is blamed on the person instead of the standard" is a longer, less punchy sentence. It will not
win a reader in one line. But:

- **It cannot be weaponized the same way.** Its truth-condition is a property of the *artifact and the
  judge* — "the JD omits the trade-off," "the commentary names it," "failure was attributed to the
  individual." None of these is a claim about what a person can hold. There is no capacity verdict to
  migrate onto the person.
- **It is falsifiable.** It fails on observable evidence (do JDs localize to stage? does commentary name
  the trade-off?). That exposure is what makes it a finding rather than an intuition.
- **It pays for the safety in reach.** It says *less*. It does not get to claim the composite is
  impossible — only that it is **issued and judged badly**. Anyone who wanted the dramatic "the CTO job
  is irrational" headline does not get it from Draft A.

> **The exchange in one sentence:** Draft B buys persuasive force by spending the fence; Draft A keeps
> the fence by spending persuasive force. **The taxonomy's own §1.4 rule already chose** — default to
> *not-reasonable*, never *not-rational* — which is to say the corpus's own language layer prefers Draft
> A's discipline over Draft B's punch.

## 3. The three other divergences (same pattern, smaller stakes)

The CTO verdict is the sharpest case, but A and B split the same way — stronger-but-exposed vs.
weaker-but-defensible — on three more claims. Each is a place Draft B's confidence outruns its evidence
and Draft A demoted it.

| Claim | Draft B (stronger / exposed) | Draft A (weaker / defensible) | Adversarial ruling |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Master variable** | "Visible attributable harm" is the master variable behind professional brakes. | No master variable; brakes install via *multiple co-equal pathways* (Abbott). | **A.** B re-commits the exact single-master-variable over-reach the corpus claims to have learned from (G-2). |
| **The lever** | The dissenter is the *robust* intervention. | The framework has **no verified lever** — only a robust *diagnostic* (the reflection gap). | **A.** B's "robust" rests on figures unverified-in-corpus; confidence runs inverse to evidence (G-4). |
| **CTO-specificity** | The composite is CTO-typical, stated without hedge. | A claim about C-suite hiring artifacts *generally*, pending CFO/CMO comparison. | **A.** B asserts specificity the corpus never tested ("under-powered" → honestly "untested", G-1). |
| **Lineage / amnesia** | "Descends from Fayol"; amnesia stated plainly. | Fayol at **origin only** (transmission a gap); amnesia at **low confidence**. | **A.** B states as settled what the corpus left as a gap (G-5) and a weak instrument. |

The pattern is identical every time: **Draft B says more and exposes more; Draft A says less and holds.**

---

## 4. What each draft is *for*

- **Draft A is the one to build the public synthesis on.** It is the post-adversarial residue; it honors
  the fence; it survives R1/R2; its claims are falsifiable. Where it is weak, it says so (the §8 open
  gaps).
- **Draft B is the one to keep as the specimen and the warning.** It is the corpus *demonstrating on
  itself* the exact slide the "Expectations and Beliefs" layer exists to catch: a verdict that feels
  strongest precisely because it has quietly crossed from the expectation onto the person. Keeping B
  visible is more honest than deleting it — it shows the reader the temptation and why it was refused.

---

## 5. The recommendation

**Ship Draft A. Retain Draft B as a labeled contrast.** Where the eventual public writing wants the
force of Draft B's "irrational" headline, it must instead do the harder work Draft A requires: show the
stage-contingency, show the JD's omission, show the mis-attribution — and let the reader conclude the
demand is not-reasonable, rather than telling them a person could not do it. **The fence is not a
softening of the argument; it is the part of the argument that keeps it from becoming the weapon it
studies.**
