# Preface
We had a support ticket backlog of 1500 tickets spanning at least 5 years.

2 volunteers stepped forward to take care of all of them. The manager refused to give it to them... At first...

# What happened
We had a staffing issue. The old guard, hated doing support tickets, so they had a support rotation. The problem. We had a 1500 ticket backlog that grew by at least 30 tickets per week.

They were tired, they were busy, they had other priorities that they were getting performance reviewed on. Support tickets was just not a priority.

2 new hires spotted that. "We can take care of them."
Manager, "No no. It's too much. I don't want to burn you out."

## Diagnosis

**Headline finding: energy, not virtue. The root variable is depletion — tired, overworked people cut corners, and the corner they cut was support.**

The old guard did not fail from laziness or malice. They were **depleted** — "tired, busy, other priorities they were getting performance reviewed on." Depleted people triage by dropping the lowest-status, least-rewarded thing, and that was support. The 1500-ticket backlog is what *corner-cutting under depletion* accretes to over five years. This is the variable that sits **underneath** the two mechanisms below — both the wrong model and the incentive structure bite *because* tired people have no slack to resist them.

**Sub-finding A — the brake was latent, and the org suppressed it.** Sometimes the brake is *present* — two people who *want to do the work* — and the org **actively suppresses it.** "No no, it's too much. I don't want to burn you out" is the suppressor: dysfunction **wearing care as a costume.** A protective-sounding veto blocked the exact correction the team already contained. (Note *why* the veto felt reasonable: in a depleted org, "this will burn you out" is the default prior — because it had burned out everyone so far. The suppression is itself a *symptom* of the energy problem.)

**Sub-finding B — the deprioritization is Axis 9 in reverse.** The incentive framework made it rational to let support rot: nobody was performance-reviewed on it, so nobody was on the hook for 1500 tickets, so 1500 tickets is what accrued. The culpability machine ([[The Report Due Friday End of Day]], Axis 9) usually *over*-attributes failure to individuals; here it attributes *no* consequence to the backlog. **Depleted people obey incentives more rigidly** — they have no surplus energy to do the un-rewarded right thing — so depletion and incentive-deprioritization compound.

So the founding condition is **Assumption × (unowned)** — support is low-status overflow, safe to defer — but the *engine* under it is energy. The assumption survived because no one had the energy to question it; the incentive bit because no one had the energy to override it.

# The insistence
3 months later, the backlog was not decreasing even with the new people. I noticed something. Every time they were on support they were doing the extra tickets that the old guard was letting accumulate.

Me to the Manager, "Just let them do it. They are already faster than all of us combined."

Manager, "OK. But I don't want them to burn out."

"I get the sense, this is going to disappear in a month."

## Diagnosis

**Cell: the intervention installed nothing — it deleted an approval gate. And the forecast is Axis 10 run forward.**

What I actually did was **subtract**, not add. "Just let them do it" installed no process, no tool, no policy. It **removed the manager's veto** — the suppressor from the prior section. This is the literal meaning of the title: *getting out of the way* is **un-suppressing a latent brake**, not building one. The capability was already in the room (the two hires were quietly clearing the old guard's overflow every rotation, "faster than all of us combined"); the only thing missing was *permission*.

**The forecast — "this is going to disappear in a month" — is the inverse of the Measurement Paradox.** In the negative anecdotes leadership *refused* the math ([[We are not going to make it]]: "we'll make it, work harder"; [[Getting a bus thrown at you]]: six-month belief after eighteen months). Here the same instrument — checking throughput against the work — is **trusted instead of refused.** I ran the numbers on the volunteers' actual pace and believed what they said. The lesson is that the measuring instrument is identical on both sides of the corpus; what differs is whether the person holding it **acts on the reading or defends against it.** Dysfunction is not a lack of measurement. It is the refusal of a measurement that is right there.

**The faster-than-all-of-us detail is also an Axis 5 marker.** The brake (the capability to clear the backlog) lived entirely in two individuals — not in any process. Hold that; the next section is where it matters.

# Their insight
The new hires, "Ya the problem is, most of these tickets they'll never do or the people who are asking don't realize we can't do things for them. We just called them up and explained what to do. We were able to cancel most of the 1500 and then renegotiate what was left."

Apparently this was a novel way to approach support tickets at the organization.

## Diagnosis

**Cell: Assumption × Unreasonable — the backlog was an artifact of a wrong model, not a real quantity of work. This fills the Assumption row the negative anecdotes couldn't reach.**

**The 1500 tickets were never the work.** The old guard treated the backlog as a *queue to grind through* — and under that model it genuinely was unreasonable (1500 × grind-time = impossible, the same shape as [[We are not going to make it]]). But the model was wrong. The two hires saw the pile was not work at all — it was **miscommunication, invalid requests, and unset expectations.** The real operation was *conversation*: call the requester, explain what support can and can't do, cancel what was never valid, renegotiate the rest. The backlog dissolved because **most of it was never a queue.**

**The reframe fell out of the energy — that is the link to the headline.** The new hires did not have a secret method the old guard lacked. They had **the right energy, sustained**, and the method *emerged from* having enough energy to actually look at the tickets instead of grinding past them. A depleted person cannot see the reframe, because seeing it requires the slack to stop, question the model, and pick up the phone — slack depletion has already spent. So the wrong model (sub-finding above) and the energy (the headline) are the same finding from two angles: **depletion keeps the wrong model invisible; energy is what makes the model questionable.** The old guard wasn't less clever; they were more tired.

This is the positive mirror of the corpus's central negative finding. In the negatives, a wrong tacit assumption made a tractable thing look mandatory **and** impossible. Here, *dropping* a wrong tacit assumption made an "impossible" thing **dissolve.** Same mechanism, opposite sign. And the assumption was perfectly invisible: *"this was a novel way to approach support tickets at the organization."* **Five years, and nobody questioned that a ticket equals work to be done** — the assumption was so tacit it never registered *as* an assumption. It took two people who hadn't yet absorbed the org's model to see it from outside. (This is why the Assumption realm is the most dangerous on the grid: it does its damage *invisibly*, never surfacing to be argued with.)

**And here is the sting — it reverted.** This is a *positive* anecdote that, examined honestly, **does not refute** [[project-brake-location-pattern]]. The brake was real, but it was **personal**, not institutional: it lived in two individuals and in one removed veto. When the un-suppressing force and the two brake-carriers were gone, support snapped back to grind-the-queue. The reframe — "call them and renegotiate" — never became *how the org did support*; it was a capability briefly un-suppressed, not a process installed.

So the counter-test returns its verdict, and it is unkind to the optimistic reading: **even the success story confirms the bleak finding.** The bubble of effective work was real and it was good — and it was load-bearing on specific people. The org did not learn the lesson; it learned nothing, because nothing in the *process* changed. The brake-location pattern survives its own counter-test: the brake was in the people, and when the people left, the brake left with them. What this anecdote adds is *why the bubble forms at all* (suppression can be lifted) and *why it doesn't last* (lifting suppression is not the same as installing a brake).

**And the energy reading explains the reversion precisely.** The brake was not just personal — it was *energy*-borne. The two hires sustained the right energy "for as long as they were there," and the backlog stayed clear for exactly that long. Energy is the least transferable thing in an organization: it cannot be documented, delegated, or installed into a process. So a bubble built on sustained energy reverts the moment the energy leaves the building — which is the most personal, least institutional brake there is. This is the bridge to the **candidate "energy" variable** ([[project-energy-variable]]): right energy is *necessary* for the bubble, but energy alone installs nothing that outlives the people carrying it. (The disconfirming partner — *right energy, **wrong** skill set* — is the next specimen, and it shows energy is not even sufficient: misdirected energy makes things worse.)
