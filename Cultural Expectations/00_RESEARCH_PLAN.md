## The Central Claim Being Tested

`R3_STEELMAN` retired "irrational" by locating it in the *role design* — and correctly found that an under-resourced-but-affordable composite can be a rational economic bet. T2 and T4 propose that "irrational" survives, but relocated to a *different locus*: the **cultural expectation** held by the people who judge the CTO.

Three candidate loci, nested:
1. **The role design** → retired by R3. May be a rational economic equilibrium.
2. **The expectation** (T2) → judged against a standard no one would endorse on reflection.
3. **The cultural permission structure** (T4) → why does the culture grant "smart → learn anything" for technical roles but not doctors, lawyers, accountants? The axes map this.

The research plan tests all three loci — not to assert all three, but to find which ones the evidence supports and which require retreat.

**The clean separation this plan must maintain:** this cannot become a backdoor to re-assert "the role is irrational" after R3 retired it. Every finding must be attributable to the *expectation/attribution* locus, cleanly separated from the role-design claim R3 conceded. If a finding implicates role design, flag it — don't smuggle it through as an expectation finding.

## Track 1: The Psychological Mechanism
### *Why the reflection gap exists and why it is stable*

**The core question:** Why do people apply a standard they wouldn't endorse on reflection? This is not a paradox — it is a documented feature of human cognition under authority and social pressure. WWII created the urgency that drove fifty years of research on exactly this.

**The research backbone — in causal/historical order:**

### 1.1 — Authority compliance and the behavior/endorsement dissociation

**Milgram (1961–1963)**
The foundational finding: ordinary people comply with authority-figure demands even when they privately disagree, even when the demand causes visible harm. The mechanism isn't agreement — it's role execution. Participants who administered what they believed were dangerous shocks didn't endorse the action; they deferred to the role structure.

*T2 application:* The reflection gap T2 names — "judged against a standard no one would endorse out loud" — is documented at scale in Milgram. The CEO applying the composite CTO standard doesn't need to endorse it privately; they need only be executing the role of "person who hires a CTO," in a room where the composite is the socially-present standard. Milgram shows this dissociation is the norm, not the exception.

*Research to find:* Milgram's original papers + the replication literature (Burger 2009, Dolinski 2017 — partial replications showing the effect holds). Also the critique literature — what limits the effect? (Proximity to victim, presence of dissenting peers.) The limits are as important as the finding for T2.

**Hannah Arendt — Eichmann in Jerusalem (1963) / "Banality of Evil"**
Not experimental but the conceptual frame that reoriented the field. Eichmann wasn't a monster — he was a bureaucrat executing a role. The demand propagates through role execution without requiring individual malice or even individual endorsement. "I was doing my job" is not a rationalization after the fact — it is the actual cognitive mode during the act.

*T2 application:* The CEO writing the impossible CTO JD isn't malicious and may not even be reasoning. They are executing the role of "person who fills this seat," using the template that exists for that role. The composite propagates through role execution. This is Arendt's banality at organizational scale — not evil, but thoughtless in the precise sense she meant.

### 1.2 — Conformity and socially-installed expectations

**Asch (1951–1956)**
Conformity under social pressure: people give answers they privately know are wrong because the group gives those answers first. The effect is strongest when the group is unanimous and the individual has no ally. It disappears almost entirely when one other person dissents.

*T2 application:* The composite CTO expectation may not be generated by any individual reasoning from first principles. It may be socially reproduced — every CEO expects it because every other CEO expects it, and the investor in the room expects it, and the recruiter's template encodes it. No individual would generate the composite independently; the conformity pressure makes it self-reinforcing. The reflection gap exists because the expectation is *socially installed*, not *individually reasoned* — and when you ask someone directly, you briefly remove the social pressure, and the privately-held answer differs.

*Research to find:* Asch's original line experiments + the organizational conformity literature that followed. Particularly: conformity in expert groups (does domain expertise reduce conformity pressure, or shift it?). Also: the "one dissenter" finding — what would it take for a single credible voice in the hiring room to break the composite expectation?

### 1.3 — The authoritarian personality and downward demand structures

**Adorno, Frenkel-Brunswik, Levinson, Sanford — The Authoritarian Personality (1950)**
The Frankfurt School's empirical response to Nazism: certain personality structures are predisposed to defer upward to authority and demand compliance downward, with reduced tolerance for ambiguity and strong in-group/out-group policing.

*T2 application:* Less directly applicable to the composite expectation, but relevant to *who maintains it under pressure*. The reflection gap may close differently depending on personality structure — some decision-makers, when asked directly, will revise their expectation; others will defend it as the correct standard. The authoritarian personality literature predicts which. Also relevant: the composite CTO demand may be more aggressively maintained by decision-makers with lower tolerance for ambiguity — "I need one person who can do everything" as a defense against the discomfort of acknowledging structural complexity.

### 1.4 — The "great man" theory of leadership and its organizational institutionalization

**Carlyle (1840) → Weber (charismatic authority) → Burns (1978) → Bass (transformational leadership)**
The "great man" theory predates WWII but was reinforced by it — Churchill, Roosevelt, and Hitler as apparent proof that individual leaders determine historical outcomes. This became the dominant model in organizational leadership theory, institutionalized through Burns' *Leadership* (1978) and Bass's transformational leadership model, which installed the expectation that exceptional leaders transcend structural limits.

*T2 application:* This is the organizational-theory version of Axis 0 — the prior, installed in management education and leadership literature, that one exceptional person can and should carry the composite. The CEO who expects the CTO to be simultaneously technical visionary, people leader, and strategic executive isn't making an unusual demand by the standards of their MBA curriculum. Transformational leadership theory *normalized* the composite as the expected form of executive excellence.

*Research to find:* Burns (1978) and Bass's model for the institutionalization claim. Also: the critique literature — does the transformational leadership model predict executive failure at role transitions? Has anyone studied whether the "transformational" expectation maps onto the composite CTO demand?

### 1.5 — Cognitive dissonance and post-hoc rationalization

**Festinger (1957)**
When behavior and belief conflict, humans resolve the dissonance by updating the belief to match the behavior — not the other way around. The person who applies a standard they don't privately endorse will, after repeated application, update their private belief to endorse the standard.

*T2 application:* The reflection gap may close over time in the wrong direction. A CEO who repeatedly applies the composite CTO standard — and watches CTOs fail to meet it — doesn't necessarily conclude "the standard is wrong." They conclude "we keep hiring the wrong CTO." The dissonance is resolved by strengthening the standard, not by questioning it. This is why the gap between applied standard and endorsed standard may be widest for *new* decision-makers and narrowest for experienced ones — but not because the experienced ones have better judgment. Because they've resolved the dissonance.

*Research to find:* Festinger's original cognitive dissonance literature + the organizational application (how do executives rationalize repeated hiring failures in the same role?).

## Track 2: The Empirical Expectation Audit
### *What the expectation actually is, in its documented artifacts*

**The core question:** What do CEOs, boards, investors, and recruiters *say* they expect of a CTO — and does it implicitly demand all three modes simultaneously? The JD is the primary artifact. Post-mortems, diligence frameworks, and board-review criteria are secondary artifacts.

### 2.1 — The JD as the expectation's primary artifact

The job description is the most legible encoding of what is expected. It is written before the hire, reflects what the organization believes it needs, and is not filtered by post-hoc rationalization. It is the expectation in its most unguarded form.

**What to look for:**
- Does the CTO JD simultaneously demand hands-on technical depth ("you will architect systems," "you will write code"), people leadership ("you will build and mentor the engineering team"), and strategic scope ("you will set technical vision," "you will work with the board")?
- Does it demand all three at seed/Series A stage — before the company is large enough to have separated any of these?
- Does the JD language change across company stage? (Seed vs. Series B vs. public) — if the composite is demanded at seed but distributed at scale, that confirms the capacity ceiling claim.
- Does the JD carry timeline pressure that makes the composite explicitly sequential-but-simultaneous? ("In your first 30 days... first 90 days... first year...")

**Comparison artifact:** Run the same analysis on CFO, CMO, and CRO JDs at equivalent company stages. Does the CFO JD demand hands-on bookkeeping *and* capital markets strategy *and* people leadership simultaneously? If not, the CTO JD is an outlier — and the outlier is documentable.

**Research method:** JD archaeology — pull CTO JDs across company stage (seed, Series A, B, C, public) and across decade (1990s, 2000s, 2010s, 2020s). Look for the composite demand and its evolution. This is direct evidence, not inference.

### 2.2 — "Why we let our CTO go" post-mortems

The post-mortem is the expectation revealed under failure. When a CTO is replaced, the stated reason is the gap between what was expected and what was delivered — which surfaces the expectation more clearly than the JD, because the JD may be aspirational while the post-mortem is specific.

**What to look for:**
- Do the stated reasons cluster around mode-transition failure? ("Couldn't scale," "lost the technical thread," "not strategic enough," "great builder but couldn't lead a team")
- Are the reasons framed as individual failure ("he wasn't the right person") or structural failure ("we needed a different kind of leader for this stage")?
- Is there evidence of the misattribution pattern — structural difficulty read as personal inadequacy?
- Do the post-mortems ever question the expectation itself, or only the individual's fit with it?

**Sources:** Practitioner accounts (First Round Review, a16z content, Lenny Rachitsky interviews, CTO Craft community), founder retrospectives, and any academic or journalistic post-mortems on startup leadership transitions.

**Discipline:** Post-mortems are self-report and retrospective — subject to rationalization (Festinger, Track 1.5). Weight them as evidence of *what the expectation feels like from inside the failure*, not as objective accounts of what happened. The pattern across many accounts is more load-bearing than any single account.

### 2.3 — Board and investor diligence criteria

The board and investor evaluation framework is the expectation in its most consequential form — the standard applied when the CTO's continued employment is formally reviewed.

**What to look for:**
- What questions do investors ask about CTOs in diligence? Do they implicitly demand the composite? ("Is he/she technical enough?" and "Can they build a team?" and "Do they have the strategic vision?" asked as a unified checklist — not as alternatives.)
- What metrics do boards use to evaluate CTO performance? Are they mode-specific (technical output, team health, strategic positioning) or composite (the CTO is "working" or "not working")?
- Is there evidence of board-level evaluation frameworks that separate the modes? If not, the absence is itself evidence of the composite expectation.

**Sources:** VC firm published diligence frameworks, board governance literature, investor blog posts on "what we look for in a technical co-founder," executive assessment firm rubrics.

### 2.4 — The reflection-gap test

This is the direct test T2 proposes: ask decision-makers plainly whether they expect one person to transition all three modes, and observe whether the answer differs from what their JDs and post-mortems reveal.

**The diagnostic question:** *"Do you actually expect a CTO to be an excellent hands-on architect, an excellent engineering people leader, and an excellent board-level strategist — simultaneously, across the company's growth from seed to scale?"*

**What the answer reveals:**
- **"No, of course not"** → the standard applied at evaluation is one nobody would defend on reflection. The gap between applied standard and endorsed standard is the irrationality's location.
- **"Yes, we do expect it"** → the expectation is sincerely held *and* (per §1–§4 of `DRAFT_SYNTHESIS`) exceeds one person's reach. Irrationality in the believer.
- **"It depends on stage"** → the composite expectation is present but stage-modulated. This is the most sophisticated answer and the most interesting — it may reveal that the expectation *is* the composite but that decision-makers have an implicit (and usually unspoken) theory of when to swap out the CTO.

**Research to find:** Direct evidence of the reflection gap in practitioner accounts is rare by definition — it requires someone to have asked the question and recorded the answer. May need to be constructed from adjacent evidence: accounts of founders who *did* separate the roles, and how they described the decision. The gap may be most visible in the contrast between what founders say they expected before the failure and what they say they should have expected after it.

## Track 3: The Axes in the Wild
### *Finding each axis operating in documented cases*

**The core question:** The ten axes in `00_AXES_SUMMARY.md` are hypotheses. Track 3 looks for cases where each axis is visibly operating — in documented organizational failures, sector comparisons, or historical events. This is not a separate research question; it is the evidentiary pass that turns the axes from hypotheses into findings.

This track is inherently open-ended — it expands as evidence is found. What follows is the initial map of where to look for each axis.

### 3.0 — Axis 0 (Fictional Media) in the wild
**Where to look:** Media studies literature on occupational portrayals and their effect on public expectations. The "CSI effect" (jury expectations shaped by forensic TV drama) is the closest documented parallel — juries expect forensic evidence that doesn't exist because TV normalized it. Is there a "CTO effect" equivalent? Also: the evolution of the "hacker" archetype across decades of film and television as a proxy for how technical competence is publicly modeled.

### 3.1 — Axis 1 (Tangibility) in the wild
**Where to look:** Decision-making research on how non-experts evaluate expert domains. Does cognitive invisibility predict where composite demands appear? Compare: domains where the work is physically observable (construction, surgery) vs. cognitively invisible (software, finance strategy). Is composite demand more common in the latter?

### 3.2 — Axis 2 (Institutional Memory) in the wild
**Where to look:** The 2008 financial crisis as a documented case of institutional memory deficit in a fast-growing subspecialty (structured finance). NASA Challenger and Columbia as cases where the brake was degraded and then reinstalled by visible failure. The CHAOS Report (1994–present) as thirty years of documented software project failure that hasn't generated the institutional brake — why not?

### 3.3 — Axis 3 (Structural Obscuring) in the wild
**Where to look:** Fee disclosure literature in financial services — the regulatory history of forcing legibility (Dodd-Frank, fiduciary rule) as evidence that the obscuring was structural and required external force to overcome. Is there an analogous case in software where organizational incentives kept complexity obscured?

### 3.4 — Axis 4 (Institutional Mediation) in the wild
**Where to look:** Fiduciary standard history — when was it established, what failure triggered it, what changed when it was imposed? The absence of any equivalent in software leadership is the finding, but the positive case (where mediation exists and demonstrably works) is what makes the absence legible.

### 3.5 — Axis 5 (Internal/External Brake) in the wild
**Where to look:** Science communication literature — the gap between scientific community norms and public consumption. The NDT critique as a documented case of internal brake not propagating externally. Engineering professional societies (IEEE, ACM) — do they have scope-policing functions? If so, do they apply to organizational demands on engineers?

### 3.6 — Axis 6 (Feedback Loop) in the wild
**Where to look:** The "two cultures" literature (C.P. Snow, 1959) — the legibility gap between scientific and humanistic communities as a documented structural phenomenon. More directly: research on how technical expertise is communicated to non-technical decision-makers, and where that communication breaks down. The JD itself as an artifact of the broken loop — written by non-technical HR or founders, encoding an expectation the technical community would challenge if it could reach the decision point.

### 3.7 — Axis 7 (Halo Effect) in the wild
**Where to look:** The Halo Effect business literature (Phil Rosenzweig, 2007) — specifically the case studies of companies where one executive's charisma or past success haloed into assumed competence across domains. Also: the Jobs mythology as a documented case where the composite genius-leader expectation was installed at cultural scale by one highly visible example.

### 3.8 — Axis 8 (Credential-Capability Conflation) in the wild
**Where to look:** Title inflation literature — the proliferation of "Chief" titles and what they signal vs. what they bound. The history of the CTO title itself — when it emerged, what it originally described, how the description drifted. Also: the MBA as a case of credential-as-capability-signal in management — does the MBA certify scope or signal general management aptitude?

### 3.9 — Axis 9 (Liability) in the wild
**Where to look:** Aviation liability history — how the Airline Deregulation Act (1978) interacted with safety liability, and what the crash record looks like before and after liability frameworks were strengthened. Medical malpractice history as a case where liability installation forced scope-bounding. The *absence* of software engineering liability as the finding — but what attempts have been made to introduce it (software liability legislation, PE licensure for software engineers) and why have they failed?

### 3.10 — Axis 10 (Measurement Paradox) in the wild

> **⚠ Priority flag — run early, paired with 3.2.** This section is the highest-stakes single finding in the plan. The Axis 2 / Axis 10 distinction — and therefore the "civilizational akrasia" capstone — holds or collapses here. If the citation trail is broken (independent rediscovery each generation rather than knowing-and-ignoring), Axis 10 collapses into Axis 2 (memory deficit, not akrasia), and the capstone claim must be revised. Run 3.2 and 3.10 as a pair before the capstone language is used in any synthesis.

**Where to look:** The full measurement timeline as a continuous tradition — Taylor through CHAOS Report. Specifically: is there evidence that each generation of management science *knew* about the prior generation's findings and proceeded anyway? Brooks cites prior software failures; the CHAOS Report cites Brooks; the IC→EM reversion literature cites the CHAOS Report. If the citation trail exists and the practice didn't change, that is the measurement paradox documented.

## Track 4: The Pre-Software History of the Composite Demand
### *The bureaucrat as prototype — smart people have always been expected to do more than their share*

**The core question:** The composite demand on cognitive workers did not originate with software. It has been applied to educated generalists since organizations existed. Track 4 traces that history — from the Weberian bureaucrat through Taylorism through the knowledge worker — to show that software engineers inherited a cultural template rather than generating a new problem.

**Why this track matters for the overall thesis:**
If the composite demand is a century-old pattern applied to every generation of smart cognitive workers, two things follow. First, the licensed professions (medicine, law, accounting) are the *exceptions* — fields that successfully argued their way out of the generalist-bureaucrat template. Second, the measurement tradition (Axis 10) has been running the entire time — which means the akrasia isn't new, isn't unique to software, and isn't going to be fixed by awareness alone. Software is the current generation's most compressed instance of a failure mode that Taylor was already measuring in 1911.

### 4.1 — Weber and the theoretical foundation of the generalist bureaucrat

**Max Weber (1922) — *Economy and Society***
Weber formalized the bureaucracy as the rational organizational form. The ideal bureaucrat in Weber's model is defined by *office*, not by person — they carry the role, not the expertise. Roles are filled by qualified persons, but the qualification is *general education*, not domain mastery. A sufficiently educated person fills any bureaucratic role because the role is defined by process and hierarchy, not by craft.

*Track 4 application:* This is the theoretical origin of the "smart people can do any cognitive task" assumption. Weber's bureaucrat is the cultural prior that every subsequent generation of knowledge worker inherited. The assumption isn't that bureaucrats are fungible in the Taylorist sense (interchangeable units of task execution) — it's that *educated people are fungible across roles* because intelligence is the relevant qualification, not domain expertise. This is the distinction that software inherited and medicine escaped.

*Research to find:* Weber's original text on bureaucracy and the *Beamter* (official) ideal. Also: the civil service history — when did civil service examinations emerge, what did they test, and what does that reveal about the assumed relationship between general intelligence and administrative competence? The British civil service (Northcote-Trevelyan Report, 1854) is a documented case of the generalist-over-specialist decision being made explicitly.

### 4.2 — Taylor and the bifurcation that didn't reach management

**Frederick Taylor (1911) — *The Principles of Scientific Management***
Taylor's model bifurcated the organization into workers (specialists, Taylorized, fungible within task) and managers (generalists, coordinators, the intelligence layer above the workers). The manager doesn't specialize — the manager *coordinates*. The composite demand on managers was not only left untouched by Taylorism — it was *amplified*. The manager's job is to understand all the tasks being coordinated, which requires a composite competence the workers are explicitly not expected to have.

*Track 4 application:* Taylor solved the fungibility problem for workers by making them genuinely fungible through task decomposition. He did not solve it for managers — he created a class whose value proposition was the composite. The CTO inherits the manager role in Taylor's model: the person who is supposed to understand all the technical work being done below them, plus the organizational work of running the team, plus the strategic work of setting direction. Taylor made the worker's composite demand manageable; he amplified the manager's.

*Research to find:* Taylor's original text. Also: the Gilbreth time-motion studies as the measurement tradition Taylor launched. And: Drucker's explicit critique of Taylorism applied to knowledge workers — where exactly did Drucker say the Taylor model breaks, and does his diagnosis match Axis 10?

### 4.3 — The Hawthorne Studies and the human variable that didn't change the demand

**Elton Mayo et al. (1924–1932) — Western Electric Hawthorne Works**
The Hawthorne Studies are the first serious empirical study of knowledge workers in organizational settings. The finding — that productivity was affected by social and psychological factors, not just task design — named the human variable in organizational performance. The "Hawthorne effect" (workers perform better when observed) became canonical.

What the Hawthorne Studies did *not* find, and did not look for: that cognitive tasks were non-fungible. The studies found that *motivation* was variable, not that *capacity* was bounded in domain-specific ways. The fungibility assumption survived the Hawthorne Studies intact, because the studies were designed to measure output variation, not capacity ceilings.

*Track 4 application:* The Hawthorne Studies are the missed opportunity in the measurement timeline. The right question — "is there a ceiling on what one person can be expected to learn and do in an organizational role?" — was not asked. The question asked was "what affects output within an existing role?" The composite demand was the independent variable that was never tested.

*Research to find:* The original Hawthorne Studies reports and the critique literature (particularly: was the Hawthorne effect real, or was it a methodological artifact?). Also: what happened to the Hawthorne findings in management practice — were they absorbed, and if so, what changed?

### 4.4 — Drucker and the knowledge worker who still got Taylorized

**Peter Drucker (1954) — *The Practice of Management*; (1959) — *Landmarks of Tomorrow***
Drucker named the knowledge worker as a distinct category and explicitly argued that knowledge work cannot be managed the way factory work is managed. Knowledge workers own their means of production (their minds). They cannot be supervised the way a machinist can. Their output is not countable the way units are. Managing them requires treating them as assets, not costs.

Drucker's diagnosis is precise and largely correct. It was widely cited, taught in business schools, and absorbed into management vocabulary. It did not change management practice at scale — knowledge workers continued to be managed as resources, measured by proxies (lines of code, story points, tickets closed), and expected to reconfigure themselves across role transitions without support.

*Track 4 application:* Drucker is the clearest case of Axis 10 in action before software was large enough to generate its own measurement tradition. The knowledge worker was named, the composite demand on them was identified as problematic, the finding was canonical — and the demand persisted. Drucker is Brooks' predecessor in the measurement tradition, a generation earlier, in the broader knowledge-worker population.

*Research to find:* Drucker's original knowledge worker texts. Also: is there evidence that Drucker's findings changed any specific management practices, or only management vocabulary? The distinction matters — if the vocabulary changed but the practice didn't, that is Axis 10 documented in the Drucker era.

### 4.5 — The computer arrives into the bureaucrat template

**The 1950s–1960s transition: programmer as machine operator → programmer as knowledge worker**

When computers arrived in organizations in the 1950s and 1960s, they entered as tools for the bureaucrat — accounting machines, payroll machines, census machines. The people who operated them were initially closer to the clerk than to the engineer. The early "computer" was a job title held by people performing arithmetic; the machine replaced the human computer.

For a brief window — roughly the 1950s through the mid-1960s — there was an implicit recognition that programming was a craft requiring specific cognitive modes. The first programmers were often mathematicians and logicians, selected for precision and systematic thinking. Grace Hopper, the ENIAC programmers — selected, not assumed.

Then the field scaled. Organizations needed more programmers. The selection discipline was replaced by the bureaucrat assumption: *any sufficiently intelligent person can learn to program*. The craft that had been recognized as specific became invisible against the generalist template. The composite demand migrated: "any bureaucrat can do any administrative task" → "any smart person can learn to program" → "any programmer can do any programming task." Three transfers, each one losing a constraint that should have survived.

Brooks wrote *The Mythical Man-Month* exactly at the moment when the consequences of the third transfer were becoming visible at scale.

*Research to find:* The history of programmer selection and training in the 1950s–1960s — particularly any evidence that early computing organizations recognized programming as a distinct cognitive capability before the scaling pressure removed that recognition. Also: the history of the "programmer shortage" narrative and how it drove the shift from selection to assumption.

### 4.6 — Lean Six Sigma and the measurement that extracted the wrong lesson

**Ohno / Shingo (1950s–1970s) → Lean Six Sigma (1980s–present)**

The Toyota Production System (TPS) is one of the few management frameworks that actually *challenges* the fungibility assumption at the operational level. Cross-training, kaizen, the andon cord, the emphasis on contextual knowledge as quality mechanism — TPS assumes that workers who understand the whole process produce better outcomes than workers who only know their station. The TPS insight is that *context is non-fungible*: you cannot replace a worker who understands the process with one who doesn't and expect quality to hold.

When TPS was exported to the West and became Lean Six Sigma, it was re-Taylorized. The statistical tools (Six Sigma, DMAIC, control charts) were adopted. The contextual-knowledge insight was largely lost. The measurement framework survived; the finding that context is non-fungible did not.

This is Axis 10 in miniature: the measurement found the right thing, the organization extracted the wrong lesson, and the composite demand persisted.

*Research to find:* The history of Lean's adoption in Western organizations and what specifically was lost in translation. Also: is there evidence from Japanese manufacturing that the TPS contextual-knowledge insight *did* produce measurably different outcomes — which would make the Western re-Taylorization a documented case of the measurement paradox?

### 4.7 — Historical occupational transitions: when known professions successfully separated the composite

**The core question for this section:** If we can find documented moments where a known occupation *successfully separated* the composite demand into distinct specializations, we can reverse-engineer what made the transition possible — and use those cases as the comparison baseline for what it would take for software to make the same transition.

**The pattern across all documented transitions:**
Every profession that successfully specialized followed the same sequence:
1. Complexity grew beyond what the generalist template could hold
2. Failures became *visibly and individually attributable* — the bridge fell, the patient died, the accounts were fraudulent
3. The profession argued for specialization *after* the failures made the composite demand undeniable
4. Credentialing and liability frameworks followed — encoding the lesson the failures taught

The trigger is never the profession deciding to specialize. It is always the complexity becoming *undeniably visible through attributed failure*.

**Medicine — the country doctor to the specialist (late 19th–early 20th century)**
The country doctor was a genuine composite: surgeon, pharmacist, psychiatrist, obstetrician, pediatrician. Germ theory (Pasteur, Koch, 1860s–1880s) made domains of expertise distinct enough that no one could claim competence across all of them. The knowledge became too complex for the generalist template to hold, *and the failures were visible* — patients died of infections that a specialist would have recognized. The AMA and specialty boards followed the knowledge; they didn't lead it. The tipping moment: germ theory made ignorance in one domain lethal and attributable to the generalist who claimed competence in it.

*Axes fed:* 1 (complexity became tangible through visible death), 2 (institutional memory formed from attributed failure), 9 (liability followed specialization, reinforcing it), 10 (the measurement — patient mortality — forced the transition).

> **⚠ Semmelweis callout — the pre-transition case that proves the trigger requirement.**
> Ignaz Semmelweis (Vienna General Hospital, 1847) demonstrated that puerperal fever mortality dropped from ~10% to ~1% when physicians washed hands between autopsies and deliveries. The data was clean, the intervention was documented, the result was unambiguous. The medical establishment rejected it. Semmelweis died in an asylum in 1865, unrecognized. Germ theory (Pasteur, Koch, 1860s–1880s) vindicated him a decade later — and *that* is what triggered specialization, not his data.
>
> **Why this matters for the pattern:** Semmelweis is the proof that *data alone is not the trigger*. The measurement existed. The intervention worked. The field had the evidence and proceeded as if it didn't. What was missing: the *mechanism* was not yet legible (germ theory didn't exist yet), so the finding could not be attributed to a specific causal chain that implicated the generalist physician's behavior. Once germ theory made the mechanism visible, ignorance in one domain became *attributable* — and the transition followed.
>
> **Gradient note (from 00_TAXONOMY.md):** The expectation that physicians could move from autopsy to delivery without handwashing is Logically Irrational by the irrational/unreasonable gradient — it violated the causal mechanism even before anyone had named that mechanism. The irrationality preceded the knowledge that made it legible. This is T4's cultural permission structure operating in a pre-scientific context: "smart physician can do all of medicine" was the prior, and it cost lives for two decades before germ theory made the cost attributable.
>
> **Axis 10 connection:** Semmelweis is the clearest pre-software case of the measurement paradox. The data existed, was published, was known — and was ignored. Not forgotten (Axis 2) — ignored (Axis 10). The field had the finding and continued without it. The 3.10 citation trail question applies here too: did the post-germ-theory medical establishment cite Semmelweis as the vindicated prior, or did it treat germ theory as a new discovery? The answer to that question is the same Axis 2 / Axis 10 boundary test, one generation earlier.

> *Primary sources: Semmelweis, I. (1861). Die Ätiologie, der Begriff und die Prophylaxis des Kindbettfiebers. — the primary text. Secondary: Nuland, S. (2003). The Doctors' Plague: Germs, Childbed Fever, and the Strange Story of Ignaz Semmelweis. Norton.*

**Law — the country solicitor to specialist practice (19th–20th century)**
The country solicitor handled wills, property disputes, criminal defense, and commercial contracts. Modern law firms are organized by practice area — M&A, IP, tax, litigation, employment. The split happened as the body of law grew large enough that no one could hold it, and court losses attributable to ignorance of specific law became career-ending. The complexity led; the structure followed.

*Axes fed:* 2 (institutional memory — bar exam history, case law volume), 9 (liability — malpractice for practicing outside competence), 8 (credential became a scope label, not just a capability signal).

**Accounting — the bookkeeper to the CPA (early 20th century)**
The original bookkeeper was a composite: record-keeper, tax filer, financial advisor, auditor. The CPA credential emerged in the United States in 1896, driven by the complexity of corporate accounting following industrialization. Corporate fraud (visible, attributable, financially catastrophic) was the trigger. Sarbanes-Oxley (2002) is a second-order specialization — the field specializing within an already-specialized domain because the complexity kept growing and the failures (Enron, WorldCom) were again visible and attributable.

*Axes fed:* 9 (liability — auditor liability drove the credential), 2 (Enron as the Tay Bridge of accounting — the catastrophic visible failure that forced the next round of specialization), 10 (the measurement tradition in accounting is the audit itself — failure to measure correctly is the attributable harm).

**Engineering — the natural philosopher to the licensed PE (19th century)**
The original engineer was a natural philosopher who happened to build things. The PE licensure emerged after catastrophic visible failures: the Tay Bridge collapse (1879, 75 deaths), boiler explosions throughout the industrial revolution, bridge failures across the United States. The complexity became *visibly dangerous* before the brake was installed. The ASCE (1852) and PE licensure followed the failures; the failures preceded the professionalization.

*Axes fed:* 1 (tangible failure — bridges fall, boilers explode — made complexity physically visible), 9 (liability — the engineer who stamps the drawing bears personal legal exposure), 2 (the institutional memory is literally built from failure post-mortems encoded in building codes).

**Aviation — from barnstormer to licensed pilot (early 20th century)**
Early aviation had no licensing, no certification, no scope limits. The barnstormer era produced visible, frequent, attributable deaths. The Air Commerce Act (1926) followed the failure record — the federal government intervening because the market wasn't installing the brake fast enough. The ATP certificate, type ratings, and crew resource management frameworks are all downstream of that initial intervention, which was itself downstream of visible attributed failure.

*Axes fed:* 1 (crashes are maximally tangible), 9 (federal liability structure), 2 (the entire aviation safety system is institutionalized failure memory — every regulation traces to a specific accident), 4 (the FAA as the institutional mediating body that exists precisely because individual demand was killing people).

**The software non-transition — why the pattern hasn't fired:**
Software is at step 1 of the pattern — the complexity has long outgrown the generalist template. But step 2 is missing: the failures are not visibly and individually attributable. The project fails slowly. The team decays invisibly. The CTO is replaced with a "not scaling" explanation that attributes the failure to the person, not the demand. Without visible attribution, the profession cannot make the argument that forced specialization in every other field.

This is Axes 1, 6, and 9 interacting simultaneously: the failures are cognitively invisible (Axis 1), attribution can't cross the legibility gap (Axis 6), and no liability framework forces attribution (Axis 9). Every field that successfully specialized had at least one of these fail — software has all three intact, which is why the composite demand persists despite the complexity having long outgrown the generalist template.

**The research question this generates:** Can we find the specific failure or complexity threshold — the Tay Bridge moment — in each profession that tipped the transition? If those moments are documentable, they become the comparison baseline. And they sharpen the prediction: software's Tay Bridge equivalent would need to be a failure that is simultaneously large, visible, attributable to a specific person's composite demand, and legally actionable. The question is whether that failure is possible given software's invisible failure modes — or whether the transition requires a different trigger entirely.

*Research to find:* The specific triggering failures for each profession above — primary sources where possible (Tay Bridge inquiry, Enron proceedings, early aviation accident records). Also: is there a documented case of a *cognitive* profession (not physical engineering) that made the transition through a non-visible-failure mechanism? If one exists, it may be the model for how software gets there.

### 4.8 — Price's Law and the bureaucratic concentration of composite demand

**Derek de Solla Price (1963) — *Little Science, Big Science***
Price's Law originated as a bibliometric observation: in any productive knowledge-work system, the square root of the total number of contributors produces half the total output. Originally observed in scientific paper authorship, it has since been replicated across software commits, patent filings, sales production, and creative output broadly.

The standard framing treats Price's Law as a curiosity about output distribution. The Track 4 framing treats it as something more structural: **the composite demand doesn't land randomly — it concentrates systematically on the square-root contributors**, because they are the people the organization can observe doing more than their share and therefore concludes should do more still.

**The bureaucratic mechanism — observed in practice:**
In a factory, the high producers are identifiable by output count. Price's Law is visible and, in principle, manageable. In a bureaucracy, the square-root contributors are the people whose judgment, relationships, synthesis capacity, and quiet problem-solving hold the organization together — output that is cognitively invisible (Axis 1) and unattributed (Axis 9) until it disappears. The organization discovers the square-root contributor's output primarily through its absence.

The composite demand concentrates on this population for a precise reason: they are the people who have *already demonstrated* the ability to hold more than their nominal role. The organization observes "this person can do X and Y" and concludes "therefore ask them to do Z as well." The logic feels sound because the observation is real — they *can* partially hold the composite. What the observation misses is that partial capacity is not infinite capacity, and the composite demand keeps growing while the contributor's capacity does not.

**The failure pattern this produces:**
The square-root contributors hold the composite longest before breaking — which makes the structure *look like it's working* right up until it catastrophically isn't. The organization gets a false signal: the composite demand appears sustainable because the most capable people are absorbing it longest. The failure, when it comes, is attributed to the individual ("burned out," "couldn't scale," "stopped performing") rather than to the accumulated composite load that the organization kept adding to the people least likely to refuse it.

This is the CTO failure pattern at organizational scale, operating across every bureaucracy before software existed.

**The Lean Six Sigma interaction:**
Lean cross-training was designed partly to address the single-point-of-failure problem that Price's Law creates — if half the output depends on the square root of contributors, losing one is catastrophic. The cross-training solution was to spread the square-root contributors' knowledge to the rest of the population. What it produced in many organizations instead: the square-root contributors were also asked to do the cross-training, adding knowledge transfer to their existing composite load. The solution compounded the problem it was designed to solve.

**The halo interaction (Axis 7):**
The square-root contributors are also the people most thoroughly haloed — they have the most visible track record of demonstrated competence across domains. The halo effect lands hardest on exactly this population, making them the least likely to refuse the composite demand (the halo creates an internal obligation to live up to it) and the most likely to be given it (the organization reads the track record as proof the composite is achievable).

**The authority compliance interaction (Track 1 / Axis 7):**
Milgram's finding — that people comply with authority-figure demands even when they privately disagree — applies here in both directions. The square-root contributor complies with the composite demand because the organizational authority applying it is real and the demand feels like the natural extension of their demonstrated capability. The organization applies the demand because the square-root contributor's history of compliance and delivery makes refusal unimaginable.

**Predicted spillage as research proceeds:**
- **→ Track 3 (Axes in the Wild):** Price's Law evidences Axis 1 (square-root output is invisible until absent), Axis 4 (no institutional body protects square-root contributors from composite overload), Axis 10 (Price's Law has been known since 1963 and organizations exploit rather than protect against it).
- **→ Track 2 (Expectation Audit):** If CTO candidates are disproportionately selected from the square-root contributor population — the best engineer, the one who's already doing more than their share — the composite demand doesn't just exist, it actively targets the people most capable of partially meeting it. JD archaeology and post-mortem analysis should test this.
- **→ Track 1 (Psychological Mechanisms):** The Price's Law concentration pattern interacts with Milgram (compliance), the halo effect (Axis 7), and cognitive dissonance (Festinger) — the contributor who has always delivered finds it harder to name the composite as unreasonable because their own history argues against them.

*Research to find:* Price's original 1963 text and the replication literature across domains. Specifically: has Price's Law been observed in organizational bureaucracies (not just research output), and is there evidence that composite demand concentrates on the square-root population? Also: the Lean Six Sigma literature on cross-training outcomes — does the evidence show cross-training reducing single-point-of-failure risk, or does it show the cross-training load landing on the square-root contributors?

### 4.9 — Where did the women go? The gendered reclassification of programming and what it installed

**⚠ Callout: This section is not a footnote.** The gender displacement of programming in the 1950s–1960s is not a diversity observation appended to the structural argument. It is the moment when the fungibility assumption and the composite demand were *actively installed* into the field — replacing a specialized workforce with a generalist template at the exact moment the field was becoming commercially important. Understanding what happened here restructures the argument: the composite demand was not an accident of the field's youth. It was a deliberate reclassification.

---

**The founding reality: programming was women's work**

In the 1940s and 1950s, programming was classified as clerical work — detail-oriented, procedural, execution-focused. Organizations had always assigned clerical work to women. The men designed the hardware. The women programmed it.

This was not incidental. It was explicit organizational logic: programming was understood as translating the (male) engineer's mathematical intent into machine instructions. Precise, skilled — but subordinate to the real engineering. The distinction between hardware (engineering, male, important) and software (clerical, female, support) was baked in from the beginning.

The ENIAC programmers — Kay McNulty, Jean Jennings (Bartik), Betty Snyder (Holberton), Marlyn Wescoff, Frances Bilas, Ruth Lichterman — were hired as "computers" (the literal job title for people who performed calculations), then became the first programmers of the first general-purpose electronic computer. They were not credited at the machine's public unveiling in 1946. The men who built the hardware stood in front of it. The women who made it compute were not in the photograph.

Grace Hopper invented the compiler. She had to fight to be taken seriously doing it — not because her work was questioned technically, but because the cultural logic said programmers executed instructions, they didn't invent new ones.

**The field was already complaining — before Brooks, before the 1968 NATO conference**

The software crisis discourse begins in the late 1950s, fifteen years before Brooks. Projects were late. Costs overran. Systems failed. The language of the problem was already present: the gap between what software was promised to deliver and what it actually delivered was documented and discussed in the early computing literature.

This is Axis 10 predating Brooks. The measurement was running. The complaints were being made. And simultaneously, the field was being reclassified.

**The reclassification: how programming became engineering and engineering became male**

As computers became commercially important through the late 1950s and into the 1960s, the cultural logic of who does important work activated. Programming stopped being clerical. It became engineering. And as it became engineering, it became male.

Nathan Ensmenger's *The Computer Boys Take Over* (2010) documents this reclassification with precision. Three mechanisms drove it:

**Mechanism 1 — Aptitude testing designed to filter for the existing male profile.**
In the late 1950s, the field introduced personality and aptitude tests to identify who "naturally" excelled at programming. The most influential was the Programmer Aptitude Test developed by IBM and variations used by other organizations. The profile these tests identified — antisocial, detail-obsessed, puzzle-oriented, male — was constructed from the *existing programmer population*, which was becoming increasingly male as the reclassification progressed. The test then filtered for that profile, creating a self-reinforcing loop: the test selected men, the selected men defined the programmer identity, the identity defined what the test measured.

The women who had built the field were being filtered out by a test measuring a profile constructed without them.

**Mechanism 2 — The professionalization narrative.**
Programming was reclassified as requiring mathematical rigor and engineering discipline, not clerical precision. This narrative served two purposes: it elevated the status of the work (and therefore the salaries), and it required the *kinds* of credentials — engineering degrees, mathematics backgrounds — that women were systematically excluded from in that era's universities. The credential requirement was not designed to exclude women; it didn't need to be. The exclusion from credential pathways was already in place.

**Mechanism 3 — The masculinization of hacker identity.**
MIT's Tech Model Railroad Club, the AI Lab culture of the early 1960s, the self-described "hacker" identity — this was the peer culture that defined what a *real* programmer was. It was overwhelmingly male, deliberately anti-social, and explicitly contemptuous of the "mere clerical" work that had defined programming a decade earlier. The genius-loner archetype (Axis 0) was being constructed in real time, and it was being constructed as male.

**What this installed — and what it destroyed**

The women didn't go anywhere. The definition of the job moved away from them.

What the reclassification installed:

- **The fungibility assumption entered through the masculinization door.** When programming was clerical and female, it was *specialized* — specific people with specific skills were identified and hired for it. When it became engineering and male, it inherited the generalist-bureaucrat template: any sufficiently smart (male) person could learn it. The fungibility assumption was not an organic product of the field's development. It arrived as the ideological justification for replacing a specialist female workforce with a generalist male one. Brooks was measuring the consequences of a substitution that had already happened.

- **The composite demand was installed simultaneously.** The "engineer who programs" was expected to understand hardware *and* software *and* systems design *and* eventually management — because the engineer was the generalist who could do all of it. The clerical programmer had been a specialist. The engineering programmer was a composite by definition.

- **The institutional memory of how the work was actually done was suppressed.** The women who built the first systems — who understood the work as specialized, precise, and non-fungible — were uncredited, photographed out of the frame, and replaced by a workforce that had been told the work was learnable by any smart person. The scar tissue that might have formed from their knowledge and experience was never allowed to accumulate.

**The 1968 NATO Software Engineering Conference — naming the crisis that had already been running for a decade**

The conference that coined the term "software engineering" was explicitly convened to address a crisis that had been documented for ten years. The participants knew the field was broken. The chosen response — formalize it as *engineering*, import the rigor and discipline of hardware engineering into software — doubled down on the reclassification rather than questioning it.

The women who had been doing the work were not prominent at the conference. The engineering identity that the conference institutionalized was the identity that had displaced them.

This is Axis 10 at its starkest: the measurement found the problem (the software crisis), the institutional response (software engineering) encoded the assumption that had caused it (smart engineers can learn to program, programming is engineering, engineering is generalist), and the demand persisted.

**The axes this section evidences simultaneously:**

- **Axis 0 (Fictional media):** The hacker/genius archetype that Axis 0 traces through media was being *constructed in real time* in the MIT culture of the early 1960s. The media archetype didn't invent the image — it amplified one that was being built by the reclassification.
- **Axis 2 (Institutional memory):** The institutional memory of how the work was actually done — specialized, non-fungible, built by people selected for it — was actively suppressed by the reclassification. The field's founding knowledge was overwritten before it could form scar tissue.
- **Axis 5 (Internal/external brake):** The internal brake that existed in the clerical-programming era — you needed specific people with specific skills — was *deliberately dismantled* by the aptitude testing and professionalization narrative. The external brake never formed because the public saw the reclassification as progress.
- **Axis 8 (Credential as capability signal):** The aptitude tests that drove the reclassification were credential-as-capability-signal in its purest form — a test measuring a constructed profile, used to certify "natural" programming ability, applied to filter out the people who had actually built the field.
- **Axis 10 (Measurement paradox):** The software crisis was being measured before Brooks, before the NATO conference, before any of the canonical management science texts on software. The field chose institutionalization over investigation. The demand persisted.

**The "Where did all the women go?" question as a research probe:**

This question is not primarily a gender question — though it is that too. It is a *structural* question: what happened to the organizational knowledge that programming was specialized work requiring specific people? That knowledge existed. It was held by the women who had built the field. It was suppressed by the reclassification. And when it was suppressed, the fungibility assumption and the composite demand moved in to fill the space it left.

If we can document the reclassification precisely — what was known, when it was known, what was lost when it was overwritten — we have evidence for the moment the irrationality was installed. Not as a gradual drift, but as a specific historical event with specific actors, specific mechanisms, and specific consequences that are still running fifty years later.

*Primary research targets:*
- **Ensmenger, Nathan — *The Computer Boys Take Over* (2010)** — the most comprehensive history of the reclassification. Primary source for mechanisms 1–3 above.
- **Abbate, Janet — *Recoding Gender* (2012)** — focuses specifically on women's experiences in computing across the reclassification period. Primary source for what was lost.
- **Light, Jennifer — "When Computers Were Women" (1999)** — Technology and Culture journal. The ENIAC programmer history. Peer-reviewed; high confidence.
- **Misa, Thomas (ed.) — *Gender Codes* (2010)** — cross-national comparative history of gender in computing.
- **The 1968 NATO Software Engineering Conference proceedings** — primary source for the institutionalization of the engineering identity. Available in full; should be read for what it assumes as well as what it argues.
- **Evans, Claire — *Broad Band* (2018)** — narrative history of women in computing; lower academic weight than Abbate/Ensmenger but broader coverage and readable for illustration.

## Methodology and Discipline

**Carry the corpus discipline throughout:**

- **Absence ≠ evidence.** The fact that no one has documented the CTO composite expectation collapsing under direct questioning does not mean it doesn't collapse — it means the question hasn't been asked systematically. Flag gaps as gaps, not as negative findings.
- **Behavioral evidence over sentiment.** JDs, post-mortems, and diligence criteria are behavioral artifacts. Practitioner accounts are sentiment. Weight accordingly — sentiment is useful for illustration, not for load-bearing claims.
- **The axes are hypotheses, not conclusions.** Track 3 tests them. If evidence contradicts an axis, update the axis — don't protect the hypothesis.
- **The clean separation must hold.** Every finding must be attributable to the expectation/attribution locus. If a finding implicates role design, name it clearly as role design and do not fold it into the expectation claim.
- **Source hierarchy:** peer-reviewed > named institution > named practitioner > content site. Flag the class of every source at the point of use.

**What would refute T2:**
- Evidence that decision-makers, asked directly, consistently endorse the composite expectation — no reflection gap.
- Evidence that CTOs who fail the transition are evaluated against a *stage-appropriate* standard, not the composite — the expectation is already modulated, and the misattribution claim weakens.
- Evidence that the composite expectation is recent (post-2010, say) and not structurally embedded — which would make it a fashion rather than a cultural permission structure, and would change the nature of the fix.

**What would refute T4:**
- Evidence that the "smart → learn anything" permission is equally applied to other cognitive domains (law, medicine) and the doctor thought experiment is culturally idiosyncratic rather than universal.
- Evidence that the permission is stage-specific to *early* software (pre-institutionalization) and is already closing as the field matures — which would make T4's claim historical rather than current.

### 5.1 — Authority Bias and Obedience to Authority
**Named mechanism:** Authority Bias / Obedience to Authority
**Axes fed:** 7 (halo-bounded authority), 4 (institutionally mediated vs. individually demanded competence)
**Fallacy form:** Appeal to Authority — treating a source's authority as sufficient grounds for a claim, independent of the claim's merits

**Original experiment:** Milgram (1961–1963)
Ordinary people administered what they believed were dangerous electric shocks to strangers when instructed to do so by an authority figure in a lab coat. 65% continued to the maximum level. The finding: compliance is driven by role structure and authority signaling, not by individual agreement with the demand. Participants frequently expressed private disagreement while continuing to comply.

The behavior/endorsement dissociation is the T2 mechanism: the same person who applies the composite CTO standard (behavioral compliance) may not endorse it when asked directly (private disagreement). Milgram documented this dissociation at scale under far less pressure than a board meeting or a hiring decision.

**Replication literature and boundary conditions:**
- **Burger (2009)** — partial replication (stopped at 150V for ethical reasons); confirmed ~70% compliance in contemporary US populations. *Spillage to Track 2:* the effect operates in contemporary organizational decision-making, not just wartime-era populations.
- **Dolinski et al. (2017)** — Polish replication, 90% compliance. *Spillage to Track 3 (Axis 6):* cross-cultural generality suggests the broken feedback loop is not culturally specific to the US tech industry.
- **Proximity and dissenter variations** — compliance drops when harm is visible and proximate; drops dramatically when one peer-level person refuses. *Spillage to Track 2:* the single-dissenter finding is the most actionable — one credible person in the hiring room naming the composite as unreasonable is predicted to substantially reduce compliance with the composite standard.
- **Meeus & Raaijmakers (1986)** — organizational replication in performance review contexts; participants harmed job applicants when instructed by authority figures. *Spillage to Track 2:* authority compliance operates in hiring and evaluation contexts specifically.

*Research to find:* Meeus & Raaijmakers (1986) in full. Any replications in executive hiring or board evaluation contexts.

---

### 5.2 — Conformity and Social Proof
**Named mechanism:** Conformity / Social Proof
**Axes fed:** 5 (internal vs. external brake), 6 (feedback loop integrity)
**Fallacy form:** Argumentum ad Populum — a claim is treated as correct because many people believe it

**Original experiment:** Asch (1951–1956)
Participants gave answers they privately knew were wrong because a unanimous group gave those answers first. The effect dropped to near-zero when a single other person gave the correct answer. Social unanimity overrides private knowledge; a single dissenter restores private judgment.

The composite CTO expectation may not be generated by any individual reasoning from first principles — it is socially reproduced through conformity. No individual would generate the composite independently; Asch's mechanism makes it self-reinforcing across every hiring room.

**Replication literature and boundary conditions:**
- **Bond & Smith meta-analysis (1996)** — 133 studies, 17 countries; effect holds universally, varies by individualism/collectivism. *Spillage to Track 3 (Axis 5):* external brake failure is not culturally specific.
- **Expert group conformity** — domain expertise reduces but does not eliminate conformity on cross-domain questions. *Spillage to Track 2:* a technical expert in a board room may resist conformity on architecture questions but remain susceptible on "what a CTO should be able to do" — a cross-domain question where their expertise doesn't directly apply.
- **The unanimity condition** — one dissenter substantially reduces conformity. *Spillage to Track 2:* same intervention as Milgram's dissenter finding — two independent experiments pointing at the same lever.

*Research to find:* Bond & Smith (1996). Any Asch replications in organizational hiring or evaluation contexts.

---

### 5.3 — The Halo Effect
**Named mechanism:** Halo Effect
**Axes fed:** 7 (halo-bounded vs. scope-bounded authority), 8 (credential as capability signal)
**Fallacy form:** Hasty Generalization — inferring a general property from a specific observed one

**Original formulation:** Thorndike (1920)
Military officers rated subordinates uniformly high or low across multiple independent dimensions — a single strong impression contaminated all subsequent ratings. Global impression drives specific judgment, not the reverse.

The founding CTO's technical excellence halos into assumed managerial and strategic excellence. Three different jobs, one halo.

**Replication literature and boundary conditions:**
- **Nisbett & Wilson (1977)** — participants rated a professor higher on unrelated dimensions when they liked his accent. *Spillage to Track 2:* a technically impressive CTO candidate generates a halo that contaminates evaluation of management and strategic capacity — dimensions the interview typically doesn't test.
- **Rosenzweig — The Halo Effect (2007)** — company performance halos into assumed quality of leadership and strategy; the same CEO is brilliant when the company does well and a poor strategist when it doesn't. *Spillage to Track 2:* CTO post-mortems describing "not strategic enough" may be reverse-halo from company underperformance, not an independent assessment.
- **Performance review halo literature** — halo effect is among the most documented biases in performance evaluation. *Spillage to Track 3 (Axis 10):* the measurement paradox is partly a halo problem — CTO performance metrics are contaminated by overall impression, making individual-dimension assessment unreliable.

*Research to find:* Rosenzweig (2007) case studies in full. Performance review halo literature in technical leadership contexts.

### 5.4 — Availability Heuristic
**Named mechanism:** Availability Heuristic
**Axes fed:** 0 (fictional media portrayal), 2 (institutional memory relative to complexity growth)
**Fallacy form:** Anecdotal Evidence — using vivid, available examples as the basis for probability estimates

**Original formulation:** Tversky & Kahneman (1973)
People estimate the probability of events based on how easily examples come to mind rather than on actual frequency. Vivid, recent, or emotionally salient examples are overweighted; abstract statistical evidence is underweighted even when it is more accurate.

The genius-composite archetype is maximally available — seen thousands of times across decades of media. When a CEO forms an expectation of what a CTO should be, the available prototype is Tony Stark, not the ~50% IC→EM reversion rate. The statistical base rate is abstract; the genius composite is vivid and immediately retrievable.

**Replication literature and boundary conditions:**
- **Kahneman & Tversky (1974)** — canonical "Heuristics and Biases" summary. *Spillage to Track 1 (1.4):* the "great man" theory of leadership is partly an availability effect — Churchill and Roosevelt outweigh the base rate of composite-leader failures.
- **Media effects on availability** — media exposure directly increases the cognitive availability of depicted scenarios. *Spillage to Track 3 (Axis 0):* the fictional media axis has an empirical cognitive mechanism — availability heuristic research provides the science behind why fiction installs priors.
- **The CSI effect (Shelton, 2008)** — jurors expected forensic evidence that doesn't exist in real cases because television normalized it. *Spillage to Track 3 (Axis 0):* the closest documented analog to the "CTO effect" hypothesis — fictional media shaping occupational expectations in a way that distorts real-world evaluation.

*Research to find:* The CSI effect literature — particularly studies measuring the gap between televised occupational depictions and realistic practitioner expectations.

### 5.5 — Cognitive Dissonance and Belief Perseverance
**Named mechanism:** Cognitive Dissonance / Belief Perseverance
**Axes fed:** 10 (measurement paradox), 6 (feedback loop integrity)
**Fallacy form:** No direct fallacy name — a documented psychological mechanism, not a formal reasoning error

**Original formulation:** Festinger (1957)
When behavior and belief conflict, humans resolve the dissonance by updating the *belief* to match the *behavior* — not the reverse.

The measurement paradox is stable partly because dissonance keeps resolving in the wrong direction. A CEO who has applied the composite CTO standard repeatedly and watched CTOs fail resolves the dissonance by strengthening the standard ("we need a better CTO") rather than questioning it ("the standard may be wrong"). Repetition doesn't erode the standard — it reinforces it.

**Replication literature and boundary conditions:**
- **Festinger & Carlsmith (1959)** — less external justification produces more internal belief revision. *Spillage to Track 2:* CEOs who have invested heavily in a CTO hire resolve failure more aggressively toward "wrong person" than "wrong standard."
- **Belief perseverance (Ross, Lepper & Hubbard, 1975)** — beliefs formed on evidence persist even after the evidence is explicitly discredited. *Spillage to Track 3 (Axis 10):* the composite expectation, formed on genius-composite mythology, persists even when the mythology is challenged.
- **Escalation of commitment (Staw, 1976)** — organizational commitments to failing strategies are maintained and escalated, not revised. *Spillage to Track 4:* the measurement paradox at civilizational scale — Taylor through CHAOS Report, all ignored — may be organizational cognitive dissonance operating across institutional generations.

*Research to find:* Staw (1976) on escalation of commitment — specifically whether the same dynamic applies to role-design decisions as to failing project strategies.

### 5.6 — Illusion of Explanatory Depth
**Named mechanism:** Illusion of Explanatory Depth (IOED)
**Axes fed:** 6 (feedback loop integrity), 1 (tangibility of complexity)
**Fallacy form:** Overconfidence Bias — systematically overestimating one's understanding of a domain

**Original formulation:** Rozenblit & Keil (2002)
People believe they understand complex causal systems far better than they do. When asked to explain the mechanism in detail, they discover the gap — and their confidence rating drops dramatically. The illusion is strongest for *causal mechanisms*, not facts or procedures.

The CEO who believes they understand what a CTO does is operating under IOED. They have observed the outputs (the product ships, the team grows) without understanding the causal mechanism (what the CTO is actually doing to produce those outputs). They will be confidently wrong rather than uncertainly ignorant — and the IOED persists because the explanatory test is rarely applied.

**Replication literature and boundary conditions:**
- **Fernbach et al. (2013)** — people who held strong policy positions became more moderate when asked to explain the *mechanisms* by which their preferred policy would achieve its goals. The explanatory demand revealed the gap; the gap reduced confidence; reduced confidence moderated the position. *Spillage to Track 2:* asking "how exactly do you expect the CTO to transition from Builder to Strategist?" is an explanatory demand that may reveal the mechanism gap and moderate the composite expectation. This may be the empirical basis for T2's reflection-gap hypothesis.
- **IOED in technical domains** — the illusion is stronger when the observer has partial knowledge — enough to feel informed, not enough to actually explain. *Spillage to Track 3 (Axis 1):* non-technical decision-makers have exactly the partial-knowledge profile that produces the strongest IOED.

*Research to find:* Fernbach et al. (2013) in full. Test whether the Fernbach mechanism applies to role-design beliefs as well as policy beliefs.

### 5.7 — Planning Fallacy and Optimism Bias
**Named mechanism:** Planning Fallacy / Optimism Bias
**Axes fed:** 2 (institutional memory relative to complexity growth), 10 (measurement paradox)
**Fallacy form:** Base Rate Neglect — ignoring statistical base rates in favor of specific case reasoning

**Original formulation:** Kahneman & Tversky (1979); elaborated Kahneman (2011)
People systematically underestimate time, cost, and risk of future actions while overestimating benefits — even with direct experience of past failures. The planning fallacy persists because people evaluate plans from the "inside view" (this specific plan, this specific person) rather than the "outside view" (the base rate of similar plans and similar people).

Every CTO hire is evaluated from the inside view. The base rate (the ~50% IC→EM reversion rate, the 18–24 month average tenure, the CHAOS Report failure data) is the outside view that would correct the optimism. The planning fallacy predicts the inside view wins — which is why the measurement exists and the demand persists unchanged.

**Replication literature and boundary conditions:**
- **Buehler, Griffin & Ross (1994)** — planning fallacy persists even when people are explicitly asked to recall their past performance on similar tasks. *Spillage to Track 2:* a CEO who has replaced two CTOs for "not scaling" will still evaluate the third candidate optimistically — the inside view of the new candidate overrides the outside view of the past failure pattern.
- **Reference class forecasting (Flyvbjerg, 2008)** — the only intervention shown to reliably correct the planning fallacy is forcing use of the outside view through reference class data. *Spillage to Track 2:* introducing base rate data at the hiring decision point ("here is the distribution of outcomes for composite CTO hires at this stage") may be the intervention that closes the reflection gap T2 is testing.

*Research to find:* Flyvbjerg (2008) — specifically whether reference class forecasting changes organizational hiring decisions, not just project planning.
