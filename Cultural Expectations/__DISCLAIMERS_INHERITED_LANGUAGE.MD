# Preface — The Arrow, Not the Spear

There are two ways this research can become a weapon, and they kill differently.

The companion file, [[__DISCLAIMERS_EXPECTATIONS_AND_BELIEFS]], guards against the **spear**: a verdict you pick up, aim, and thrust. "Irrational." You are holding it. The target is in front of you. There is a wielder, and the guards there mostly work by *restraining the wielder's hand* — don't aim past the fence, don't sentence the person, don't re-compress the word.

This file guards against the **arrow**, and the arrow is worse in a specific way: **it was already nocked in your hand before you could choose it.** The words *normal*, *failure*, *flaw*, *fault*, *"that's just how she is,"* *"he failed to,"* *"throws like a girl"* — you absorbed these from parents, teachers, peers, before you could consent to them. So the weaponization is not an act you commit. **It is a reflex you inherited.** You fire it without registering it is a weapon, because that is simply how people talk.

That single fact — *installed before consent* — produces everything that makes the arrow distinct:

- **No conscious wielder.** The spear guards assume someone you can ask to lower the weapon. The arrow has no one to ask. The "archer" is the culture that handed everyone the bow in childhood.
- **It lands on a third party.** A reflex does not aim. It fires at whoever is standing there — the junior developer in the room, the reclassified woman in the record — not at a chosen opponent. Collateral, not target.
- **It travels further from the source.** The harm compounds far from where the word was fired, across years and generations, because an inherited reflex is *taught onward* to everyone you raise and manage. The access-restriction loop runs for generations on this.
- **It arrives before it is seen.** You cannot hear the connotation in a word you have heard your whole life as neutral. The harm has landed before awareness catches up — if it ever does.

This is **Axis 0 operating in the weaponization layer.** The same "installed in childhood, before any institution" mechanism that installs the genius-composite prior also installs the loaded vocabulary. By the time anyone writes a job description or runs a post-mortem, the words are already in hand, already feel neutral, already fire on reflex.

> *The spear is a choice. The arrow is a habit. You can ask a person to drop a spear. You cannot ask them to drop a reflex they cannot feel themselves holding.*

# The Defense: Detect, Then De-inherit

Because there is no wielder to restrain, restraint is the wrong defense. You cannot will yourself to stop firing a weapon you cannot feel in your hand. The defense has two steps, in order.

## Step 1 — Detect: make the reflex visible

You cannot examine what you cannot hear. The first job is to surface the connotation you have been deaf to — to make the inherited word *audible to its own speaker.* This is what the language layer is built to do (see [[00_README]] in `Expectations and Beliefs/`):

- The **grammatical-migration test** ([[00_TAXONOMY]] §4.1): does the word complete *"I am a ___"* or *"that was your ___"*? If so, it is an arrow — it carries onto a person before you decide. *failure, flaw, mistake, fault, defect* all pass this test and most speakers never notice.
- The **common/normal probe** ([[common_vs_normal_definition_doc]]): am I describing a frequency, or smuggling in a prescription? "Common" describes; "normal" sentences. The slide is invisible from the inside.
- The **failure-word audit** ([[failure_language_framework]]): the same word ("failure") is a feature in one room and a verdict in another; the speaker rarely notices which room they are in.
- The **automated auditor** ([[language_auditor_prompt]]): because human reviewers develop *familiarity blindness to their own habitual language* — the arrow is, by definition, the word you cannot hear yourself say — a consistent outside check is required. **A missed flag is more harmful than a false positive here**, precisely because the whole danger is the words that do not announce themselves.

Detection does not convict. It only makes the reflex visible enough to examine — the same "flag, don't sentence" discipline as the spear file's guard #6, applied to language instead of verdicts.

## Step 2 — De-inherit: re-examine the word you did not choose

Once a word is visible, the second step is to consciously re-decide whether to keep it — to *de-inherit* it. This is the "point the gun back at yourself" discipline ([[README]] in `Author's Experiences/`) applied to vocabulary you absorbed before you could consent:

- *Did I choose this word, or did I receive it?*
- *What does it actually land on — the process, or the person?*
- *Whose voice am I repeating when I say it — and did they examine it either?*
- *If I had to defend the connotation out loud, could I — or would I find it indefensible the moment it was said plainly?*

De-inheritance is not censorship. It is the difference between a word you *use* and a word that *uses you*. The inherited reflex says "she failed to lead." The de-inherited speaker asks what the *role* expected and what the *process* did or did not supply — and finds the verdict was never the person's to carry.

> *Detection makes the arrow visible in flight. De-inheritance unstrings the bow you were handed as a child. Neither is possible by restraint alone, because you cannot restrain what you cannot feel — you can only learn to feel it, and then choose again.*

# Why this is its own file

The spear and the arrow share a target — both turn this research into a weapon against people and beliefs. But they require **opposite defenses**, which is why they cannot live in one list of guards:

| | The spear ([[__DISCLAIMERS_EXPECTATIONS_AND_BELIEFS]]) | The arrow (this file) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **The weapon** | a verdict (irrational/illogical/unreasonable) | an inherited word (normal/failure/fault/"throws like a girl") |
| **The wielder** | someone who chose to aim | no one — a reflex installed in childhood |
| **When it's chosen** | at the moment of the thrust | before the speaker could consent |
| **Who it hits** | the chosen target | whoever is standing there (a third party) |
| **How it's seen** | visible — you know you're attacking | invisible — feels like neutral speech |
| **The defense** | restrain the hand (don't aim past the fence) | detect, then de-inherit (you can't restrain a reflex) |

A reader could obey every guard in the spear file — never aim a verdict past the fence, never sentence a person on purpose — and still spend all day firing arrows: "normal," "failure," "that's your fault," fired on reflex, landing on third parties, taught onward to the next generation. **Guarding the spear does not lower the bow.** That is why the arrow needs its own discipline, and its own file.
