# Research Capture — Lossy Information Hierarchy
**Date:** June 26, 2026
**Status:** Research flag — open track; needs grounding
**Connects to:** 09_RESEARCH_bullet_problem.md; Axis 6b (blocked loop); Brake-Location Pattern; Authority/Consequence Misalignment

---

## The Framing

The system, culture, or environment encourages highly lossy tiered information flow toward leaders. Some decisions need to be closer to the **professional experts who were hired to understand the problem** — and instead a summary given to the leader.

The failure is not that leaders are unintelligent or inattentive. The failure is a **system design that routes decision authority upward while routing information upward in progressively lossier form.** By the time the decision reaches the person with authority, the information required to make it well has been stripped by the layers it passed through.

The correct inversion: **the decision travels toward the expertise. The summary travels toward the leader.**

---

## The Core Questions

1. How does information actually reach leadership — what transformations does it undergo at each layer?
2. How do leaders prefer to receive information — and why does that preference shape what gets sent?
3. Why do leaders carry cognitive load that makes full-fidelity information unprocessable?
4. Why is high-level authority granted to people furthest from the problem rather than closest to it?

**These are one question with four facets:** Why does authority concentrate at the point furthest from the information?

---

## The Load-Bearing Claim

The authority/distance inversion is the structural condition all other problems sit on top of:
- Leaders receive condensed information *because* they're furthest from the source and decision volume is too high for full narratives
- Leaders prefer condensed formats *because* condensed formats are the only thing processable at their decision volume
- Leaders carry cognitive load *because* the role design places maximum decision authority on the person with minimum domain proximity
- The inversion persists *because* the organizational model was designed for a world where decisions were fewer, slower, and more legible

---

## What the System Should Do Instead

**Current state:** Information → [stripped at each layer] → Leader → Decision → [travels back down]

**Correct design:** Information → Expert Decision → Summary → Leader (ratification / awareness / resource allocation)

The leader's role in this model is not to make the technical decision. It is to:
- Allocate resources the expert identified as needed
- Remove organizational blockers the expert can't remove from their position
- Ratify decisions that require organizational authority to execute
- Receive a summary sufficient to understand what was decided and why

The expert's role is not to produce a bullet that makes the leader comfortable. It is to make the decision they were hired to make and produce a summary that keeps leadership informed.

## Known Connected Cases

**Columbia (Tufte / CAIB 2003):** Engineers held the expertise; decision authority was held by management; information traveled upward in PowerPoint format that stripped the causal structure; the decision was made by people furthest from the foam strike data. The correct design would have been: engineers decide whether the shuttle is safe to re-enter; management receives the summary.

**18-month performance fight:** Engineer held the expertise and the evidence; CTO held the decision authority; information traveled upward in whatever form the CTO would receive; decision was deferred for 18 months. The correct design: engineer decides the architecture needs to change; CTO receives the summary and allocates resources.

**The 10^17 combinations / repository pattern case:** Engineers identified the prerequisite (repository pattern refactor) for the fix (automated testing). Management declined the prerequisite without engaging with the dependency structure. The correct design: engineers own the technical decision about what's required; management owns the resource allocation decision about whether to fund it.

## Research Areas to Pursue

1. **Cognitive load in executive decision-making** — Kahneman (Thinking Fast and Slow, 2011); decision fatigue (Danziger et al. 2011 — judges making worse decisions as the day progresses; decision quality degrades with volume regardless of stakes).

2. **Information filtering in organizational hierarchies** — how information transforms as it moves upward; what gets stripped at each layer and why. Look for: "information asymmetry," "hierarchical communication," "upward distortion."

3. **Span of control research** — what assumptions about information flow were baked into classical organizational theory (Fayol, Taylor, Weber); whether those assumptions hold in knowledge-work organizations.

4. **The proximity-authority inversion as a named concept** — is there organizational literature that explicitly names the problem of authority residing furthest from expertise? Look for: "decision rights," "knowledge-based decision making," "subsidiarity principle" (Catholic social teaching / EU governance — decisions should be made at the lowest competent level; may have organizational behavior analogues).

5. **Subsidiarity as the correct design principle** — the subsidiarity principle states that decisions should be made at the lowest level capable of making them competently, with higher levels involved only when the lower level cannot handle it alone. This is the organizational theory version of "decision travels toward expertise, summary travels toward leader." Verify whether subsidiarity has been applied in organizational behavior literature outside its governance/political origins.

6. **The Westrum organizational culture typology** — Ron Westrum (2004) classified organizations as pathological (power-oriented), bureaucratic (rule-oriented), or generative (performance-oriented). Generative organizations route information toward decision-makers who can act on it; pathological organizations filter information to protect power structures. This may be the existing framework that names the lossy hierarchy as a culture type rather than a design failure.

## The Axis Connection

This finding is the **structural explanation for why Axis 6b (blocked loop) is stable.** The loop is blocked not because individual leaders are malicious or incompetent but because the system design routes decision authority to the people with the least information fidelity. The blockage is the predictable output of a system that was not designed for knowledge-work decision-making.

**Possible new axis or condition:** The authority/distance inversion may warrant its own axis entry — distinct from Axis 6b (blocked loop) because it explains *why* the loop is structurally blocked rather than incidentally blocked. The loop is blocked by design, not by accident.
