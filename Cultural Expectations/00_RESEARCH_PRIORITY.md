**Purpose:** Weight every named research section by depth of available evidence, value to the framework regardless of outcome, and axis relevance — so the first pass returns the most information, whether it confirms or disconfirms the hypotheses.

## First-Pass Shortlist (Top 8–10)

For each: what a **confirmed** finding means, what a **disconfirmed** finding means, and spillage.

### 1. 4.9 — Gendered reclassification
- **Confirmed:** The fungibility assumption arrived as the ideological justification for a specific 1950s–60s substitution, with named actors and mechanisms (aptitude tests, professionalization, hacker-identity). The composite demand is *installed*, not inherited-by-drift — the argument gains a birth date.
- **Disconfirmed:** If the reclassification was gradual, market-driven, and not accompanied by suppression of specialist knowledge, the "deliberate installation" claim retreats to "the field masculinized as it scaled," and Axis 2's section-4.9 dependency weakens to ordinary youth-of-field.
- **Spillage:** Axes 0, 2, 5, 8, 10 → Track 3 (every "in the wild" section for those axes inherits its origin event here). → Track 4.5 (same substitution, told as migration). Feeds the Axis 0 archetype-construction claim with a real-time documented case.

### 2. 4.7 — Occupational transitions (Tay Bridge pattern)
- **Confirmed:** Each profession's specialization was triggered by *visibly and individually attributable failure* (germ theory, Tay Bridge, Enron, barnstormer deaths). Establishes the precondition software lacks and sharpens the prediction about software's required trigger.
- **Disconfirmed:** If any profession specialized *without* a visible-failure trigger (credentialing fashion, guild economics), the "attributed failure is necessary" claim breaks — high-information, because software might then transition without a catastrophe.
- **Spillage:** Axes 1, 2, 9, 10 → Track 3. The "cognitive profession that transitioned without visible failure" sub-question, if answered yes, spills directly into the CTO-corpus *fix* discussion.

### 3. 2.1 — JD archaeology
- **Confirmed:** CTO JDs demand all three modes simultaneously, at seed stage, with timeline pressure — and CFO/CMO/CRO JDs at equivalent stages do not. The composite is documentable as a CTO-specific outlier.
- **Disconfirmed:** If CFO/CRO JDs are equally composite, the CTO isn't special and the argument generalizes to all executives (still interesting, but T2's CTO-specific framing weakens). If JDs are already stage-modulated, the "unreflective expectation" claim weakens toward "calibrated expectation."
- **Spillage:** Axes 1, 5, 6, 7, 8 → Track 3. Comparison-artifact result feeds 2.3 (diligence) and 2.2 (post-mortems). Also tests 4.8's prediction (are CTOs drawn from the square-root population?).

### 4. 3.2 — Axis 2 in the wild (2008 / NASA / CHAOS)
- **Confirmed:** 30 years of CHAOS data with no institutional brake documents the memory deficit; 2008 shows an *old* field failing in a *young* subspecialty (validates the ratio claim over the age claim).
- **Disconfirmed:** If the field *did* respond to CHAOS with structural brakes (Agile-as-brake, regulatory attempts), Axis 2's "no scar tissue formed" claim needs revision.
- **Spillage:** Axes 1, 9, 10 → all of Track 4 (the measurement timeline ends here). CHAOS is the empirical spine shared with 3.10 and 4.4.

### 5. 4.8 — Price's Law
- **Confirmed:** Price's Law observed in organizational bureaucracies (not just research output) *and* evidence that composite demand concentrates on square-root contributors. Converts the personal observation into a tested targeting mechanism.
- **Disconfirmed:** If Price's Law doesn't replicate in bureaucracies, or load distributes evenly, the "composite targets the most capable" claim is just intuition — and the CTO-selection prediction in 2.1 loses its mechanism.
- **Spillage:** Axes 1, 4, 7, 10 → Track 3. → Track 2 (CTO candidate selection). → Track 1 (Milgram/halo/dissonance interaction on the over-loaded contributor).

### 6. 5.6 — Illusion of Explanatory Depth (Fernbach)
- **Confirmed:** The explanatory demand ("how exactly does the CTO go from Builder to Strategist?") reduces confidence and moderates the composite expectation — the *empirical engine* of the T2 reflection-gap test, making T2 testable rather than rhetorical.
- **Disconfirmed:** If role-design beliefs resist the Fernbach effect (unlike policy beliefs), the reflection-gap intervention may not work — redirecting the "fix" toward structural (credential/title-split) rather than cognitive remedies.
- **Spillage:** Axes 1, 6 → Track 3. **Strong spillage to Track 2.4** — this section may *be* the mechanism the reflection-gap test relies on (see Dependency Notes).

### 7. 1.4 — Great-man / transformational leadership
- **Confirmed:** Burns/Bass transformational-leadership theory institutionalized the composite as the *expected form* of executive excellence in MBA curricula — the organizational-theory version of Axis 0.
- **Disconfirmed:** If the leadership literature actually warns against composite overreach (or predicts transition failure), the academy is *not* installing the prior, and Axis 0's organizational arm weakens.
- **Spillage:** Axes 0, 7 → Track 3. Feeds 5.4 (great-man theory as an availability effect — Churchill/Roosevelt outweighing base rates).

### 8. 3.9 — Axis 9 in the wild (liability history)
- **Confirmed:** Fields that acquired liability frameworks late (aviation post-crashes, financial advising post-2008) show brake installation *after* the liability event — validating liability as the master enforcement variable.
- **Disconfirmed:** If brakes installed *before* or *without* liability (professional norms alone), liability drops from "master variable" to "one of several" — a foundational revision to Axis 9.
- **Spillage:** Axes 1, 2, 4 → Track 3 and Track 4.7 (liability is step 4 of the transition sequence). The "why has software liability legislation failed?" sub-question spills to the CTO fix discussion.

### 9. 4.2 — Taylor
- **Confirmed:** Taylor solved fungibility for *workers* by decomposition but *amplified* it for managers, whose value proposition became the composite. The CTO inherits the Taylorist manager role directly.
- **Disconfirmed:** If Taylor's model actually bounded managerial scope, the "manager composite was amplified" claim weakens and the bureaucrat→CTO lineage loses a link.
- **Spillage:** Axes 2, 8, 10 → Track 3. Pairs with 4.4 (Drucker's critique of Taylorism) and 4.1 (Weber's bureaucrat as Taylor's manager-precursor).

### 10. 3.10 — Axis 10 in the wild (citation trail)
- **Confirmed:** Brooks cites prior failures, CHAOS cites Brooks, reversion literature cites CHAOS — and practice didn't change. The measurement paradox is *documented*, making the Axis 10 capstone load-bearing.
- **Disconfirmed:** If the citation trail is broken (each generation rediscovered independently), it's a *memory deficit* (Axis 2), not akrasia (Axis 10) — collapsing the Axis 2 / Axis 10 distinction and weakening the "civilizational akrasia" capstone.
- **Spillage:** Axes 2, 10 → Track 4 (the entire pre-software timeline is the citation trail's body). Directly tests the Axis 2 / Axis 10 boundary (see Redundancy Flags).

## Dependency Notes

Sequencing constraints where one section's finding changes what a later section is looking for:

1. **4.9 before 4.5, 3.0, and the Axis-0/2/5/8 "in the wild" sections.** 4.9 dates and mechanizes the installation of the fungibility assumption; researched first, the others inherit a concrete origin event. **4.9 is the keystone dependency of Track 4.**
2. **5.6 (IOED/Fernbach) before 2.4 (reflection-gap test).** 2.4's diagnostic question is mechanically a Fernbach explanatory demand. Whether the effect transfers from policy beliefs to role-design beliefs determines whether 2.4's test can work at all.
3. **5.1 / 5.2 (Milgram/Asch dissenter findings) before 2.4 and 2.3.** The single-dissenter intervention is the predicted lever for breaking the composite expectation; the audit should know whether the lever exists before designing its "what would change the answer" component.
4. **4.7 before the software-non-transition synthesis and any CTO "fix" discussion.** 4.7 defines the precondition (attributed visible failure) software lacks; any claim about how software *could* transition depends on it.
5. **3.2 (CHAOS/2008) and 3.10 (citation trail) are co-dependent** — both rest on the same measurement timeline. Research as a pair; the citation trail is only legible once the data points are pinned.
6. **2.1 (JD archaeology) before 2.2 (post-mortems) and 2.3 (diligence).** The JD is the unguarded baseline expectation; post-mortems and diligence are the expectation under failure and under review — you need the baseline to read the deviation.
7. **4.8 (Price's Law) feeds a specific test in 2.1/2.2** — "are CTO candidates disproportionately drawn from square-root contributors?" Soft dependency (parallel-OK, but 4.8's result enriches the Track 2 search).

## Missing Research Targets

Gaps the axes predict but the tracks don't yet cover:

1. **Axis 3 (structural obscuring) in *software specifically*.** Track 3.3 only researches the finance analog (fee disclosure). No section hunts the software-side evidence (consultancy billing models, "10x engineer" rhetoric as headcount suppression, outsourcing economics). Axis 3 is the **least-evidenced axis in the plan** and risks remaining an assertion.
2. **Axis 7's "internal trap" (self-applied halo).** The axes file claims the CTO applies the halo to *themselves*, generating an internal obligation. No section tests it. Self-efficacy, impostor-phenomenon, and identity-based-obligation literature is deep and would directly evidence the identity-trap claim several downstream arguments lean on.
3. **A positive control for the reflection gap.** No section runs the JD/diligence audit on a *licensed profession*. The CFO/CMO comparison is within knowledge-work; a medicine/law comparison would test the cultural-permission claim (T4) directly and give 2.1 a calibrated baseline.
4. **The "one dissenter" intervention as a field-observable phenomenon.** 5.1/5.2 establish the dissenter effect in the lab but no section looks for *documented real cases* (founders reporting one board member/advisor broke the composite expectation). This is the most actionable lever in the framework and currently has only lab evidence.
5. **Axis 6 measured directly (the legibility gap as a quantity).** 3.6 looks for the two-cultures concept and the JD-as-artifact, but no section attempts to *measure* the gap (technical due-diligence translation, CTO-to-board communication breakdown). It's treated as self-evident; it's testable.
6. **Survivorship / base-rate data on CTOs who *did* transition all three modes.** No section seeks a measured population of CTOs who successfully held all three across seed→scale. Its absence is a falsification blind spot — finding even a small such population would force a major revision (the composite is rare-but-real, not impossible).

## Redundancy Flags

Axes or sections describing the same mechanism, flagged for tightening before research runs:

1. **Axis 2 vs. Axis 10 — sharpest redundancy risk, but ultimately *not* redundant.** The plan anticipates this. Axis 2 = memory *doesn't exist yet*; Axis 10 = memory *exists, is cited, and is ignored*. **The distinction lives or dies on 3.10's citation-trail finding.** If the trail is broken (independent rediscovery each generation), the two axes collapse into one. Not a redundancy to fix now — the **highest-stakes boundary test in the plan**, which 3.10 will confirm or dissolve.
2. **Axis 7 vs. Axis 8 — genuine overlap; hold the line in evidence.** Axis 7 generalizes from *demonstrated* competence; Axis 8 needs only the *title*. Clean in theory, hard to keep separate in practice (a haloed CTO is also a titled CTO). Sections 5.3, 3.7, 3.8 all touch both. Risk: double-counting the same evidence. Recommend a single pass tagging each datum as title-only (8) vs. demonstration-generalized (7).
3. **Track 1 vs. Track 5 — structural duplication by design.** 1.1≈5.1 (Milgram), 1.2≈5.2 (Asch), 1.5≈5.5 (Festinger). Intended (Track 5 = mechanism home base, Track 1 = T2 application), but the *research cost is shared*: research 5.1/5.2/5.5 once, then write Track 1 as synthesis, not a second literature pass.
4. **3.7 vs. 5.3 (halo) and 3.0 vs. 5.4 (CSI/availability) — near-duplicates across the wild/bias split.** Mechanism established in Track 5; applied to cases in Track 3. One literature pass, two write-ups.
5. **3.4 vs. 3.9 (fiduciary mediation vs. liability) — overlapping evidence base.** Axis 4 "exists because there's a liability framework behind it" (Axis 9). Same financial-regulation and medical-malpractice sources. Research as one "liability→mediation" pass, not two.
6. **Tension, not redundancy: Axis 0 vs. 1.4.** Two *delivery channels* for the same prior (one person can hold the composite) — fictional media vs. management education. Not redundant (different mechanisms, different evidence bases) but should be framed as **two arms of one claim** so the synthesis doesn't present them as independent corroboration.

## Closing Note on Discipline

The thin-evidence/high-value sections (2.1, 2.4, 4.8) earn their rank from collectible primary data or framework-moving potential, not from existing literature depth. The single most consequential result in the plan may be **3.10's citation-trail finding**, because it is the hinge on which the Axis 2 / Axis 10 distinction — and therefore the entire "civilizational akrasia" capstone — either holds or collapses.
