Here's the C-suite timeline and what it tells us:
CEO — title appears ~1917, coinciding with the formalization of modern corporate structure. Created to answer one question: who is in charge? The job is leadership from day one. No mode rotation required — it's the same job at every scale, just larger.
CFO — for most of the 20th century the role did not exist. Financial considerations played a much smaller role in corporations so there was no reason for companies to seek out the strategic advice of their back-office financial department. The title crystallized in the 1970s-80s, in response to an ambiguous regulatory change in accounting rules in 1979, which threatened to reduce reported earnings further at a time when corporate earnings already were under great strain. The CFO was created by external pressure — regulation, inflation, shareholder complexity. The skill set was always financial stewardship. What changed was the stakes, not the domain. LinkedInSage Journals
CMO — Coca-Cola introduced the CMO title in 1993. Born at large enterprises already operating at scale, already with marketing departments beneath the person receiving the title. Interestingly, average CMO tenure in 2020 was 40 months — the lowest in a decade — compared to average CEO tenure of 7 years. The CMO is actually showing signs of the same irrational composite problem, which is worth noting. AMAWikipedia
CTO — late 1980s, large industrial firms, Strategist from day one. Already established.

Now here's the pattern that jumps out:
Every other Chief Officer title was created at scale, for the mode they currently occupy. The CFO was created when companies needed a financial strategist — not a bookkeeper who had to become one. The CMO was created when companies needed a marketing strategist — not a brand designer who had to evolve. These titles described a mature function being elevated to the C-suite.
The CTO was also created this way — at GE and Allied-Signal for a Strategist function. That's consistent with every other Chief Officer title.
The anomaly isn't the CTO title. The anomaly is applying any C-suite title to a pre-scale startup. Every Chief Officer title was designed for a company that already had the organizational infrastructure supporting that function. When startups borrowed these titles in the 1990s, they applied executive-scale job descriptions to pre-organizational contexts across the board.
But — and this is the crucial distinction — for every other C-suite role, the core skill doesn't rotate.
The CFO at seed stage does the same thing the CFO at Series B does: manage money. The scope expands but the focus stays on financial stewardship. No identity change required.
The CMO at seed stage does the same thing the CMO at Series B does: own the market narrative. The channels and budget scale, but the skill points in the same direction throughout.
The CTO at seed stage does something structurally different from the CTO at Series B. Not harder — different. The focus rotates from product to people to market. That rotation is unique to the technical function because of the IC/management bifurcation that exists in no other discipline.
The sharpest version of your finding:
The C-suite title migration to startups was universally irrational — but it was only catastrophically irrational for the CTO, because the technical function is the only one where the job genuinely changes direction rather than merely scaling. Every other executive title imported a mismatch of scope. The CTO imported a mismatch of kind.
That's the sentence your thesis has been building toward.
