But here's the sharper finding your hypothesis points toward:

The dot-com era context amplifies this. Through a process dubbed "management by press release" during the dot-com era, entrepreneurs announced future fundraising rounds to sway prospective investors. Such "blitzmarketing" was extremely valuable in a crowded startup environment, yet it hardly reflected true business fundamentals. Over 80% of the internet startups that went public in the late 1990s were loss-making. 
CFA Institute

The CTO title wasn't just adopted — it was adopted in a specific cultural moment when signaling mattered more than substance, when investors were making decisions on team composition and vision rather than revenue or proof. The C-suite title pattern carried the credibility of established enterprises into a context where that credibility was borrowed rather than earned.

The mechanism in full:

Enterprise CTOs existed at GE, Allied-Signal, IBM — large companies with established technical organizations
VCs in the 1990s, trained on institutional investment patterns, looked for institutional signals in startup teams — C-suite completeness being one of them
Startups, responding to that investor pattern, adopted C-suite titles not because the roles existed but because the slots on the pitch deck required filling
The CTO slot specifically signaled "technical execution risk is covered by a named, senior executive" — exactly what VCs needed to hear
The title stuck because it worked for fundraising, regardless of whether the role it described was structurally coherent
The brutal implication:

The CTO title was arguably more about the pitch deck than about the organization. It was a fundraising instrument first, an organizational design decision second. The person receiving it was a Builder doing an individual contributor job, given a Strategist title to satisfy an investor pattern recognition heuristic — and then expected to actually become the thing the title implied, without any support structure, over the course of the company's growth.

The investors who required the signal helped create the structural problem they were trying to avoid. By demanding a "CTO" rather than a "technical co-founder," they imported a title that carried incompatible expectations — and then measured the person against those expectations at the next funding round.

That's not just an irrational composite. That's an irrational composite that was institutionalized by the very people whose job it is to evaluate execution risk.
