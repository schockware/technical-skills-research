# You Crossed the Loud Rotation. Your Reports Are Hitting the Quiet One.
## For engineering managers and managers-of-managers — the people who already made the jump and now shepherd reports across it

You already lived the hardest version of this. The IC→EM move — the one where about half the people who attempt it walk back to writing code — you made it and it stuck. That's the *loud* rotation: it has a name, a title change, a visible reversion event when it fails.

There's a quieter one your senior people are hitting right now, and the structure gave it no name at all: Senior→Staff. When a strong Senior stalls there, nobody can usually say *what* they're not ready for — and that vagueness is the problem, not the person. (Larson's field research: "Staff Engineer" isn't one job, it's four — Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand. A Senior measured against an unnamed composite is being graded on a target nobody specified.)

This is the practitioner version of a longer argument. Stripped to what survives a hostile read, here's the part that's *yours to act on*: **your job at these boundaries is to supply the conditions that make the rotation survivable — before the rung — not to coach a person through a structural gap the org under-resourced.**

## The fact that should change how you read your reports

Roughly half of newly promoted engineering managers revert to IC. That number is large-sample and behavioral — it measures what people *did*, not what they felt — and the honest reading of it is the one that takes weight off your reports and puts it on the structure.

One caution, stated plainly so you don't over-claim it: that ~50% is a *cross-industry* rate. The IC→management boundary is hard for everyone who crosses it, not just software. So the number proves the *boundary is hard*; it does **not** prove software is uniquely broken. (What makes software's version distinctive is the missing cushions — below — not the reversion number itself.)

What it does mean for you on Monday: when a report struggles at the manager jump, the base rate alone says "this is a hard boundary half of people don't hold," before you reach for any explanation about *this* person. And the reason it resists training is that crossing it is an **identity loss, not a skills upgrade.** Becoming a manager means giving up being the person who does the work — and "engineer" is an identity built almost entirely out of doing the work, with no external license to fall back on the way a surgeon-turned-administrator is still a surgeon. Reverting under pressure isn't weakness; it's reaching for the only identity anchor that exists. (The mechanism is peer-reviewed — though the studied population is founders, and applying it to engineers is the extension, not borrowed fact. State it once, lightly.)

A gentle personal reframe before you apply this to anyone else: *your own reversion risk back then was structural too.* You didn't hold the rotation because you were tougher than the people who didn't. You almost certainly had better conditions.

## What actually carries people across — and why it's the actionable part

Here is the finding that's genuinely yours to use. When you look at engineers who crossed IC→EM *durably*, the factors that consistently separate them from the ones who reverted are **organizational and contextual, not individual.** The durable crossers were not more committed, self-aware, or determined. They were more **structurally supported.** Specifically, they tended to be in situations with:

- **Psychological safety in the receiving team** — the team could actually take ownership of technical decisions, so the new EM could let go. On unsafe teams the team can't carry the work, the EM can't release it, and reversion follows. (Peer-reviewed, `AX-VL2023`.)
- **Value-fit with their people** — the EM trusted the team to care about the work, so they didn't keep pulling the critical-path code back.
- **Active manager-of-managers investment** — *this is you.* The EMs whose own managers checked in regularly, gave explicit feedback on management behavior (not just on team outcomes), and modeled the practice, transitioned more durably. The ones left to figure it out alone reverted more.
- **A real imprint target** — a high-potential junior the new EM could genuinely develop into the technical role they were vacating, which is what psychologically releases the IC identity.

Two of those four you directly control or supply: your own investment, and whether the team and pipeline around the report are set up to receive the handoff. That's the actionable core. The work is not "coach the person to try harder." It's "stand up the conditions before the boundary."

## Stop writing "not ready" on people the structure under-resourced

Now the part that holds even if you throw out every statistic above. When the ladder produces its predictable difficulty, the language that follows is reliably about the *person*: reversion → "management wasn't for them"; the Senior stall → "not ready for the next level"; the imposter spiral → "needs coaching"; the departure → "culture fit."

Each is plausible *in any single case* — which is exactly why the pattern is invisible. But said every time, across every team, for years, that consistency is the tell. A difficulty the structure would produce for almost anyone you put in that spot keeps getting recorded as a fact about the individual. The structure is never in the sentence.

This isn't "your reports never underperform" — sometimes anyone does. It's about your *default*: when a structural explanation and a personal one are both available, reaching reflexively for the personal one is what keeps the structural question from ever being asked — and therefore keeps the fix from ever being built. So the next report hits the same wall and gets the same sentence.

The diagnostic sentence to keep in your pocket, the one that flips it: **"we're asking this person to do a different job, and we named no transition for it."** Say that instead of "not ready," and a *different* set of moves becomes available — moves you actually have.

## What to do on Monday

The point of the reframe is that it unlocks concrete cushions. Before the boundary, not after the struggle:

1. **Name the rotation honestly, out loud.** Tell the person the move is a *different job*, not a deeper version of their current one — and tell them the runway you're giving them. The cheapest cushion is honest labeling, and most reports have never been told the magnitude of the change they're being asked to make.
2. **Make the receiving team able to receive.** Before you move a strong IC into managing it, ask whether the team has the psychological safety to take ownership and whether there's a junior they can imprint onto. If not, you're setting up the offload to fail for reasons that have nothing to do with the new EM.
3. **Be the manager-of-managers investment you're now the source of.** Regular check-ins, explicit feedback on *management behavior* (not just team metrics), modeling the practice. This is the structural support the durable crossers had and the reverters didn't — and it's the one entirely in your hands.
4. **For the Senior→Staff stall, name the archetype.** Don't grade someone against an undifferentiated "Staff" bar. Decide which of the four jobs the org actually needs from them, name *that*, and resource it (a sponsor who names the org-level work as Staff-level impact, protected time, real authority). An unnamed target is an illegible one, and illegibility is what breeds the "not ready" misattribution.

## The experiment you're actually positioned to run

There's a falsifier in the underlying research with your name on it — call it **F3**: *do the three cushions (honest label, respected fallback, scaffolded transition), funding held constant, actually improve transition survival?* The honest answer is that nobody has cleanly measured it. You are one of the few people who can. You have a team, a sequence of transitions, and the ability to change the conditions on some and not others and watch what happens. Treat the cushions as a hypothesis to test on your own team, not gospel to recite. If you run it and they don't help, that's a real finding — report it.

## The honest promise — and the one it won't make

Two guardrails, because the failure mode here is turning this into a management listicle that overpromises.

First: there is no "how to guarantee your report makes Staff" recipe, and you should distrust anyone who hands you one. The conditions that carry people across are *necessary, not sufficient*, and several are partly outside anyone's control. What separates durable transitions is structural support, not a method the survivors discovered — so a checklist of survivor habits is just luck mistaken for technique.

Second, and most important to be straight about: good management does **not** eliminate the ~50%. The rate holds even with support. What you can change is the *conditions* and the *sentence* — you can move the odds for an individual report and you can stop misattributing a structural difficulty to them. What you can't do is repeal the throughput limit of a hard boundary. The honest promise is "you can change the conditions and the sentence," not "you can make everyone cross."

And note what this is *not* asking you to do: it is not "stop promoting people," and it is not "manage people out pre-emptively before they hit the boundary." The whole point is that cycling people through an uncushioned rotation until one happens to fit is the broken loop. You break it by resourcing the rotation, not by managing the person.

> **The sentence to carry:** *I crossed the loud rotation. My reports are hitting the quiet one unnamed. My job isn't to coach them through a structural gap — it's to supply the cushions before the boundary, change the conditions I control, and change the sentence said about the ones who struggle.*
