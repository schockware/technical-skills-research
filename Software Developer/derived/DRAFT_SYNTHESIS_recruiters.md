# The Composite Req Is the First Link in the Chain
## For the people who write and place the job descriptions — recruiters, talent teams, leveling-rubric and job-board taxonomy owners

You are the actors who turn a leveling philosophy into searchable text. When a req says *"Senior Engineer — deeply hands-on AND a technical leader who multiplies the team,"* that line isn't describing a person who exists in the market at the volume you're sourcing for. It's describing a **rotation** — two different jobs, fused into one bullet list — and writing it that way is the first link in a chain that ends with a hire being blamed for not being superhuman. This is the practitioner version of a longer argument. The usable takeaway: **stop specifying the unicorn; specify the archetype.**

## What the composite req actually demands

Software did something almost no other field did: it split "do the work" and "lead the people/org who do the work" into two separate, separately-evaluated career tracks — and then a lot of reqs quietly fuse them back together. "Hands-on AND strategic/multiplying" asks for both sides of a line the industry's own org charts say is a *role change, not a deeper version of the same role*.

That matters operationally because the line is hard to cross. Roughly **half of newly promoted engineering managers revert to individual-contributor work** — a large-sample, *behavioral* figure (what people did, not what a survey said). One honest caveat so you don't over-cite it: that ~50% is a *cross-industry* rate, so it proves the IC→leadership boundary is hard for everyone, not that software is uniquely broken. But "hard for everyone" is exactly the point at the req-writing desk: when you write a req that demands both sides of that boundary at once, you are sourcing for the minority who hold both — and then leveling, evaluating, and (eventually) blaming everyone who doesn't.

## The Larson hook: "Staff Engineer" is four different jobs

Here is the operational gold for this audience. Will Larson's field research (2021) found that **"Staff Engineer" is not one job — it's four**:

| Archetype | What they actually do |
|---|---|
| **Tech Lead** | Guides one team's technical approach and execution, without management authority. The most common. |
| **Architect** | Owns technical direction and quality across a domain spanning multiple teams. Tends to emerge around ~100 engineers. |
| **Solver** | Parachutes into the org's hardest problems; highly autonomous, context-switches hard. |
| **Right Hand** | Extends an executive's attention and authority back into the technical org. Tends to emerge around ~1,000 engineers. |

A req that says **"Staff Engineer"** without naming *which* of these the org actually needs is, in search terms, unsearchable — you cannot screen, level, or calibrate against a target nobody specified — and it sets up a mis-hire: a brilliant Tech Lead dropped into a Right Hand seat isn't facing a harder version of their job, they're facing a *different* one. The same logic runs one rung down: "Senior" reqs that bundle deep individual delivery with cross-org influence are bundling two of these directions into one undifferentiated ask.

## The req is the first link in the misattribution loop

Why this lands on your desk specifically: the composite req is where a structural problem becomes a *person* problem.

1. You write "does the work AND leads the org" into the req.
2. You hire against it — selecting, inevitably, someone stronger on one side than the other.
3. The role demands both at full strength, with no runway built in to grow the missing side.
4. The hire struggles on the half they were never resourced for.
5. The struggle gets read as *the person* — "not ready," "not strategic enough," "great IC, weak leader" — and the next req goes out **unchanged**.

The req wrote the composite; the composite wrote the blame. Two cushions the composite req silently omits make this worse: it names no **fallback** (a respected way to stay in the craft if the leadership half isn't the fit) and it names no **runway** (any acknowledgment that the second half is a thing you grow into, not arrive with). The fix isn't a better candidate. It's a better req.

## "Rockstar full-stack lead who also sets direction" is a budget compromise dressed as a requirement

Worth saying plainly to a taxonomy owner: the undifferentiated composite req usually isn't a considered spec — it's what you write when the org **can't yet afford to differentiate the role.** The tell is that companies *do* differentiate it the moment they can: Larson's archetypes emerge as the org grows (Architect ~100 engineers, Right Hand ~1,000); leveling rubrics and dual IC/management tracks get built once there's budget. Nobody keeps the all-in-one composite once they can split it apart. So when a req demands the rockstar-who-does-everything, that's a budget constraint wearing the costume of a requirement — and naming it as such is more honest, and more sourceable, than pretending the unicorn is the spec.

## What to actually put in the req (and what to stop)

The move is to give candidates and hiring managers the **distinction** instead of the composite:

- **Name the archetype, not the title.** Don't post "Staff Engineer." Post the job: *Tech Lead for X team*, *Architect across the Y domain*, *Solver for the hardest Z problems*, *Right Hand to the VP*. Now it's searchable, screenable, and levelable.
- **Pick a side of the rotation, on purpose.** If the role is genuinely IC-weighted, say so and stop loading it with "and multiplies the org." If it's leadership-weighted, say *that* and don't gate it behind "must still ship the most code." Demanding both at full strength is demanding the half-the-field-can't-cross combination.
- **State the runway, or admit there isn't one.** If the role needs someone to *grow into* the influence half, say it's a development hire and resource it. If it needs both on day one, you're sourcing a small population — price that honestly rather than acting surprised when the pipeline is thin and the hires "underperform."

## Two things this is *not* saying

- **Not "stop writing senior/Staff reqs."** (Concession 7 of the underlying argument: this isn't an attack on the existence of senior roles.) It's "stop writing the *undifferentiated composite* one." Differentiated senior reqs are exactly the goal.
- **Not "the skills are incompatible."** They aren't — engineers and leaders draw on each other over a career. The problem isn't that the two sides can't coexist in one person; it's that the req provides **no archetype and no runway**, so it sources for people who already happen to be both and then blames everyone else.

And don't let this flip into the opposite failure mode: a "how to source a unicorn who can do it all" playbook is the *exact inverse* of the message. There's no recipe for finding someone who crosses every rung at once. The message is the smaller, usable one: **specify the archetype, name the side of the rotation, and stop writing the composite.**

> **The line to carry:** *A req that demands "does the work AND leads the org" — or "Staff Engineer" with no archetype — isn't a spec, it's a composite. Name which of Larson's four jobs you actually need, pick a side of the rotation, state the runway, and you've stopped writing the first link in the misattribution chain.*
