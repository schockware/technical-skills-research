# It Wasn't You. The Next Rung Is a Different Job.
## For the people the research is actually about — working engineers, from Mid through Senior and Staff, and anyone staring at the manager jump

You've heard these said. Maybe one was said about you. Maybe you've said one to yourself.

- *"Management just wasn't for them."*
- *"They're not ready for the next level."*
- *"I think I have imposter syndrome."*
- *"I went back to IC — I guess I couldn't hack it."*

This is written to put a different sentence in your hands. Not a kinder one — a *more accurate* one. We took the whole argument about the software career ladder apart, kept only the parts that survive a hostile read, and the single most useful thing left standing is this: **the next rung up is usually a different job, not a deeper version of you — and "not ready for the next level" is far more often the structure talking than a verdict on you.**

## The jump that half of people walk back

Start with the one fact that's hard, behavioral, and measured at large scale: **roughly half of newly promoted engineering managers go back to being engineers.** Not "half think about it." Half *do* it.

And — this is the part that matters for you — the rate holds *even at companies that train their managers well.* That's the tell. If this were a skills gap, training would close it, and it largely doesn't. So it isn't a skills gap.

Here's what it actually is. Being a manager means *giving up being the person who does the work* — not a promotion on top of what you already are, an exchange. And the thing you're giving up isn't a task, it's an identity. "Software engineer" is built almost entirely out of *doing the work*: you became one by writing the code, shipping the thing, being trusted with the hard part. When the work stops, that identity has nothing to stand on.

A surgeon who becomes Chief of Surgery is still, by license, a surgeon — the identity has somewhere to live through the change. An engineer who becomes a manager has no such anchor; nothing external keeps you "an engineer" once you stop engineering. So when the pressure comes, reaching back toward the keyboard isn't weakness. **It's reaching for the only identity anchor that exists.**

That's why this resists training. The hard part isn't learning a skill — it's an identity loss, and you can't workshop your way out of grief. (This mechanism is peer-reviewed, though in founders, not engineers; applying it to you specifically is the paper's extension, not borrowed fact — the same mechanism, with fewer of the conditions that let people survive it.)

## What actually separates the people who stay across

Here's the finding that should take some weight off your shoulders, because it points away from you. The engineers who crossed into management *and stayed* were not tougher, smarter, more self-aware, or more committed than the ones who reverted. They were more **structurally supported** — a team safe enough to take real ownership off their hands, a manager-of-managers who actually invested in them, a high-potential junior to hand the old work to, value-fit with the people they now led.

None of that is something you can install by wanting it harder; most of it isn't even yours to control. ("I felt ready" is the most common story people tell *after* a transition works — and almost never what actually made the difference.) So if you crossed and it didn't take, the honest reading is not "I wasn't cut out for it." It's: *the conditions weren't there, and they were mostly not mine to set.*

## "Not ready for the next level" — read it again

Now the part that holds even if you throw out every statistic above.

When the ladder produces its predictable difficulty, the words that follow are always about the *person*. Imposter feelings → "they need coaching." Going back to IC → "management wasn't for them." Stalling at Senior → "not ready for the next level." Leaving → "culture fit."

Each one is *plausible in any single case.* That's exactly why the pattern is so hard to see. Any one of them might genuinely be about a person. But said **every time, at every company, for decades** — that consistency is the tell. A difficulty that the structure would produce for *almost anyone you put in that spot* keeps getting filed, every single time, as a fact about the individual. The structure is never in the sentence.

That's the whole misattribution. Not "you never underperform" — sometimes anyone does. It's about the *default*: when both a structural explanation and a personal one are sitting right there, the field reflexively reaches for the personal one. And because it does, it never asks the structural question, and never builds the structural fix — so the next person hits the same wall, and gets the same sentence.

The clearest *measured* version of this is the reversion: "management wasn't for them," said over someone who is, by the numbers, one of roughly *half* who make that exact move. The difficulty is documented. The personal framing is the misattribution. You don't have to take anything on faith to see it.

## About the Senior plateau — the honest version

There's a quieter version of this: the engineer who stalls at Senior and never makes Staff, and gets "not ready" with no explanation — often because nobody can say *what* they're not ready for. (Larson's field research finds "Staff Engineer" isn't one job; it's four — Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand. A vague Staff bar is sometimes a target nobody named.) Where the criteria are genuinely illegible, some quiet, misattributed non-promotions plausibly happen, and that's real.

But here's the honest line, and it cuts toward relief: **plateauing at Senior is real for *some* people — and for most, it is not a failure at all.** A huge share of people who "stall" at Senior simply *chose the craft.* They like writing code, they're good at it, they don't want the rotation — and now that respected Staff/Principal IC tracks exist, that's a legitimate, good outcome, not a ceiling you failed to break. So carry the plateau as a *possibility* worth naming, never as "most plateaued Seniors washed out." That story is exactly the misattribution this whole paper is about. Don't apply it to yourself.

## "But isn't this just… growth?"

The strongest pushback on all of this is worth stating, because it actually concedes the point: *"This is just growth. Every career asks you to change as you advance. And startups can't afford anything fancier — that's reality, not a flaw."*

Both halves give the game away.

Growth is getting *deeper at your craft* — more of the same work, done better. You've been doing that your whole career. This is not that: this is being asked to do a **different craft** — the object of the work rotates, from code to team to org — with no runway to learn it. Calling that "just growth" is what hides it.

And "we can't afford to support this transition" only makes sense as a sentence if the transition *actually needs support.* You don't say "we can't afford to scaffold it" about something people do fine on their own. The objection assumes the thing it's denying. The job didn't get easier at a small company; the budget to admit it just wasn't there. You can watch the industry concede this with its own behavior: the moment companies can afford to, they build the IC track and split the giant Staff role into its four real jobs. Nobody keeps the impossible composite once they have the money to break it apart. **That's the industry admitting the cushions were always needed.**

## The one sentence to keep

You weren't handed the things every other rotating profession hands its people at this exact moment: an honest name for what was changing, a respected way to stay in the craft, or a real runway to learn the new thing. That's not a story about your limits. It's a story about what you were and weren't given.

So here is the more accurate sentence, the one worth carrying out of this:

> **The ladder asked me to become a different kind of worker, and set up no real way to become one. The difficulty was structural. It wasn't a verdict on me.**

A note on tone, kept in the spirit the research itself keeps: this is a *pattern*, not a prophecy. It describes what happens to populations of engineers; it doesn't predict your particular Tuesday. Some people cross these rungs and thrive, conditions and all. The point isn't to call which way you'll go — it's to make the thing *legible*, so that when the sentence comes, from someone else or from yourself, you can hear how much of it is the structure talking.

And note what this is *not*: a recipe for "winning" the transition. There isn't an honest one to give — what most reliably separates the people who make it are conditions you mostly don't control, and any "5 habits to make Staff" list is just the survivors mistaking their luck for a method. Nor is it telling you to revert, or not to. Going back to IC isn't failure — the swing between building and leading is a healthy pendulum over a career. It just gets *counted* as one. It isn't.

It wasn't you. It was a different job, and nobody named it.
