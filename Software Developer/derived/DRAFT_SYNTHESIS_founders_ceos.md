# The Ladder Rotates Your Best People at the Load-Bearing Rung
## For founders and CEOs who run the career ladder their company offers — who promote, level, and sometimes lose their strongest engineers to it

You built a ladder so your engineers can advance without leaving. Then you watched some of your best ICs get promoted into management and walk back out within a year, or stall at Senior and quietly drift toward the door. The usual story is that those were individual misfires — wrong person, wrong time, "management wasn't for them." This is the operator's version of a longer argument that the story is wrong, and that the thing it's hiding is something you can actually resource.

The one sentence to take away: **the ladder rotates your people into a different kind of job at one load-bearing rung — name the rotation and resource it *before* the rung, because the default move (promote your best IC and hope) is a loop that never converges.**

## What's actually happening at the rung where you lose people

Software did something almost no other field did: it split "do the work" and "manage the people who do the work" into two separate, separately-evaluated career tracks, and made engineers choose. That split is your own org chart admitting the two are *different jobs* — not a deeper version of the same one. The IC→manager jump is where your ladder forces a person across that line.

That one jump is the corroborated one — it has an external witness in your org chart and in a hard behavioral fact (below). The broader story, that each rung up is steadily less about the code and more about people and strategy, is a fair way to describe the arc, but only that one jump is a proven hard line. (Senior→Staff is a real, recurring second instance — "Staff Engineer" is really four different jobs behind one title — but carry it as a pattern, not a measured cliff.) You don't need the strong version. The one rung is enough.

## The number that should reframe how you read the losses

Roughly **half of newly promoted engineering managers revert to individual-contributor work.** That figure is large-sample and *behavioral* — it measures what people actually *did* (went back to IC), not what they said on a survey — which is why it's worth more than any sentiment number you've seen about engineer burnout or imposter syndrome.

One honest caution so you don't over-claim it: that ~50% is a *cross-industry* rate. The IC→management boundary is hard for everyone who crosses it, in every field — so the number proves the *boundary is hard*, not that software is uniquely broken. (What makes software's version distinctive is the missing cushions, which is the part you control — more below.)

What it means for you: when a promoted engineer washes back to IC, the base rate alone says *this is a hard boundary half of people don't hold* — before you reach for any explanation about that specific person. And the reason it resists training is that crossing it is an **identity loss, not a skills upgrade.** Becoming a manager means giving up being the person who does the work, and "engineer" is an identity built almost entirely out of *doing the work*, with no external license to stand on the way a surgeon-turned-administrator is still a surgeon. Reverting under pressure isn't weakness; it's reaching for the only identity anchor that exists. (That mechanism is peer-reviewed — in founders, a population a lot like you; extending it to your engineers is the reasonable inference, not borrowed fact. State it once and move on.)

## The loop that's quietly costing you your bench

Here's the part that holds even if you throw out every statistic above, and it's the one that should worry an operator most — because it's a loop that burns money and people without ever resolving:

1. The structure produces predictable difficulty at the rung.
2. You attribute it to the person — "management wasn't for them."
3. So the remedy is *individual*: coach them, replace them, hire someone more senior.
4. The replacement enters the **same uncushioned rung, with the same missing supports.**
5. Back to step 1.

Cycling people through the boundary until one happens to fit *is* this loop, and **it never converges** — because each iteration treats a recurring structural failure as a fresh individual one. You pay the full cost of a failed manager transition (the lost IC, the disrupted team, the re-hire, the morale hit) over and over, and you never buy down the underlying cause, because the underlying cause is never in the sentence you say about it.

The fix isn't a better engineer. It's a better sentence — and then the moves the better sentence unlocks. **You cannot resource a transition you won't name.** As long as the difficulty is "wasn't for them," your only move is to replace the person. The moment it's "we demanded an identity rotation here and resourced no transition for it," a *different* set of moves opens up.

## You already act like you know the rotation needs support

This isn't just an argument that lives in the logic. Your own company *acts out* the belief that the rung exceeds one unsupported person — you just do it late, once you can afford to:

- **You build the IC-track fallback the moment you can afford it.** Staff and Principal tracks, dual-ladder leveling, the EM/Staff split — these are corrections to a problem the simple ladder created. Medicine never needed a "senior surgeon who doesn't operate" path and then a correction; it never built the forced rotation in the first place. That you build the cushion at all is the admission that you knew it was needed. The correction is the confession.
- **You split the composite role apart the moment you can afford it.** A small company's "Staff Engineer" has to be all four of Larson's archetypes at once; a larger one differentiates them. Nobody keeps the undifferentiated, uncushioned composite once the budget exists to break it up. That's revealed preference: the cushioned, differentiated ladder is what you build when money isn't the constraint — which means the uncushioned version was a budget compromise, not a considered design.

This matters because it pre-answers your own best objection.

## Your strongest objection concedes the point

The honest pushback here is: *"This is just growth — every career asks people to change as they advance. And I'm a startup; I can't afford residency-style transition programs. The lean ladder is an economic necessity, not a flaw."*

The necessity half is **true** — and true in exactly the way that proves the point. "We can't afford to scaffold this transition" only makes sense if the transition genuinely *requires* scaffolding. You don't say "we can't afford to support this" about something people do comfortably on their own; you say it about something that *should* be supported and isn't, for reasons of money. So the objection's own logic concedes the ceiling: *the rotation exceeds what an unsupported person reliably crosses,* and you're pleading budget. Fair enough — but then plan for it, instead of being surprised when half revert.

And "it's just growth" misses the specific thing. Growth is getting *deeper* at your craft. This rung is being asked to do a *different* craft, with no runway. Most careers don't put that on the default advancement path — and the ones that do, cushion it.

To be precise about the size of the claim, because it changes how you should hear it: **this is not "your company is run irrationally."** The lean ladder may be a perfectly efficient bet for the company. The claim is narrower and harder to escape — the rung is *under-cushioned*, the cost of that lands on the person rather than the structure, and you can choose to resource it. "Under-cushioned," not "irrational." This isn't a critique of your judgment; it's a line item you haven't been pricing.

## What to do with this

The actionable shift is: instead of "promote the best IC and hope," provide **one cushion early** — and they're cheap relative to what the failure loop costs you.

1. **Honest labeling — the cheapest cushion, do this one first.** When you move someone into management (or toward Staff), tell them plainly it's a *different job*, not a deeper version of their current one, and tell them the runway you're giving them. Most people have never been told the magnitude of the change they're being asked to make, so they can't prepare for it or attribute a struggle to structure instead of self. This costs you a conversation.

2. **A respected IC track that's a real outcome, not a consolation prize.** If "stay technical" is a legitimate, well-compensated, high-status path at your company, then management stops being the only way up — and the people who'd revert anyway don't have to make the round trip through a failed promotion to get there. The track only works if it's *respected*; a Staff title nobody levels into or sponsors is decoration.

3. **A scaffolded transition proportionate to the ask.** Not a residency — you're right that you can't fund that. But a named hand-off: who receives the work the new manager is putting down, an explicit window measured against the person's development rather than this quarter's headcount, and a manager-of-managers who actually invests rather than leaving them to figure it out alone. The durable crossers had these conditions; the reverters didn't.

The diagnostic sentence to keep in your pocket, the one that flips the whole thing from a people problem to a design problem you can fund: **"we demanded an identity rotation here, and resourced no transition for it."**

## Two things this is *not* telling you to do

Because the failure mode for an operator reading this is to over-correct:

- **It is not telling you to stop promoting people.** The point isn't that the rung is bad; it's that it's unresourced. You break the loop by resourcing the rotation, not by freezing the ladder.
- **It is not telling you to pre-emptively manage people out** of a transition you think they'll fail. That's the broken loop one level up — deciding the person is the problem before the structure has done its part. The move is to change the conditions and the sentence, not to triage the people.

And keep it honest about where the underlying argument could be wrong, lightly: other professions aren't idylls either — medicine and law rotate and burn people out too; the large-company correction (real IC tracks) genuinely works where it exists, so the gap is specifically the startup and mid-market scale you're operating at; and "every rung is a rotation" is a useful map, not a cliff at each step — the one hard, proven line is the manager jump. None of those caveats changes what's on your plate. They just keep you from over-reading it.

> **The sentence to carry:** *The ladder rotates my best people into a different job at the manager rung. Serial replacement never converges. I can name the rotation, give one cushion early — starting with an honest label and a respected IC track — and resource the transition before the rung, instead of paying for it one washed-out promotion at a time.*
