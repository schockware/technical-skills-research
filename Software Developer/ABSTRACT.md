# Abstract — Software Developer

*Folder-level abstract. Summarizes the career-ladder-scope area for a reader who wants the claim before deciding whether to read further. For the defensible spine, see [`09_SYNTHESIS_dev_ladder_thesis.md`](09_SYNTHESIS_dev_ladder_thesis.md) and [`DRAFT_SYNTHESIS.md`](DRAFT_SYNTHESIS.md); for what it should not be built on, see the "do not build on" table in `09_SYNTHESIS`.*

---

In most professions, seniority deepens the craft. In software's career ladder, most significant promotions — Junior→Mid, Mid→Senior, Senior→Staff, IC→Engineering Manager, and beyond — require a different kind of worker at each rung: a different cognitive mode, a different feedback relationship, a different source of professional meaning. The promotion framing calls this "growth." The actual ask, at several rungs, is closer to a career change. Only one seam is institutionally verifiable outside the framework — the IC↔management split the industry itself formalized into two separate, separately-evaluated career tracks — and it is this seam that carries the corpus's load-bearing evidence: roughly half of newly promoted engineering managers revert to individual-contributor work (CEB/Gartner, n≈30,000), a large-sample behavioral number, not a survey or sentiment figure.

Other professions that rotate senior practitioners at all provide at least one of three cushions: honest labeling (the military's Warrant Officer track names the choice as a track, not a promotion), a respected craft-track fallback (the surgeon stays licensed and operating after becoming Chief), or a scaffolded, multi-year transition (medical residencies, officer academies). Software, at startup and mid-market scale, typically provides none of the three — a quiet fracture that shows up again at Senior→Staff, where Larson's four Staff archetypes describe distinct jobs hiding behind one title, and where the failure mode isn't reversion but disappearance into a permanent plateau.

The corpus is explicit that these are *distinct* skill sets demanded on a compressed timeline with no on-the-job runway to acquire the next one — not *incompatible* skill sets, a stronger and now-retired claim the corpus's own disconfirming search (the engineer/manager pendulum, which shows positive transfer between the two) does not support. The actionable harm is not the difficulty of the rotation itself but the misattribution that follows it: reversion is read as "management wasn't for them," the Staff plateau as "not ready for the next level" — never as what the structure would predict for nearly anyone routed through it uncushioned. Naming the structural cause is the deliverable; it does not by itself fix the structure.

**What this does not claim.** Not that software has no stable craft — algorithms, data structures, and core theory are durable; the evaluation and promotion machinery simply credits the volatile layer over the durable one. Not that other professions escaped rotation — they rotate; they cushion it, and the comparators (medicine, law, academia) have real pathologies of their own. The thesis is scoped explicitly to startup and mid-market conditions, where the large-company correction (dual-track IC paths, leveling rubrics) has not yet reached. This area generalizes up into [`../Software Industry/`](../Software%20Industry/) (the same mismatch as an industry-structural failure to specialize) and down into [`../CTO/`](../CTO/) (the same mismatch compressed and accelerated into one role, pre-scale, alone).
