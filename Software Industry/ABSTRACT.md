# Abstract — Software Industry

*Folder-level abstract. Summarizes the widest-scope area of the study for a reader who wants the claim before deciding whether to read further. For the full argument, see [`06_MMM_synthesis.md`](06_MMM_synthesis.md); for the defensible form, see [`R3_STEELMAN_strongest_form.md`](R3_STEELMAN_strongest_form.md); for what remains open, see [`R4_OPEN_RESEARCH_agenda.md`](R4_OPEN_RESEARCH_agenda.md).*

---

Every mature profession that hit the limits of human cognitive capacity responded the same way: specialization — medicine's forty-plus specialties, law's practice areas, engineering's licensed disciplines. Software didn't. It built career ladders that require an identity-level rotation at nearly every significant rung and labeled each rotation "natural advancement" rather than the career change it is, while a founding cultural belief — "smart people can learn anything" — supplies the reason not to examine the structure when the predictable failures arrive.

The argument's anchor is Brooks' *Mythical Man-Month* (1975): the fungibility assumption — that a developer is a configurable unit of cognitive output, swappable and reconfigurable like a resource — is what makes rotating a person through Builder, Multiplier, and Strategist identities look like reallocation rather than what it actually demands. This is not a new mistake. Taylor measured the same overreach in 1911; Drucker named knowledge work as un-Taylorizable in 1954; Brooks named the fungibility fallacy directly in 1975; the CHAOS Report has published consistent large-project failure rates since 1994; the ~50% IC→EM reversion rate (CEB/Gartner, n≈30,000) has been measured since the 2000s. Each generation of management science rediscovers that the composite demand exceeds capacity, and the next generation inherits the demand intact. Pre-internet, that repetition is explicable as genuine information loss between generations. Post-internet — with all of this literature a search away — the same non-response is a different and sharper claim: the field knows and proceeds anyway.

The industry has partially corrected itself where it can afford to: dual-track IC paths, leveling rubrics, Staff/Principal engineering as a real destination, DevRel and Principal PM as differentiated roles. This "ray of hope" is real and is treated as evidence *for* the thesis, not against it — the correction is a confession that the composite was never sustainable, arriving only once the correction became affordable. It reaches large, mature companies. It has not reached the startup and mid-market scale where most practitioners spend most of their careers, and where the founding CTO — the flagship case in [`../CTO/`](../CTO/) — is the terminal, most compressed expression of the same pattern.

**What this does not claim.** Not that software has no stable craft — algorithms, data structures, and networking primitives are decades-stable; the claim is narrower: the evaluation and promotion machinery credits the volatile, fashionable layer rather than the durable one. Not that other professions are clean — medicine and law have their own rotation-driven failure patterns; the claim is that they provide at least one cushion (honest labeling, a respected fallback, a scaffolded transition) that software withholds at the scale where most engineers actually work. The steelman objection ("startups can't afford specialists") is treated as an accidental concession: you only say you can't afford to split a job you believe should be split.
